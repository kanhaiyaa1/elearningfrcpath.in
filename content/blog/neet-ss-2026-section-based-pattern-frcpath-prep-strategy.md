If you sat NEET-SS in any year before 2026, your muscle memory of how to attempt the paper is now wrong. The exam has changed structurally — and candidates who don't adapt their preparation to the **new section-based, time-locked format** will lose marks not because they didn't know the content, but because they ran out of time in the wrong place.

**NEET-SS Pathology 2026** is now structured as three independent sections of 50 questions each, with a hard 50-minute timer per section and no ability to return to a previous section once you move forward. That is 150 MCQs in 2.5 hours — the total duration is unchanged, but the sectional walls change everything about how you must approach the paper. This article gives you the strategy, the drill structure, and the topic map to prepare intelligently.

---

## The 2026 Pattern in Full — What Has Actually Changed

### The New Structure at a Glance

| Feature | NEET-SS 2025 and Earlier | NEET-SS 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Total questions | 150 MCQs | 150 MCQs |
| Total time | 2.5 hours (150 minutes) | 2.5 hours (150 minutes) |
| Section structure | Single continuous paper | **3 sections × 50 questions** |
| Time per section | N/A — move freely | **50 minutes per section (hard limit)** |
| Navigation between sections | Move freely across all 150 Q | **No going back to previous sections** |
| Marking scheme | +4 correct, −1 wrong | +4 correct, −1 wrong |

The headline change is the **sectional lock**. Once you submit Section 1 or its timer expires, those 50 questions are gone. You cannot revisit a question you flagged in Section 1 while you were in Section 2. This changes the risk calculus for every question decision you make.

### Why NBEMS Made This Change

The sectional format was introduced to reduce two known problems in open-navigation papers:
1. **Time bunching** — candidates spending 80 minutes on their strongest section and running out of time for the rest
2. **Late-question advantage** — candidates returning to difficult early questions with context gained from later questions

The new format forces more uniform time distribution. For a well-prepared candidate, this is neutral. For a candidate who relies on going back and revising, it is a significant challenge.

## What This Means Section by Section

NBEMS has not officially published which topics fall in which section for Pathology. However, based on the general NEET-SS structure and the pathology syllabus, the working assumption from previous papers — now that sections are time-locked — is:

| Section | Likely broad domains (Pathology) | Strategic priority |
|---|---|---|
| Section 1 | General pathology, haematopathology, immunopathology | Accuracy-first — these are conceptual, can bog down fast |
| Section 2 | Systemic pathology — GI, respiratory, renal, liver, CNS | Speed-critical — high topic count, must triage quickly |
| Section 3 | Oncopathology, molecular diagnostics, recent advances, IHC | Highest difficulty — don't arrive here with less than 45 minutes |

This is the key strategic insight: **you need to exit Section 2 with time to spare for Section 3**. The "recent advances" and molecular diagnostics questions in Section 3 are where the top percentile is made — but they are also the slowest questions to read and process. Arriving at Section 3 with 35 minutes instead of 50 is a competitive disadvantage you created in Section 2.

## Section-Wise Time Management Drills

### The 50-50 Rule — Your Core Training Unit

One timed mock block per week should now be your non-negotiable training unit: **50 questions in 50 minutes, no pausing, no looking up answers mid-session**. This is the unit that builds the sectional pace muscle memory you need.

**How to structure the weekly drill:**

| Week | Drill type | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Week 1–2 | Section 1 simulation: General + haematopathology questions | Mix of past NEET-SS and FRCPath-style MCQs |
| Week 3–4 | Section 2 simulation: Systemic pathology (GI + CNS + renal) | High-topic-count timed block |
| Week 5–6 | Section 3 simulation: Oncopathology + molecular + IHC | Highest-difficulty questions, timed |
| Week 7 onward | Full 150-question simulated paper in 3 locked blocks | Simulate exact exam conditions |

The [NEET-SS Pathology preparation track at ElearningFRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology) is now structured around exactly this sectional drill model — weekly 50-question, 50-minute blocks with difficulty calibrated to the new pattern.

### Your Pace Target: 1 Minute Per Question

50 questions in 50 minutes means **60 seconds per question average**. In practice, that means:

- Questions you know cold: 25–35 seconds
- Questions requiring elimination: 45–60 seconds
- Questions requiring reasoning through a vignette: up to 90 seconds

That 90-second question budget is **borrowed time** from your cold-recall questions. If you have more than 8–10 slow vignette questions in a section, you will run short. This is exactly why FRCPath-style preparation — where you learn *why* an answer is correct, not just *what* it is — dramatically increases the proportion of questions you can answer in under 45 seconds.

## The Skip vs Mark Decision — A Triage Rule That Works

In a locked-section format, the decision to skip, mark, or commit to an answer carries different stakes than in an open-navigation paper.

### The 30-Second Decision Tree

```
Read the question stem fully (do not skim).
    ↓
Can you identify the answer within 30 seconds?
    ├── YES → Answer and move on.
    └── NO → Can you eliminate 2 options confidently?
              ├── YES → Mark your best guess, flag for review (if time remains in section).
              └── NO → Mark the question for the last 5 minutes, move on immediately.
```

**The 5-minute rule:** Keep the last 5 minutes of each section for flagged questions only. Do not spend those 5 minutes on new questions — you will get the answering pace wrong and rush.

**The most dangerous error in a sectional exam:** Getting absorbed in a single complex question and spending 3–4 minutes on it. A single 4-minute question costs you the time budget for 3–4 other questions. In FRCPath terms, this is what Part 1 timed practice — with the 2-minute-per-question EMQ discipline — trains out of you.

## Building Section 3 Fluency: The Molecular Diagnostics Sprint

Section 3 — oncopathology and molecular diagnostics — is where most candidates leak marks in a sectional format. The questions are longer, the stems include IHC panels and molecular results, and the options are often subtly differentiated. Under time pressure, this is where knowledge depth separates candidates most sharply.

### Three Targeted Drills for Section 3

**Drill 1 — IHC Panel Speed Read (5 minutes per day)**

Each day, look at one IHC panel (e.g., CK7+, CK20−, TTF-1+, Napsin A+) and name the primary in under 10 seconds. Extend to less familiar panels daily. This builds the pattern-recognition automaticity that makes IHC questions in Section 3 fast rather than slow.

**Drill 2 — Molecular Marker Flash Cards (10 minutes per day)**

Cover: which molecular marker defines which entity, which marker is predictive vs prognostic, and what WHO 5th edition changed. Keep cards to one fact per side — depth is built in explanations, not in crowded notes.

**Drill 3 — Timed Full Section 3 Mock (weekly)**

50 oncopathology/molecular/recent-advances questions in 50 minutes, scored, with explanation review immediately after. The [pathology MCQ bank](https://elearningfrcpath.com/pathology-mcq-2026) includes Section 3–style difficulty-graded question sets with full explanations referenced to WHO 5th edition.

## Exam Pearls & Mnemonics

**Mnemonic — Section timing splits:**

> **"50-50-50 — but never even"**
> Aim to finish Section 1 in **45 minutes** (bank 5 min), Section 2 in **48 minutes** (bank 2 min), and use the full **50 minutes** in Section 3. The hardest questions come last — arrive there with maximum time.

**Mnemonic — When to skip in a sectional exam: "FLAG it, don't fight it"**

> **F**lag the question (mark for review)
> **L**eave immediately — do not re-read
> **A**nswer your best guess before leaving (never leave blank)
> **G**o back only in the last 5 minutes of the section

Never leave a flagged question blank. Negative marking only applies to wrong answers — a guessed answer based on elimination gives you a positive expected value if you can eliminate even one option.

**Pearl — The "WHO 5th edition molecular lock" for NEET-SS speed:**

Know these six molecular rules cold; they resolve approximately 15–20% of high-difficulty NEET-SS questions in under 30 seconds:
1. NPM1 mutation → AML regardless of blast % (≥10%)
2. IDH mutation + 1p/19q codeletion → Oligodendroglioma (always)
3. H3 K27M alteration + midline → Diffuse midline glioma grade 4 (regardless of morphology)
4. MLH1+PMS2 lost → think MLH1 methylation first; BRAF V600E confirms sporadic
5. MSH2+MSH6 lost → Lynch syndrome until proven otherwise
6. SF3B1 mutation → MDS with ring sideroblasts (reclassified in WHO 5th edition)

**Pearl — IHC panel speed-reads for Section 3:**

| Panel | Diagnosis in under 10 seconds |
|---|---|
| CK7+, CK20+, CDX2+ | Colorectal adenocarcinoma (metastatic) |
| CK7+, CK20−, TTF-1+, Napsin A+ | Lung adenocarcinoma |
| CK7+, CK20−, ER+, PR+, GCDFP-15+ | Breast carcinoma |
| CK7+, CK20−, PAX8+, Tg+ | Thyroid carcinoma |
| CK7+, CK20−, PAX8+, Tg− | Renal/other Müllerian primary |
| CD20+, BCL2+, BCL6+, MYC+ | High-grade B-cell lymphoma (consider DHL/THL) |
| S100+, HMB45+, Melan-A+, SOX10+ | Melanoma |

## Practice MCQs

**Q1.** NEET-SS 2026–style (Section 3). A 62-year-old female with lung mass. Biopsy IHC: CK7+, CK20−, TTF-1+, Napsin A+, p40−. Molecular testing: EGFR exon 19 deletion detected. Which statement about PD-L1 testing in this patient is CORRECT?

- A) PD-L1 TPS should be assessed before deciding on EGFR TKI therapy and guides the choice of TKI
- B) PD-L1 TPS result does not change first-line treatment decision — EGFR TKI is first-line regardless of PD-L1 score
- C) PD-L1 CPS > 1 qualifies this patient for pembrolizumab monotherapy as first-line treatment
- D) EGFR mutation renders PD-L1 IHC technically invalid and the result should not be reported

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — PD-L1 TPS result does not change first-line treatment decision — EGFR TKI is first-line regardless of PD-L1 score**

In EGFR-mutated NSCLC, first-line treatment is an EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor (osimertinib, gefitinib, erlotinib) regardless of PD-L1 expression level. Anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy (pembrolizumab etc.) is NOT recommended as first-line treatment in the presence of an actionable driver mutation — it has shown inferior outcomes vs TKI and is associated with increased toxicity. PD-L1 TPS (option A) does not guide TKI choice; TKI selection is guided by EGFR mutation subtype (exon 19 del vs L858R vs exon 20 insertion etc.). CPS (option C) is used for specific tumour types (gastric, cervical, TNBC) — not for EGFR-mutated NSCLC. Option D is incorrect — PD-L1 can still be reported but does not alter the treatment algorithm in driver-positive NSCLC.

**Exam tip for sectional timing:** This question type (driver mutation + predictive IHC interaction) takes 60–90 seconds to read and process. With the molecular lock pearl above, you immediately recognise: EGFR mutation = TKI first regardless → Option B.

</details>

**Q2.** Section 1-style (haematopathology). A 55-year-old male has persistent lymphocytosis. Flow cytometry: CD5+, CD23+, CD19+, FMC7−, CD10−, surface Ig dim. FISH: no t(11;14). According to the Matutes scoring system, this patient's score is most consistent with:

- A) Mantle cell lymphoma (MCL)
- B) Hairy cell leukaemia (HCL)
- C) Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL)
- D) Marginal zone lymphoma (MZL)

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL)**

The Matutes CLL scoring system assigns 1 point each for: CD5+, CD23+, FMC7−, surface Ig dim/negative, CD22/CD79b dim/negative. A score of 4–5 is diagnostic of CLL. This case scores: CD5+ (1), CD23+ (1), FMC7− (1), surface Ig dim (1) = **score 4** — consistent with CLL. The absence of t(11;14) by FISH rules out MCL (option A), which is CD5+, CD23−, FMC7+, cyclin D1+. HCL (option B) is CD5−, CD103+, BRAF V600E mutated. MZL (option D) is CD5−, CD23−, variable FMC7. Knowing the CLL Matutes score cold allows this question to be answered in 35–40 seconds — a typical Section 1 speed target.

</details>

**Q3.** Section 2-style (CNS pathology). A biopsy from a 35-year-old with a frontal lobe mass shows: diffuse astrocytic morphology, mild nuclear atypia, no necrosis, no microvascular proliferation. IDH1 R132H immunohistochemistry: positive. ATRX: lost. 1p/19q codeletion by FISH: not deleted. Ki-67: 4%. According to WHO 5th edition (2022), the correct diagnosis and grade is:

- A) Oligodendroglioma, IDH-mutant and 1p/19q-codeleted, grade 2
- B) Diffuse astrocytoma, IDH-mutant, grade 2
- C) Diffuse astrocytoma, IDH-mutant, grade 3 (due to ATRX loss)
- D) Glioblastoma, IDH-mutant, grade 4

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Diffuse astrocytoma, IDH-mutant, grade 2**

The WHO 5th edition glioma classification requires the following logic: IDH-mutant + **no 1p/19q codeletion** → Astrocytoma (not Oligodendroglioma). ATRX loss supports astrocytic lineage (ATRX loss and 1p/19q codeletion are mutually exclusive — ATRX is lost in astrocytomas, preserved in oligodendrogliomas). Grading within IDH-mutant astrocytoma: Grade 2 = IDH-mutant, no necrosis, no microvascular proliferation, CDKN2A/B intact. Grade 3 = mitotically active (significant mitoses). Grade 4 = necrosis and/or microvascular proliferation, OR CDKN2A/B homozygous deletion. **ATRX loss alone does not upgrade to grade 3.** Ki-67 4% with no necrosis and no microvascular proliferation = grade 2. Option A is wrong because 1p/19q is NOT codeleted — rule out oligodendroglioma first. Option D requires necrosis or microvascular proliferation, absent here.

</details>

## FAQs

**Q: How many questions should I attempt per section if I want to maximise my score given negative marking?**

The optimal attempt strategy under −1 negative marking with +4 correct is: attempt a question only if you can reduce the options to two (i.e., your probability of being correct is ≥ 50%). At 50% accuracy on attempted questions, expected value = (0.5 × 4) + (0.5 × −1) = +1.5 per attempt — still positive. The only time to skip entirely (not even guess) is when all four options are equally unfamiliar. In practice, for a well-prepared NEET-SS candidate, this means attempting 135–145 of the 150 questions. Blanking large blocks of a section due to time pressure is far more costly than a few wrong guesses.

---

**Q: Is the 3-section structure confirmed for all NEET-SS subjects or only Pathology?**

The section-based, time-locked format is the confirmed structure for the NEET-SS 2026 examination across super-speciality subjects, as per the NBEMS notification for the 2026 cycle. It applies to all subjects including Pathology, Oncopathology, and the INI-SS equivalent papers. Candidates appearing for both NEET-SS and INI-SS should note that INI-SS may have a different internal structure — always verify directly from the NBEMS and institution-specific notifications before exam day.

---

**Q: In the old NEET-SS format I was used to reviewing my answers at the end. How do I mentally adjust to the new locked format?**

The key shift is to treat each section as a complete exam in itself — not a third of a larger paper. Before you enter each section, set a mental plan: "I have 50 minutes and 50 questions. I will spend no more than 90 seconds on any single question. I will flag and move on if stuck." Practise this mindset in every timed mock block — not just in full paper simulations. The candidates who struggle most with sectional locking are those who prepared with open-navigation practice papers and built the habit of returning to difficult questions. Breaking that habit requires deliberate timed practice with sectional rules enforced.

---

**Q: Does FRCPath preparation directly help with NEET-SS sectional speed, or is it only useful for content depth?**

Both — but the speed benefit is underappreciated. FRCPath Part 1 preparation under timed conditions (EMQs and SBAs at approximately 2 minutes per question) builds the habit of processing a clinical vignette efficiently and selecting an answer without over-deliberating. NEET-SS at 60 seconds per question is actually a faster pace than FRCPath Part 1 — so the timed discipline transfers directly. More importantly, FRCPath-depth content knowledge means more questions become sub-45-second recalls rather than 90-second reasoning exercises, which is the core mechanism by which content depth translates to sectional time advantage.

## Key Takeaways

- **NEET-SS 2026 is section-based** — 3 sections × 50 questions × 50 minutes, with hard sectional locks and no going back
- **Core time target:** Finish Section 1 in ~45 min, Section 2 in ~48 min, arrive at Section 3 with full 50 minutes — the hardest questions come last
- **Weekly drill:** One 50-question, 50-minute timed block per section per week; full 150-question paper simulation from week 7 onward
- **Skip rule:** "FLAG it, don't fight it" — best guess, flag, move on; reserve last 5 minutes of each section for flagged questions only
- **FRCPath depth = sectional speed:** Content understood at mechanism level is answered in 30–45 seconds; surface-memorised content becomes a 90-second question — the difference that decides section timing
- **WHO 5th edition molecular lock:** Six molecular rules resolve 15–20% of hard Section 3 questions in under 30 seconds — know them cold
- **IHC speed-reads:** Practise panel → diagnosis in under 10 seconds daily; this is a trainable skill, not an innate one
