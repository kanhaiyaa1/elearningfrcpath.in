NEET-SS 2026 now runs on a sectional-timer format — 150 MCQs split across three 50-minute blocks, all computer-based. Meanwhile, NEET-INI-SS 2026 retains its two-stage structure: Stage-I MCQ screening, followed by Stage-II practical/clinical skill assessment. If you are an MD Pathology or DNB Pathology resident trying to decide which superspecialty entrance to target, this comparison will help you make that call based on your strengths, career goals, and the institutes you actually want to train at.

This is not an abstract "both are good" article. Let's break down the specifics — pattern, pathology weightage, preparation demands, and the practical career logic behind each route.

---

## The 2026 Exam Pattern at a Glance

Before comparing, you need to know exactly what each exam looks like on test day.

**NEET-SS 2026** is conducted by the National Board of Examinations (NBE). For the MD Pathology → DM Oncopathology or DM Histopathology route, the structure is:

- **Total questions:** 150 MCQs
- **Duration:** 150 minutes (2.5 hours), divided into **3 sections of 50 minutes each**
- **Mode:** Computer-Based Test (CBT)
- **Sectional timer:** Once a section ends, you cannot return to it
- **Negative marking:** Yes, −1 for each wrong answer
- **Subject coverage:** Core specialty (Pathology) forms the dominant section, with Clinical Sciences and Basic Sciences in adjacent sections

The sectional timer is the biggest change for 2026. Earlier batches could redistribute time across the paper — that flexibility is gone. A candidate who is slow on image-based morphology questions now has a real time-management problem baked into the structure itself.

**NEET-INI-SS 2026** (conducted by AIIMS New Delhi on behalf of the INI consortium — AIIMS campuses, PGIMER, JIPMER, NIMHANS) has a fundamentally different design:

- **Stage-I:** MCQ-based screening exam (similar question density to NEET-SS, but without sectional timing)
- **Stage-II:** Practical/clinical skill assessment — OSPE/OSCE-style stations including frozen section interpretation, gross specimen evaluation, IHC panel interpretation, cytology slides, and clinical case integration
- **Mode:** Stage-I is CBT; Stage-II is in-person at the host institute
- **Institutes covered:** AIIMS Delhi, AIIMS Bhopal, AIIMS Bhubaneswar, PGIMER Chandigarh, JIPMER Pondicherry, NIMHANS Bangalore (for relevant superspecialties)

The practical stage is the differentiator. NEET-INI-SS does not just test what you know — it tests what you can do with a microscope and a specimen in front of you under time pressure.

## Pathology Weightage — Where the Marks Actually Come From

### NEET-SS 2026: Subject Distribution for Pathology Candidates

| Section | Content | Approx. Weightage |
|---|---|---|
| Section A (Core Specialty) | Surgical Pathology, Oncopathology, Histopathology, Cytopathology, Hematopathology, Molecular Pathology | ~50% |
| Section B (Clinical Sciences) | Oncology (medical, surgical, radiation), Clinical correlation of pathology findings | ~30% |
| Section C (Basic Sciences) | Genetics, Immunology, Biochemistry relevant to pathology | ~20% |

For DM Oncopathology, expect heavy MCQ coverage on:
- WHO 5th edition tumor classifications (breast, GI, kidney, hematology)
- IHC markers — predictive vs prognostic distinction
- Molecular testing — FISH, NGS, PCR applications in surgical pathology
- Image-based questions (H&E morphology on screen)

Our students consistently report that image-based MCQs account for 20–25% of the Pathology section — which is why timed mock practice on image MCQs is non-negotiable for NEET-SS. The [NEET-SS pathology mock test bank at elearningfrcpath.com](https://www.elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-mock-tests-online) is structured with this distribution in mind.

### NEET-INI-SS 2026: What Stage-II Actually Tests

Stage-I of INI-SS covers similar MCQ ground. But Stage-II stations commonly include:

- **Frozen section station:** Diagnosis under time limit (5–7 minutes per station), margin assessment, clinical communication of intraoperative result
- **Gross specimen station:** Systematic grossing protocol, identifying key landmarks, dictating findings
- **Cytology station:** FNAC smear interpretation, adequacy assessment, reporting category (e.g., Bethesda for thyroid, Milan for salivary gland)
- **IHC panel station:** Integrating clinical history + morphology + IHC panel to give a final diagnosis
- **OSPE stations:** Short structured questions on technique, stain principles, lab quality metrics

If you have been doing surgical pathology rotations actively — grossing, frozen sections, cytology sign-outs — you are building the Stage-II skillset without realising it. If you have spent most of residency in administrative or academic roles with minimal bench time, Stage-II will expose that gap.

## Head-to-Head Comparison

| Parameter | NEET-SS 2026 | NEET-INI-SS 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Conducting body | NBE | AIIMS Delhi (INI consortium) |
| Exam stages | 1 (MCQ only) | 2 (MCQ + Practicals) |
| Total MCQs | 150 | ~150 (Stage-I) |
| Sectional timing | Yes — 3 × 50 min, no carry-over | No (flexible within Stage-I) |
| Practical/OSPE | No | Yes (Stage-II, in-person) |
| Pathology seats available | Across multiple private + govt medical colleges | AIIMS, PGIMER, JIPMER, NIMHANS only |
| DM Oncopathology route | Primary route (majority of seats) | Available at AIIMS Delhi, PGIMER |
| Prep style needed | Speed, image MCQs, time-box discipline | MCQ depth + hands-on bench skills |
| Negative marking | Yes (−1) | Yes (Stage-I); OSPE: structured marks |
| Frequency | Annual | Annual |
| Rank validity | NEET-SS rank for NEET-SS counselling | Separate INI-SS merit list |

## Which Exam Should You Target?

This is the question that actually matters, and the honest answer depends on three things: your career goal, your clinical skill profile, and the institutes you want to train at.

### Target NEET-SS 2026 if:

**1. Your primary goal is DM Oncopathology** — the majority of DM Oncopath seats nationally are filled through NEET-SS counselling. INI institutes offer only a handful of seats for this superspecialty, and competition at AIIMS specifically is brutal on a separate rank list.

**2. You are strong at MCQ speed under time pressure** — the sectional timer format rewards candidates who have done extensive timed mock practice. If you have been consistently hitting 75%+ accuracy on our [sectional-timed pathology test series](https://www.elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-mock-tests-online), NEET-SS 2026 suits your preparation style.

**3. You want institute flexibility** — NEET-SS opens seats at government medical colleges, autonomous institutions, and private institutions across India. Your rank gives you a menu of options. INI-SS only covers 6 institutions, making it extremely rank-dependent.

**4. You prefer a pure MCQ exam** — no practical component means your entire score is determined by knowledge and speed, not hands-on assessment. If bench skills are not your strongest suit currently, NEET-SS is the lower-risk route.

### Target NEET-INI-SS 2026 if:

**1. You specifically want to train at AIIMS Delhi, PGIMER, or JIPMER** — the training quality, faculty, case volume, and brand value at these institutes are genuinely different. If an AIIMS Pathology residency is your goal, INI-SS is the only path.

**2. You have strong practical skills** — if you have done active frozen sections, fine needle aspiration cytology sign-outs, grossing protocols, and IHC interpretation routinely, Stage-II plays to your advantage. Candidates who have been thorough bench pathologists often find Stage-II more comfortable than expected.

**3. You are comfortable with Stage-I without a sectional timer** — INI-SS Stage-I gives you flexibility to allocate time across the paper. If you work better with full paper visibility and your own pacing, this structure suits you.

**4. You want the most rigorous training environment** — AIIMS and PGIMER pathology programs are known for high case volumes, research exposure, and faculty mentorship. If training quality matters more than seat security, target INI-SS.

### Can you prepare for both simultaneously?

Yes — and most serious candidates do. The MCQ content overlaps approximately 80%. The incremental investment for INI-SS is Stage-II preparation, which requires:

- Weekly frozen section drills (with your department's cases)
- Systematic grossing practice with dictation
- Cytology FNAC smear review (Bethesda, Milan, Paris systems)
- IHC panel integration practice

This is realistic if you start 3–4 months before Stage-II with structured bench sessions. ## Prep Strategy by Exam

### NEET-SS 2026 Pathology Prep Plan (Condensed)

▶ **Months 1–2:** WHO 5th edition tumour classifications — systematically cover breast, GI, kidney, hematology, soft tissue. Focus on diagnostic criteria and key IHC panels.

▶ **Months 2–3:** Molecular pathology — FISH targets (HER2, ALK, ROS1), NGS applications, MMR/MSI testing, predictive biomarkers. High MCQ density here.

▶ **Month 3 onwards:** Sectional-timed mock tests — minimum 3 full mocks per week. Review every wrong answer with reference to PathologyOutlines or your notes. Track image-based MCQ accuracy separately.

▶ **Final month:** Revision-only. No new topics. Mock test + error log review cycle daily.

### NEET-INI-SS 2026 Prep Plan (Condensed)

▶ **Stage-I:** Same MCQ prep as NEET-SS above.

▶ **Stage-II Preparation (parallel, starting Month 2):**
- Frozen section: Practise at least 2–3 per week in your department. Focus on margin assessment language and intraoperative communication protocols.
- Cytology: Review all FNAC reporting systems — Bethesda (thyroid), Milan (salivary gland), Paris (urine), Yokohama (pancreaticobiliary). Each system has 6 categories — know them cold.
- Grossing: Standardise your dictation format. Stations are marked on completeness and systematic approach, not just the diagnosis.
- IHC panels: Build a panel → diagnosis → clinical significance card for the 20 most common IHC scenarios (breast, lymphoma, carcinoma of unknown primary, neuroendocrine tumours).

- **Speed:** Sectional timer demands rapid, accurate MCQ performance
- **Scale:** Hundreds of seats across India through counselling

**Q3.** The NEET-SS 2026 sectional timer structure (3 × 50-minute sections) primarily disadvantages which type of candidate?

- A) Those with high accuracy on image-based morphology MCQs
- B) Those who rely on redistribution of time across the full paper based on question difficulty
- C) Those who have done extensive timed mock practice
- D) Those with strong molecular pathology knowledge

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Those who rely on redistribution of time across the full paper**

The sectional timer locks each 50-minute block — once time expires on Section A, you cannot transfer unused time to Section B or return to unanswered questions. Candidates who historically managed their exam by skipping harder questions and returning later lose this strategy entirely. Those who have trained with timed, section-by-section mock tests (the recommended approach) are not disadvantaged. Image-based accuracy and molecular knowledge are assets regardless of format.

## FAQs

**Q: Can I appear for both NEET-SS 2026 and NEET-INI-SS 2026 in the same year?**

Yes. Both exams are independent, conducted by different bodies (NBE vs AIIMS-led INI consortium), and have separate application processes, schedules, and merit lists. Most serious candidates apply to both. Your NEET-SS rank is used only for NEET-SS counselling; your INI-SS rank is used only for INI institute allotment. There is no conflict in applying to both, and appearing in both maximises your options for the year.

**Q: What pathology superspecialties are available through NEET-SS 2026?**

NEET-SS 2026 offers seats for DM Oncopathology and DM Histopathology (where available at respective institutes). The exact superspecialty seat matrix is published by NBE before each exam cycle. DM Oncopathology is the most common pathology superspecialty available through NEET-SS across government and private medical colleges nationally.

**Q: Is the NEET-INI-SS Stage-II the same across all INI institutes?**

The broad structure (OSPE/OSCE stations for practical skills) is standardised by the INI consortium, but individual institutes may customise specific station content. AIIMS Delhi, being the coordinating institute, sets the benchmark. Candidates shortlisted for Stage-II are generally assessed at their preferred institute or at the host institute designated for that exam cycle. Always verify the current year's notification for exact logistics.

**Q: How much pathology weightage is there in NEET-SS vs NEET-INI-SS Stage-I?**

For MD Pathology candidates appearing in the Pathology superspecialty stream, the core specialty (Pathology) dominates approximately 50% of NEET-SS Section A. NEET-INI-SS Stage-I has a similar distribution with pathology forming the majority of specialty-specific content. Both exams additionally include clinical sciences and basic science content — roughly 30% and 20% respectively — meaning pathology alone does not determine your rank.

## Bottom Line

NEET-SS 2026 is the practical choice for most pathology candidates — more seats, pure MCQ format, institute flexibility, and a preparation framework that is well-defined. If your target is specifically an INI institute (AIIMS/PGIMER/JIPMER), then NEET-INI-SS is the route, and Stage-II preparation must begin alongside MCQ prep, not after it.

Start with a strong pathology foundation that serves both exams. The morphology, IHC, and molecular content you master for NEET-SS MCQs is exactly what Stage-II of INI-SS asks you to apply with a slide in front of you. Prepare once, aim at both.
