Mismatch repair (MMR) deficiency is one of those topics that appears in virtually every FRCPath Part 1 and NEET-SS Pathology paper — and for good reason. It bridges molecular biology, morphology, immunohistochemistry, hereditary cancer syndromes, and now, directly informs immunotherapy eligibility. If you can master **MMR deficiency pathology** in depth, you've covered ground that spans colorectal cancer, endometrial carcinoma, gastric cancer, and Lynch syndrome in one stroke.

This guide consolidates everything you need — mechanisms, IHC interpretation patterns, Lynch vs. sporadic distinctions, tumour-specific pearls, and practice MCQs — written the way I'd explain it to a resident preparing for their exam.

---

## What Is the Mismatch Repair System?

The mismatch repair (MMR) system is a highly conserved DNA repair pathway that corrects errors introduced during DNA replication. When the DNA polymerase slips — particularly at repetitive short sequences called **microsatellites** — it can insert or delete bases without completing the strand correctly. The MMR machinery recognises these mismatches and excises the error before the next replication cycle locks it in permanently.

The four key proteins in the MMR system are **MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2**. They don't work alone — they form obligate heterodimers:

- **MutSα**: MSH2 + MSH6 → recognises single base mismatches and small insertions/deletions
- **MutSβ**: MSH2 + MSH3 → handles larger loop mismatches (less tested clinically)
- **MutLα**: MLH1 + PMS2 → the endonuclease complex that initiates repair after recognition

Understanding the heterodimer pairings is the single most important thing you need to know for IHC interpretation.

### Why Heterodimer Pairing Matters for IHC

When the primary protein in a heterodimer is lost, it destabilises the partner protein — so both are lost on IHC. When the secondary partner is lost, the primary can still pair with an alternative partner and is **retained** on IHC.

This gives us predictable loss patterns that examiners love to test:

| Gene/Protein Lost (Germline or Somatic) | Expected IHC Loss |
|---|---|
| MLH1 | MLH1 **and** PMS2 (both lost) |
| PMS2 alone | PMS2 only lost, MLH1 retained |
| MSH2 | MSH2 **and** MSH6 (both lost) |
| MSH6 alone | MSH6 only lost, MSH2 retained |
| MLH1 promoter methylation (sporadic) | MLH1 **and** PMS2 (both lost) |
| EPCAM deletion → MSH2 silencing | MSH2 **and** MSH6 (both lost) |

Memorise this table. It comes up as a one-liner question as often as anything else in molecular pathology.

## Microsatellite Instability — What Exactly Is It?

When MMR fails, errors at microsatellite sequences accumulate and are propagated to daughter cells. This leads to alleles of varying length at these loci — a state called **microsatellite instability (MSI)**.

The gold standard for MSI testing uses the **Bethesda panel**: five microsatellite markers (BAT25, BAT26, D5S346, D2S123, D17S250). Results are categorised as:

- **MSI-High (MSI-H)**: ≥2 of 5 markers unstable
- **MSI-Low (MSI-L)**: 1 of 5 markers unstable
- **Microsatellite Stable (MSS)**: 0 markers unstable

For clinical and exam purposes, **MSI-L is grouped with MSS** — the meaningful distinction is MSI-H vs. MSS.

### IHC vs. PCR-Based MSI Testing

| Feature | MMR IHC | PCR-Based MSI |
|---|---|---|
| What it detects | Protein expression loss | Actual allele length instability |
| Concordance | ~95% | Reference standard |
| Advantage | Identifies which protein is lost (guides germline testing) | More sensitive for MSI-L; BRAF/MLH1 methylation can be added |
| Limitation | Rare aberrant retained expression despite mutation | Doesn't tell you *which* gene is affected |
| Turnaround | Fast, runs on FFPE | Slightly longer, lab-dependent |

IHC is the **recommended first-line test** in most guidelines because it's widely available, fast, and points you to the gene to test in the germline. For [FRCPath Part 1 preparation](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-histopathology-course), knowing *when* each test is preferred is frequently asked.

## Lynch Syndrome vs. Sporadic MMR Deficiency

This is the most commonly examined distinction in the MMR topic — and it's a distinction where a single detail can flip your answer.

### Lynch Syndrome (Hereditary)

Lynch syndrome results from a **germline pathogenic variant** in one of the four MMR genes (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2) or in **EPCAM** (which sits upstream of MSH2 and when deleted, causes MSH2 promoter hypermethylation and silencing).

Key features:

- **Autosomal dominant** inheritance
- Lifetime CRC risk: ~40–80% depending on gene (MLH1/MSH2 highest)
- Endometrial carcinoma risk: up to 60% (often the **sentinel cancer** in women)
- Also associated with: ovarian, gastric, urinary tract, small bowel, sebaceous skin tumours (Muir-Torre syndrome variant), CNS tumours (Turcot syndrome variant)
- Amsterdam II criteria and revised Bethesda guidelines are used to identify which patients need testing — know these for your [FRCPath exam guide](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-exam-guide-2026)

### Sporadic MMR Deficiency (Non-Hereditary)

Sporadic MSI-H tumours account for **approximately 15% of colorectal cancers** and arise through a completely different mechanism: **MLH1 promoter hypermethylation** — a somatic epigenetic event, not a germline mutation.

This is most often associated with the **CpG Island Methylator Phenotype (CIMP)** pathway and is strongly linked to **BRAF V600E mutation** (~80% of sporadic MSI-H CRCs). This BRAF association is the key differentiating feature from Lynch syndrome.

**The Reflex Testing Algorithm:**

```
MMR IHC → MLH1/PMS2 lost
       ↓
BRAF V600E testing
       ↓
BRAF mutant → Sporadic (MLH1 methylation, NOT Lynch)
BRAF wild-type → Refer for germline MLH1 testing (possible Lynch)
```

In practice, MLH1 methylation PCR can also be used directly. Either way, the principle holds: **MLH1/PMS2 loss alone does not equal Lynch syndrome**.

I've seen this exact algorithm turn up as an extended matching question in FRCPath Part 2 paper analysis — the correct pathway saves a patient from unnecessary germline testing (and the wrong psychological burden).

## MMR Deficiency Across Tumour Types

MMR deficiency is not confined to colorectal cancer. The [NEET-SS Pathology curriculum](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology) now expects you to know MMR-d across all major sites.

### Colorectal Cancer — The Classic Setting

**Frequency**: MSI-H in ~15% of all CRC; nearly all Lynch-associated CRCs are MSI-H.

**Morphologic clues to MSI-H CRC** — features that should make you think "run MMR IHC":

- **Right-sided (proximal colon)** location
- Mucinous or signet-ring cell differentiation
- Medullary growth pattern (sheets of cells, no gland formation, pushing border)
- **Prominent tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs)** — Crohn's-like lymphocytic reaction
- Poor differentiation paradoxically associated with **better prognosis** in MSI-H

**Prognostic impact**: MSI-H CRC has **better stage-for-stage prognosis** than MSS CRC — a counterintuitive fact that is routinely examined.

**Predictive impact**: MSI-H tumours are **resistant to 5-FU monotherapy** but respond dramatically to **PD-1 checkpoint inhibitors** (pembrolizumab is FDA-approved as first-line for MSI-H/dMMR metastatic CRC).

### Endometrial Carcinoma — The TCGA Context

MMR deficiency is one of the four molecular groups in the **TCGA / ProMisE classification** of endometrial carcinoma:

1. **POLE ultramutated** — best prognosis
2. **MMR-d (MSI-H)** — intermediate prognosis (~30% of endometrial carcinomas)
3. **No specific molecular profile (NSMP)** — intermediate
4. **p53 abnormal** — worst prognosis

MMR-d endometrial carcinomas are mostly **endometrioid type**, often Lynch-associated in younger women (<50 years). When you see MSH6 or PMS2 loss alone on IHC of an endometrial carcinoma, think Lynch syndrome — these secondary protein losses are more common here than in CRC.

### Other MMR-d Tumour Sites

| Site | Notes |
|---|---|
| Gastric cancer | MSI-H ~10–15%; EBV-positive tumours overlap; right stomach, antrum; better prognosis |
| Ovarian cancer | Rare (<5%); endometrioid/clear cell types; Lynch-associated |
| Urothelial carcinoma | Lynch-associated (upper tract > bladder); MSH2/MSH6 most common |
| Sebaceous tumours | Muir-Torre syndrome (Lynch variant); sebaceoma, sebaceous carcinoma; MSH2/MSH6 |
| Small bowel adenocarcinoma | Lynch-associated, often MSH2/MSH6 |
| CNS — glioblastoma | Turcot syndrome (MLH1/MSH2 with CRC-type tumours vs. APC with medulloblastoma) |

For the [pathology MCQ bank](https://elearningfrcpath.com/pathology-mcq-2026), tumour site + IHC loss pattern questions are high-frequency. Practice spotting the combination.

## Exam Pearls & Mnemonics

**Mnemonic for heterodimer pairs: "MutS Matches, MutL Links"**

- **MutS = MSH proteins** (recognise mismatch)
- **MutL = MLH/PMS proteins** (link to repair machinery)
- Secondary partners: PMS2 is PMS2-ary (secondary) to MLH1; MSH6 is 6 (less critical) vs MSH2

**The BRAF Rule**: MLH1/PMS2 loss + BRAF V600E = Sporadic. No exceptions needed for exam purposes.

**The Isolated Secondary Loss Rule**: PMS2 loss alone or MSH6 loss alone always points to a **germline variant in that secondary gene** — reflex directly to germline testing.

**Right-sided, mucinous, TIL-rich = MSI-H until proven otherwise.**

**MSI-H = Better prognosis but 5-FU resistant = Immunotherapy sensitive.** Three facts in one sentence.

**Lynch syndrome endometrial cancer** tends to appear in women under 50 — if the question gives you a 42-year-old with endometrial carcinoma and MMR-d on IHC, Lynch syndrome should be at the top of your differential.

Looking at [our full FRCPath course material](https://elearningfrcpath.com/), MMR deficiency topics appear in at least 3 sections: molecular pathology, colorectal pathology, and gynaecological pathology — which is why it rewards a unified understanding rather than compartmentalised memorisation.

## Practice MCQs

**Q1.** A 68-year-old man undergoes right hemicolectomy for a moderately differentiated colorectal adenocarcinoma with medullary features and prominent tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes. MMR IHC shows: MLH1 lost, PMS2 lost, MSH2 retained, MSH6 retained. BRAF V600E mutation is detected on reflex testing. What is the correct interpretation?

- A) Lynch syndrome due to MLH1 germline mutation
- B) Lynch syndrome due to PMS2 germline mutation
- C) Sporadic MSI-H tumour due to MLH1 promoter methylation
- D) Sporadic MSS tumour with aberrant IHC staining

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Sporadic MSI-H tumour due to MLH1 promoter methylation**

MLH1 and PMS2 are co-lost because MLH1 is the obligate partner — its loss destabilises PMS2. MSH2 and MSH6 are retained. The presence of **BRAF V600E mutation** effectively rules out Lynch syndrome (Lynch-associated MLH1-mutant tumours are BRAF wild-type). This is a sporadic CIMP pathway tumour with MLH1 promoter hypermethylation. The medullary morphology with TILs is classic for MSI-H CRC. Germline testing is **not** indicated here.

</details>

---

**Q2.** A 44-year-old woman is diagnosed with endometrioid endometrial carcinoma. MMR IHC shows isolated MSH6 loss with retained MLH1, PMS2, and MSH2. What should be the next step?

- A) BRAF V600E testing
- B) MLH1 promoter methylation analysis
- C) Germline MSH6 testing for Lynch syndrome
- D) No further testing required; this is a sporadic finding

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Germline MSH6 testing for Lynch syndrome**

Isolated MSH6 loss (with retained MSH2) means MSH6 is the only protein affected. MSH6 is a secondary partner to MSH2 — its isolated loss points directly to a pathogenic variant in the **MSH6 gene itself**, not a methylation-driven event. BRAF testing is only relevant when MLH1/PMS2 are lost. MLH1 methylation cannot cause MSH6 loss. This patient is young (44), has an endometrial carcinoma, and isolated MSH6 loss — a classic Lynch syndrome scenario. Direct germline MSH6 testing is the correct next step.

</details>

---

**Q3.** Which of the following combinations is **least** likely to be caused by Lynch syndrome?

- A) MLH1/PMS2 loss with BRAF V600E
- B) MSH2/MSH6 loss in a 38-year-old with right-sided CRC
- C) Isolated PMS2 loss in a 50-year-old with endometrial carcinoma
- D) MSH6 loss in a sebaceous carcinoma of the eyelid

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: A — MLH1/PMS2 loss with BRAF V600E**

BRAF V600E mutation is essentially a molecular signature of sporadic MLH1-methylated MSI-H colorectal cancer. Lynch syndrome-associated tumours are **BRAF wild-type**. Options B, C, and D are all consistent with Lynch syndrome: MSH2/MSH6 loss in a young patient (B), isolated PMS2 loss pointing to PMS2 germline mutation (C), and sebaceous carcinoma in Muir-Torre syndrome — a Lynch variant with MSH2/MSH6 mutations (D).

</details>

## FAQs

**Q: Does MSI-H always mean Lynch syndrome?**

No — this is one of the most common misconceptions. Approximately 75–80% of MSI-H colorectal cancers are **sporadic**, caused by somatic MLH1 promoter methylation. Lynch syndrome accounts for only 20–25% of MSI-H CRCs. The proportion is higher in endometrial carcinomas (~30% of MMR-d endometrial cases may be Lynch-associated, especially in younger women). Reflexive germline testing without first excluding sporadic cause leads to unnecessary testing and patient anxiety.

---

**Q: Can an MMR-deficient tumour show retained MMR protein expression on IHC?**

Yes — rarely. This is called **aberrant retained expression** and occurs when a point mutation or frameshift leads to a functionally deficient but structurally intact protein that still binds the antibody. PCR-based MSI testing may reveal instability despite "normal" IHC. This is one reason why equivocal or discordant cases may need both tests. In practice, the concordance between IHC and PCR MSI is approximately 95%.

---

**Q: Is MMR IHC testing mandatory for all colorectal cancers?**

Most international guidelines (ESMO, NCCN, CAP) now recommend **universal MMR testing** for all newly diagnosed CRC, regardless of age or clinical features. This is because: (1) it identifies Lynch syndrome patients who may have been missed by clinical criteria; (2) it has direct therapeutic implications (5-FU resistance, immunotherapy eligibility); and (3) tumour morphology is an unreliable predictor of MSI status.

---

**Q: What is the Amsterdam II Criteria?**

The Amsterdam II Criteria (the "3-2-1 rule") are used to identify families with a high clinical likelihood of Lynch syndrome:
- At least **3** relatives with a Lynch-associated cancer (CRC, endometrial, small bowel, ureter/renal pelvis)
- Spanning **2** successive generations
- At least **1** diagnosed before age **50**
- One of the three must be a first-degree relative of the other two
- FAP must be excluded

These criteria are **highly specific but poorly sensitive** — up to 50% of Lynch syndrome families don't meet them. Bethesda guidelines are more sensitive and are used to decide who to test, while Amsterdam II is used for clinical risk assessment.

---

**Q: What is the significance of EPCAM deletion in Lynch syndrome?**

EPCAM (also called TACSTD1) sits immediately upstream of MSH2 on chromosome 2p21. Large deletions in the 3' end of EPCAM cause read-through transcription into the MSH2 promoter, leading to **MSH2 promoter hypermethylation** — a somatic silencing event but driven by a germline structural variant. On IHC, you see MSH2 and MSH6 loss (same pattern as a germline MSH2 mutation). EPCAM-associated Lynch syndrome has a **higher risk of CRC but lower risk of endometrial carcinoma** compared to MSH2 germline mutation. EPCAM testing is now included in comprehensive Lynch syndrome germline panels.

## Key Takeaways

- **MMR proteins form heterodimers**: MLH1+PMS2, MSH2+MSH6. Losing the primary protein brings down both; losing the secondary alone is isolated.
- **MLH1/PMS2 loss + BRAF V600E = Sporadic (MLH1 methylation)**. Do not refer for Lynch syndrome germline testing.
- **Isolated PMS2 or MSH6 loss = germline variant in that gene**. Refer directly.
- **MSI-H CRC**: right-sided, mucinous/medullary, TIL-rich, better prognosis, 5-FU resistant, immunotherapy sensitive.
- **Lynch syndrome** affects CRC, endometrial, ovarian, gastric, urothelial, sebaceous, and CNS tumours.
- **EPCAM deletion** → MSH2/MSH6 loss on IHC; higher CRC than endometrial risk.
- **Universal MMR/MSI testing** is now standard for all CRC — clinical criteria alone are insufficient.
- In endometrial carcinoma, MMR-d is one of four **TCGA molecular groups** — associated with intermediate prognosis.
