The **WHO 5th Edition Classification of Urinary and Male Genital Tumours (2022)** rewrote the renal cell carcinoma chapter more dramatically than any previous edition. If you are preparing for **FRCPath Part 1, NEET-SS Pathology, or INI-SS** using a textbook or notes from before 2023, a significant chunk of your RCC knowledge is now outdated — and examiners know it.

In this guide we'll walk through every new molecularly defined RCC entity, the key nomenclature changes, and the IHC patterns you need to know to answer both short-answer and MCQ-format questions confidently. These topics are now being directly tested in recent FRCPath papers, and NEET-SS 2025 included at least one question on novel renal entities.

---

## Why WHO 5th Edition Changed RCC Classification

The old WHO 2016 (4th edition) classification of kidney tumours was largely morphology-driven. Clear cell, papillary (type 1 and 2), and chromophobe RCC accounted for the bulk of cases, with a handful of rarer entities tacked on. The problem was that "type 2 papillary RCC" in particular was a heterogeneous wastebasket — it included morphologically similar but molecularly distinct tumours with wildly different prognoses.

The 2022 edition corrects this by integrating molecular data. The result is a longer list of named entities, many defined by a **single molecular alteration** — a gene mutation, fusion, or copy number change. This reflects how these tumours are now diagnosed in practice: morphology raises the suspicion, IHC confirms it, and molecular testing validates.

For exam purposes, this means you need to know **which molecular alteration defines each entity** and **which IHC marker detects it**.

---

## Overview: What Changed in WHO 5th Edition RCC

Major structural changes:

- **Three brand-new entities**: ELOC-mutated RCC, Eosinophilic solid and cystic (ESC) RCC, ALK-rearranged RCC
- **Renamed/redefined entities**: FH-deficient RCC (now includes all fumarate hydratase-deficient tumours), SDH-deficient RCC (expanded), SMARCB1-deficient RCC (new name for what was renal medullary carcinoma and related tumours)
- **Nomenclature change**: Clear cell papillary RCC → **Clear cell papillary renal cell tumour** (removed "carcinoma" — it's indolent)
- **Classification change**: Papillary RCC type 1/2 → abolished; now just "papillary RCC" with molecular subtyping
- **Expanded MiT family**: TFE3-rearranged and TFEB-altered RCC — now separate entities with expanded molecular scope

## The New Entities — One by One

### 1. ELOC-Mutated RCC

**Previously:** These were misclassified as clear cell RCC because of morphologic overlap.

**Molecular basis:** Somatic mutation in the **ELOC gene** (previously called TCEB1 — Elongin C). ELOC is part of the VHL ubiquitin ligase complex, but these tumours are **VHL wild-type**. The ELOC mutation disrupts the complex differently.

**Morphology:**
- Clear cells with eosinophilic cytoplasm
- Prominent fibromuscular stroma — the key morphologic clue
- Generally low nuclear grade
- Resembles clear cell RCC on H&E but the stroma is distinctive

**IHC:**
- CK7 **positive** (unlike conventional clear cell RCC which is CK7 negative)
- Carbonic anhydrase IX (CAIX) **positive**
- CD10 variable

**Prognosis:** Generally favourable — behaves better than stage-matched clear cell RCC.

**Exam angle:** Any question where you see "clear cell morphology + CK7 positive" — think ELOC-mutated RCC. Clear cell RCC is CK7 negative; that single marker should redirect your thinking.

---

### 2. Eosinophilic Solid and Cystic (ESC) RCC

**Molecular basis:** Somatic (sporadic) or germline (tuberous sclerosis complex) mutations in **TSC1 or TSC2**. In patients with tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC), these were previously misclassified or grouped loosely with other oncocytic tumours.

**Morphology:**
- **Biphasic** architecture: solid sheets alternating with cystic spaces
- Abundant **eosinophilic cytoplasm** with coarse granules — sometimes described as "flocculent"
- Cells often resemble oncocytoma or chromophobe RCC on low power
- Intracytoplasmic stippling (hyaline globules) visible on H&E

**IHC — The Key Pattern:**
- **CK20 positive** (focal dot-like positivity) — **highly characteristic and specific**
- CK7 negative to focally positive
- CD117 positive
- Cathepsin K positive
- Vimentin negative

**Clinical associations:**
- Sporadic cases: young-to-middle-aged women, solitary tumour
- TSC-associated: younger patients, multifocal, bilateral, known TSC diagnosis

**Prognosis:** Generally indolent. Rare aggressive cases reported.

**Exam pearl:** CK20 positivity in a renal tumour is almost diagnostic of ESC RCC. No other major RCC subtype is CK20 positive (CK20 is normally a colon/urothelial marker). This is a favourite examiner trick.

---

### 3. ALK-Rearranged RCC

**Molecular basis:** Rearrangements involving the **ALK gene** on chromosome 2p23. Partners include VCL (most common), TPM3, EML4, and HOOK1.

**Classic clinical association:** **Sickle cell trait** — patients are often young (children or young adults) with sickle cell trait, not disease. This is one of two renal tumour-sickle cell trait associations you must know (the other being SMARCB1-deficient RCC).

**Morphology:**
- Papillary or solid architecture
- Cells with abundant pale or eosinophilic cytoplasm
- **Mucinous extracellular matrix** — distinctive and characteristic
- Psammoma bodies in some cases
- Spindle cell areas may be present

**IHC:**
- **ALK positive** (nuclear or cytoplasmic, depending on partner gene)
- Vimentin positive
- EMA positive
- Negative for CD10, RCC antigen

**Prognosis:** Variable — some cases have behaved aggressively, others are indolent. Importantly, ALK-rearranged tumours may be **amenable to ALK inhibitors** (crizotinib, alectinib) — a direct therapeutic implication.

**Exam pearl:** Young patient + sickle cell trait + renal tumour + ALK-positive IHC = ALK-rearranged RCC. The therapeutic angle (ALK inhibitors) is also fair game in newer FRCPath papers.

## Redefined / Expanded Entities

### 4. FH-Deficient RCC

**Syndrome:** Hereditary Leiomyomatosis and Renal Cell Carcinoma (HLRCC) — germline **FH (fumarate hydratase)** mutation. Now expanded to include sporadic FH-deficient cases.

**Morphology:**
- Papillary or tubulopapillary architecture
- Large cells with extremely prominent nucleoli — described as **"owl-eye" nucleoli** with a clear halo
- Eosinophilic cytoplasm, nuclear atypia

**IHC:**
- **FH protein lost** (absent staining — most specific)
- **2-succino-cysteine (2SC) positive** — a surrogate marker because fumarate accumulates and reacts with cysteine residues; detected by a specific antibody
- SDHB retained (distinguishes from SDH-deficient RCC)

**Prognosis:** **Aggressive** — stage II and above at diagnosis is common. Even small tumours can metastasise.

**Exam pearl:** HLRCC patients also develop **cutaneous and uterine leiomyomas** (hence the name). If the question mentions a young woman with uterine fibroids and a renal tumour, FH-deficient RCC should be your first thought. Couple that with "owl-eye nucleoli" and it's confirmed.

---

### 5. SDH-Deficient RCC

**Molecular basis:** Mutations in SDH subunit genes — **SDHB** (most common), SDHC, SDHD, SDHA.

**Association:** SDH-deficient tumours form a spectrum: paraganglioma, GIST, pituitary adenoma, and RCC. Know this as the **"SDH-deficient tumour syndrome"** for exam purposes.

**Morphology:**
- Solid nests of cells with abundant **pale, flocculent cytoplasm** — vacuolated with intracytoplasmic inclusions
- Cells appear to have "bubbly" cytoplasm
- Low nuclear grade, no necrosis typically

**IHC:**
- **SDHB lost** in ALL SDH-deficient tumours regardless of which subunit is mutated — because loss of any subunit destabilises the whole complex
- SDHA additionally lost only when SDHA itself is mutated

**Prognosis:** Generally indolent but with metastatic potential at high stage.

---

### 6. SMARCB1-Deficient RCC

**Previously:** This category now consolidates what were called **renal medullary carcinoma (RMC)** and other SMARCB1/INI1-deficient renal tumours.

**Molecular basis:** Loss of **SMARCB1 (INI1)** — a chromatin remodelling complex subunit.

**Classic association:** **Sickle cell trait** (RMC) — almost exclusively. This is the other sickle cell trait–renal tumour association (see ALK-rearranged above for the other).

**Morphology (RMC-type):**
- Young patients (mean age ~25 years)
- Reticular or yolk sac–like architecture
- Marked cytologic atypia, prominent nucleoli
- Desmoplastic stroma
- Admixed neutrophils

**IHC:**
- **SMARCB1 (INI1) completely lost** — absent staining
- Vimentin, CAM5.2, EMA positive
- OCT3/4 may be positive

**Prognosis:** **Very aggressive** — most patients present with metastatic disease. Median survival <13 months. One of the most lethal renal tumours.

## Nomenclature Changes You Must Know

### Clear Cell Papillary Renal Cell Tumour (formerly Carcinoma)

The "carcinoma" designation was dropped — it's now a **tumour** because long-term follow-up confirms essentially no metastatic potential.

**IHC pattern** (unchanged, but still testable):
- CK7 **diffusely positive**
- CAIX positive — distinctive **"cup-shaped" / basolateral** pattern (not circumferential as in clear cell RCC)
- CD10 **negative**
- AMACR (racemase) **negative**
- VHL **wild-type**

This IHC combination is practically diagnostic. The cup-shaped CAIX is the most characteristic single feature. See our [FRCPath Part 1 course](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-histopathology-course) for annotated IHC image sets showing these staining patterns.

### Papillary RCC — Type 1/2 Classification Abandoned

Type 1 and type 2 designations are no longer used in WHO 5th edition. The new approach:
- **MET-altered papillary RCC** (corresponds to most former type 1; indolent)
- **Papillary RCC with CDKN2A homozygous deletion** (aggressive — poor prognosis)
- Remaining cases: just "papillary RCC" without molecular qualifier

## Exam Pearls & Mnemonics

**"LOST markers tell the story"** — the new RCC entities are largely defined by what is LOST on IHC:
- **FH lost** → FH-deficient RCC
- **SDHB lost** → SDH-deficient RCC
- **INI1/SMARCB1 lost** → SMARCB1-deficient RCC

**"CK20 = ESC, CK7 = Clear cell papillary"** — the two CK exceptions in kidney:
- CK20 positivity in kidney → ESC RCC (normally a colon marker)
- Diffuse CK7 in kidney + cup CAIX → Clear cell papillary tumour

**Sickle cell trait + kidney tumour** → two possibilities:
1. **ALK-rearranged RCC** — younger, ALK+
2. **SMARCB1-deficient RCC (RMC)** — very young, INI1 lost, extremely aggressive

**The "Owl-Eye Nucleolus" Rule** → FH-deficient RCC. This morphology is also seen in cytomegalovirus inclusions — but in context of a renal tumour in a young woman with leiomyomas, it's FH-deficient RCC.

**TFEB biphasic pattern** → Large cells + small cells clustered around PAS-positive hyaline basement membrane globules. The small cells are the key — they resemble podocytes surrounding glomerular-like structures.

I've found in [our MCQ bank](https://elearningfrcpath.com/pathology-mcq-2026) that questions on these entities often combine clinical history (sickle cell trait, young age, family history of leiomyomas), morphologic description, and a single IHC result — and ask you to name the entity. The pattern becomes immediately recognisable once you've practised it a few times.

## Practice MCQs

**Q1.** A 28-year-old woman with known tuberous sclerosis complex undergoes nephrectomy for a 3.5 cm renal mass. Histology shows solid and cystic architecture with abundant eosinophilic granular cytoplasm. IHC: CK20 positive (dot-like), CK7 focally positive, CD117 positive. What is the diagnosis?

- A) Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma
- B) Oncocytoma
- C) Eosinophilic solid and cystic RCC
- D) SDH-deficient renal cell carcinoma

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Eosinophilic solid and cystic (ESC) RCC**

The key features are: TSC association, solid + cystic architecture, eosinophilic cytoplasm, and — most specifically — **CK20 dot-like positivity**. No other major RCC subtype is CK20 positive. Chromophobe RCC is CK7 diffusely positive and has characteristic "raisinoid" nuclei and perinuclear halos. Oncocytoma is CD117 positive but CK20 negative. SDH-deficient RCC has vacuolated bubbly cytoplasm and SDHB loss. ESC RCC is the correct answer here.

</details>

---

**Q2.** A 19-year-old man with sickle cell trait presents with haematuria. CT shows a 4 cm centrally located right renal mass. Resection reveals sheets of highly atypical cells with reticular architecture, desmoplastic stroma, and admixed neutrophils. IHC: SMARCB1 (INI1) **completely absent**, Vimentin positive, EMA positive. What is the prognosis?

- A) Excellent — this tumour is effectively benign
- B) Good — 5-year survival >80% with surgery alone
- C) Intermediate — responds well to targeted therapy
- D) Very poor — median survival under 13 months even with treatment

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: D — Very poor — median survival under 13 months even with treatment**

This is **SMARCB1-deficient RCC** (formerly renal medullary carcinoma). The classic scenario: young patient + sickle cell trait + central renal location + reticular architecture + INI1 loss. This is one of the most aggressive renal malignancies known. Most patients present with stage III–IV disease. Median survival is under 13 months. Neither surgery nor conventional chemotherapy produces durable remissions. Platinum-based regimens are used palliatively. This question tests that you don't confuse this entity's dramatic morphology with a tumour that responds to treatment.

</details>

---

**Q3.** A nephrectomy specimen from a 45-year-old woman shows a renal tumour with papillary architecture and cells containing very prominent nucleoli with a clear halo ("owl-eye" appearance). IHC: FH protein **absent**, 2SC **positive**, SDHB retained. Her sister was recently diagnosed with multiple uterine leiomyomas. What gene is mutated?

- A) VHL
- B) SDHB
- C) TSC2
- D) FH (Fumarate Hydratase)

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: D — FH (Fumarate Hydratase)**

All features point to **FH-deficient RCC** in the context of **Hereditary Leiomyomatosis and Renal Cell Carcinoma (HLRCC) syndrome**: owl-eye nucleoli, FH protein loss by IHC, 2SC positivity (fumarate accumulates and succinylates proteins — detected by the 2SC antibody), retained SDHB (which rules out SDH-deficient RCC), and the family history of uterine leiomyomas. VHL mutations cause clear cell RCC. SDHB loss would show absent SDHB staining. TSC2 mutations cause ESC RCC. FH germline mutation is the answer.

</details>

## FAQs

**Q: Is SMARCB1-deficient RCC the same as renal medullary carcinoma?**

Largely yes, but not entirely. The WHO 5th Edition uses "SMARCB1-deficient RCC" as the overarching category and includes renal medullary carcinoma (RMC) as the prototypical form — strongly associated with sickle cell trait, young patients, and a specific reticular/yolk-sac-like morphology. However, the category also encompasses SMARCB1-deficient renal tumours that don't fit the classic RMC morphology or lack the sickle cell association. In exam questions, when you see sickle cell trait + young patient + aggressive central renal tumour + INI1 loss — think SMARCB1-deficient RCC (RMC-type). The prognosis is uniformly grim.

---

**Q: How do I distinguish ESC RCC from chromophobe RCC on IHC?**

This is a common source of confusion because both have eosinophilic cytoplasm and CD117 positivity. The key differentiator is **CK20**: ESC RCC is **CK20 positive** (focal, dot-like), while chromophobe RCC is **CK20 negative**. Chromophobe RCC also shows diffuse CK7 positivity (in contrast to the weak/focal CK7 in ESC RCC) and the characteristic Hale's colloidal iron positivity. ESC RCC has solid + cystic architecture (not the typical solid sheets or tubular patterns of chromophobe), and the cytoplasmic texture is coarsely granular rather than the perinuclear clearing seen in chromophobe. TSC association and younger female predominance also favour ESC RCC.

---

**Q: Why was the "type 1" and "type 2" classification of papillary RCC abandoned?**

The type 1/2 classification was morphology-based but biologically incoherent. "Type 2" in particular was a heterogeneous group containing tumours with vastly different molecular alterations and clinical outcomes — including FH-deficient RCC, TFEB-altered RCC, and true papillary RCC with CDKN2A deletion. By abandoning the type system, WHO 5th Edition pushes pathologists toward molecular characterisation: MET-altered (indolent, formerly type 1) vs. CDKN2A-deleted (aggressive) vs. unclassified papillary RCC. This shift mirrors what's happened in CNS tumours and lymphomas — morphology alone is no longer sufficient for classification.

---

**Q: Does the name change from "carcinoma" to "tumour" for clear cell papillary RCC affect management?**

Yes — that's exactly why the change was made. Long-term follow-up data showed that clear cell papillary RCC (now renamed clear cell papillary renal cell **tumour**) has no documented metastatic potential in pure form. Renaming it removes the implicit threat of the word "carcinoma" and should translate to less aggressive surveillance and avoidance of unnecessary adjuvant therapy. For exams, the key points remain unchanged: CK7 diffuse+, CAIX cup-shaped/basolateral+, CD10−, AMACR−, VHL wild-type. Recognise the morphology and IHC pattern — the name change is the new knowledge to add on top.

---

**Q: Can a patient have more than one of these new RCC entities?**

Yes — patients with germline syndromes can develop multiple tumour types. A TSC patient may develop ESC RCC alongside angiomyolipoma and clear cell RCC. An SDH-mutation carrier can have SDH-deficient paraganglioma, GIST, and SDH-deficient RCC simultaneously. This is why family history and syndromic features are essential clinical context — and why examiners sometimes embed a syndrome clue in the clinical stem. For a complete breakdown of hereditary RCC syndromes and their associated tumour types, the [FRCPath exam preparation guide](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-exam-guide-2026) covers these in a dedicated hereditary tumour chapter.

## Key Takeaways

- **Three new WHO 5th Edition RCC entities**: ELOC-mutated (CK7+, fibromuscular stroma), ESC RCC (CK20 dot+, TSC association), ALK-rearranged (ALK+, sickle cell trait, mucinous stroma)
- **"LOST" IHC rule**: FH lost → FH-deficient; SDHB lost → SDH-deficient; INI1/SMARCB1 lost → SMARCB1-deficient (RMC)
- **Sickle cell trait + kidney** = ALK-rearranged RCC (young, indolent) or SMARCB1-deficient RMC (very young, very aggressive)
- **Owl-eye nucleoli + papillary tumour** → FH-deficient RCC; check family history for leiomyomas
- **CK20 in kidney** = ESC RCC — remember this because CK20 is almost never positive in any renal tumour otherwise
- **Cup-shaped CAIX + CK7+ + CD10−** = Clear cell papillary renal cell tumour (no longer a "carcinoma")
- **Papillary type 1/2 abandoned** — now MET-altered vs. CDKN2A-deleted vs. unclassified
- All of these can appear in [NEET-SS and FRCPath MCQs](https://elearningfrcpath.com/) — practice the IHC pattern recognition, not just the names
