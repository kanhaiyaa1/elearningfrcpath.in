For Indian MD Pathology graduates, FRCPath has always felt like a distant goal. The UK-based exam centres, visa logistics, time off work, and cost barriers made it a "maybe later" qualification. But in 2026, something significant shifted.

From Autumn 2026, the Royal College of Pathologists of the United Kingdom (RCPath) is expanding overseas centres for the **FRCPath Part 2 Histopathology exam**, and applications are now open. For many Indian pathologists, this is a watershed moment.

This isn't just an administrative convenience. It's a structural change that redefines whether FRCPath fits into a realistic India-based career path. Let's unpack what's changing, why it matters, and how you can use it as a real career lever.

---

## What Changed: The Overseas Expansion

Historically, FRCPath Part 2 Histopathology was predominantly UK-centred. If you wanted to take the exam, you had to:

- Travel to the United Kingdom for the exam date
- Manage visa applications and timelines
- Coordinate extended time off from your job or training
- Bear significant financial costs (travel, accommodation, exam fees)

For Indian pathologists mid-career—whether as DM residents, fellows, or junior consultants—these barriers were often insurmountable. Many simply shelved the idea.

**Now, RCPath has announced:**

> "The College is looking to expand its available overseas centres for the Part 2 Histopathology examination from Autumn 2026 and is now accepting applications for that."

In practical terms:

- From Autumn 2026, there will be exam centres **outside the UK** for Part 2 Histopathology
- Applications for these overseas seats are **already open**
- RCPath will announce the specific cities/countries progressively
- The expansion signals a deliberate policy shift toward global accessibility

This is a policy change, not a pilot project. It reflects RCPath's recognition that international candidates represent the future of the specialty.

## How FRCPath Adds Value in 2026

FRCPath does not automatically open UK jobs. But it does three critical things:

### 1. It Establishes a Global Standard

FRCPath is:
- Recognized across UK NHS and private pathology networks
- Increasingly valued in the Middle East, Europe, and multinational Asian labs
- A credential that signals standardized, internationally-benchmarked training

For Indian pathologists, this translates to:
- Stronger applications for consultant roles in multinational hospitals
- Competitive positioning in private diagnostic networks (diagnostic chains, oncopathology labs)
- A recognizable credential if you move to the Middle East or eventually to the UK

### 2. It Differentiates You in India's Competitive Market

India's pathology market is crowded. An MD Pathology from a top institute is table stakes. FRCPath, however, is:
- Rare among Indian pathologists
- Difficult to obtain (two-part exam, international standard)
- A story: "I trained to international standards in histopathology"

In specialties like **oncopathology**, **haematopathology**, and **molecular pathology**—where FRCPath places strong emphasis—this credential can be the differentiator between you and 10 other equally qualified candidates.

### 3. It Future-Proofs Your Career Options

If you later decide to:
- Move to the UK
- Work in the Middle East (especially diagnostic labs in UAE, KSA)
- Join a multinational lab network

FRCPath:
- Makes your CV materially stronger
- Is sometimes a stated requirement in job postings
- Shows commitment to international-standard practice

## Combining FRCPath Part 1 + NEET-SS 2026 (If You're Dual Targeting)

Many Indian pathologists are dual-targeting:
- **NEET-SS 2026 Pathology** (for DM in India)
- **FRCPath Part 1** (for international qualification)

This is feasible if you **share a core knowledge base** and layer exam-specific strategies.

### The Shared Core (What Overlaps)

Both exams test:
- **General pathology**: Cell injury, inflammation, neoplasia, apoptosis
- **Systemic pathology**: Lung, GI, breast, kidney, liver, nervous system
- **Oncopathology**: WHO classifications, grading systems, prognostic markers
- **Haematopathology**: Leukemias, lymphomas, marrow patterns, flow markers
- **Immunohistochemistry**: Marker panels, interpretation

### The Difference (Exam Style)

| **Aspect** | **NEET-SS 2026** | **FRCPath Part 1** |
|---|---|---|
| **Format** | MCQ (150 Qs, 3.5 hours) | MCQ (100 Qs, 3 hours) |
| **Timing** | Sectional (50 min per section) | Continuous (no sectional break) |
| **Emphasis** | Image spotters, pattern recognition, PYQs | Clinical scenarios, reasoning, morphology-to-diagnosis logic |
| **Knowledge Depth** | Broad coverage, high-yield lists | Deeper pathophysiology, differential diagnosis |
| **Difficulty** | MCQ-driven, pattern-based | Concept-based, clinically oriented |

### A Realistic Dual-Prep Timeline

**If NEET-SS is in December 2026:**
- FRCPath Part 1: Sept/Oct 2026
- NEET-SS: Dec 2026
- Gap: 2–3 months (tight but manageable)

**If NEET-SS is in March 2027:**
- FRCPath Part 1: Sept/Oct 2026
- NEET-SS: March 2027
- Gap: 4–5 months (comfortable)

### Practical Dual-Prep Strategy

1. **Core phase (Months 1–4)**: Build the shared knowledge base
   - Master histopathology (H&E morphology, normal anatomy)
   - Study haematopathology systematically
   - Learn oncopathology classifications (WHO 5th edition)
   - Build IHC marker knowledge

2. **NEET-SS layer (Months 4–5)**: Add exam-specific skills
   - Solve NEET-SS PYQs and image spotters
   - Practice sectional timing (50 min per section)
   - Refine pattern-recognition speed

3. **FRCPath layer (Months 5–6)**: Add conceptual depth
   - Solve FRCPath-style MCQs (more scenario-heavy, less pure image spotting)
   - Study clinical correlation and differential diagnosis
   - Build reasoning skills, not just pattern matching

This shared-core approach reduces total study load and makes dual prep realistic without burnout.

## Practice MCQs

**Q1. A 42-year-old woman undergoing routine colonoscopy is found to have a polyp in the sigmoid colon. Histopathology shows glandular dysplasia with loss of nuclear polarity, increased mitotic activity, and mucin production. There is no invasion beyond the muscularis mucosae. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the prognostic significance of this finding?**

- A) Early gastric cancer with a good prognosis; endoscopic mucosal resection is curative
- B) High-grade dysplasia in Barrett's oesophagus; chemotherapy is indicated
- C) High-grade intraepithelial neoplasia in the colon; en bloc endoscopic resection is the treatment of choice
- D) Invasive adenocarcinoma; surgical resection with lymph node dissection is mandatory

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — High-Grade Dysplasia with Strong Prognosis**

This case describes high-grade dysplasia (HGD) or carcinoma in situ in colonic mucosa. The key features are:
- Glandular dysplasia with loss of nuclear polarity
- Increased mitotic activity and mucin production
- **Absence of invasion beyond muscularis mucosae** (this is the critical feature separating dysplasia from invasive cancer)

High-grade dysplasia in the colon has a **very good prognosis** because it has not yet invaded the submucosa. The standard of care is **en bloc endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR)** or endoscopic submucosal dissection (ESD), which is curative in >95% of cases. This is why identifying this lesion early—before invasion—is prognostically significant.

Invasive adenocarcinoma (choice D) would show tumour cells breaching the muscularis mucosae and invading the submucosa or beyond, which is not present here.

</details>

**Q2. A 35-year-old male with a history of chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML) on imatinib therapy for 5 years develops a new BCR-ABL1 mutation (T315I) detected on molecular testing. His bone marrow shows progressive blast transformation with 25% blasts, increased basophils, and dysmorphic megakaryocytes. What is the next best step in management according to current RCPath and CAP guidelines?**

- A) Continue imatinib and increase the dose
- B) Switch to a second-generation tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI); if T315I is confirmed, consider ponatinib
- C) Initiate allogeneic stem cell transplantation immediately
- D) Add chemotherapy to imatinib

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Switch to Second-Generation TKI; Consider Ponatinib for T315I Mutation**

The T315I mutation in BCR-ABL1 is a **classic gatekeeper mutation** that confers resistance to first-generation TKIs like imatinib. This patient also shows blast transformation (>20% blasts), which indicates accelerated phase or blast crisis CML.

Current guidelines (NCCN, ESMO, RCPath) recommend:
1. **First step**: Confirm the T315I mutation
2. **Next step**: Switch to a second-generation TKI (dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib)
3. **If T315I is confirmed**: These drugs are also ineffective. **Ponatinib** is the only approved TKI active against T315I

The patient may also be a candidate for **allogeneic stem cell transplantation** if ponatinib induction fails, but that's not the immediate next step.

Increasing imatinib dose (choice A) is futile with T315I. Chemotherapy alone (choice D) is outdated management.

</details>

**Q3. A 28-year-old woman with stage 1B2 squamous cell carcinoma of the cervix (tumour size 4.5 cm) undergoes radical hysterectomy and pelvic lymph node dissection. Histopathology shows a keratinising SCC with deep stromal invasion but no lymph node metastases. The surgical margins are negative. Immunohistochemistry for p16 is diffuse and strong, and HPV16 is detected by in situ hybridisation. What is the risk of recurrence, and should adjuvant therapy be offered?**

- A) Low risk; no adjuvant therapy needed; follow-up surveillance is sufficient
- B) Intermediate risk; consider concurrent chemoradiation if high-risk features are present (deep stromal invasion, lymphovascular invasion)
- C) High risk; adjuvant concurrent chemoradiation is indicated
- D) Intermediate-to-high risk; adjuvant radiation alone is sufficient without chemotherapy

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Intermediate Risk; Adjuvant Chemoradiation if High-Risk Features**

Stage 1B squamous cell carcinoma of the cervix (≤4 cm) with **negative lymph nodes** has relatively **low-to-intermediate risk** of recurrence. However, this patient has **deep stromal invasion**, which is a high-risk feature for recurrence even in early-stage disease.

Current guidelines (NCCN, GOG, ESGO) recommend:
- **No lymph node metastases + negative margins** = generally good prognosis
- **Deep stromal invasion + large tumour (4.5 cm)** = consider adjuvant therapy
- **Gold standard**: Concurrent chemoradiation (platinum-based) reduces recurrence by ~30–40% in high-risk early-stage disease

The p16 and HPV16 positivity confirm HPV-driven carcinoma, but this does **not change adjuvant recommendations**—it provides prognostic information.

Choice A (no adjuvant) would be reasonable for very low-risk cases (<2 cm, superficial invasion, no lymphovascular invasion). This case warrants at least considering adjuvant therapy given tumour size and depth of invasion.

</details>

## FAQs

**Q: I haven't passed Part 1 yet. Can I apply for Part 2 overseas centres now?**

A: No. You must first pass FRCPath Part 1 and meet the postgraduate training requirement (typically 1 year of ST2-equivalent histopathology experience). However, if you pass Part 1 in November 2026, you would be eligible to apply for Part 2 in Spring 2027 or later, with the overseas centre option available.

---

**Q: Will overseas Part 2 centres be cheaper than the UK?**

A: Possibly. Exam fees may be similar, but travel and accommodation costs will be significantly lower if the centre is in Asia (likely India, Singapore, or Middle East). However, RCPath has not yet published fee details for overseas centres. Check the official RCPath website once the specific cities are announced.

---

**Q: Can I prepare for FRCPath and NEET-SS simultaneously without burnout?**

A: Yes, if you use a **shared-core strategy**. Both exams test the same foundational knowledge (histopathology, haematopathology, oncopathology, IHC). The difference is style, not content. By mastering the core deeply, you can layer exam-specific skills (NEET-SS image spotting vs. FRCPath reasoning) with minimal additional effort. Most dual-aspirants spend 3–4 hours daily on core, then 1–2 hours on exam-specific practice.

---

**Q: Is FRCPath recognized in India for job applications?**

A: Increasingly, yes. Multinational diagnostic chains, oncopathology-focused labs, and premium private hospitals recognize FRCPath as a marker of international-standard training. It's not a requirement, but it's a differentiator—especially in specialties like oncopathology and haematopathology. For government jobs or traditional academic postings, FRCPath adds credential value but may not directly increase salary.

---

**Q: If I take Part 2 overseas, do I still get the "FRCPath" title awarded?**

A: Yes. Passing Part 2 anywhere (UK or overseas) grants you the FRCPath fellowship. The overseas location does not diminish the credential. The exam standard, difficulty, and grading are identical to UK centres.
