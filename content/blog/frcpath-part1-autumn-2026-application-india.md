## Why This Session Specifically Matters

This is not a routine application deadline. **Autumn 2026 is the last sitting of FRCPath Part 1 in the current examination format.** From Spring 2027, a new format commences — RCPath has confirmed this.

What that means in practice:

- Every question bank, mock test, past paper, and set of notes built for the current format will need to be rebuilt or substantially revised for the new format. There will be a transition period where preparation resources are incomplete.
- Candidates who have been building preparation within the current format — familiar with BOF/EMQ structure, current syllabus weighting, image types tested — are at a relative disadvantage the moment they defer to Spring 2027.
- The "I'll do the next sitting" logic does not hold here the way it normally would. Next sitting = different exam.

If you are at 60–70% readiness in your core subjects right now, the rational choice is to apply for Autumn 2026 and use the next 3 months to close the gap — not to wait for a different exam you have not prepared for.

## Why FRCPath Matters for Indian Pathologists

FRCPath is not just another credential to add to a CV. For Indian pathologists, it functions as:

**A global portability document.** The Fellowship is recognised in the UK, most Commonwealth countries, the Middle East (many Gulf hospitals explicitly list FRCPath as a preferred or required qualification), and in an increasing number of private multinational lab networks operating in India. The MD/DM combination opens Indian career pathways; FRCPath opens international ones.

**A standardised benchmark.** Unlike internal institutional assessments, FRCPath is externally set, externally moderated, and internationally normed. Passing it tells any employer — anywhere — that your diagnostic reasoning and histopathological knowledge has been tested against a defined global standard.

**A career differentiator at the consultant level.** At the point where two candidates have similar MD/DM credentials, the FRCPath Fellowship is a visible, credible differentiator. For academic positions, research collaborations, and senior lab leadership roles in international organisations operating in India, it carries weight that purely domestic qualifications do not.

## What FRCPath Part 1 Actually Tests (Honest Assessment)

FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology is MCQ-based:

- **Paper 1:** 90 Best-of-5 (BOF) questions — 2.5 hours
- **Paper 2:** 60 Extended Matching Questions (EMQs) — 2.5 hours

The emphasis is on **histopathology pattern recognition, clinical correlation, and conceptual reasoning** — not on the exhaustive marker-list recall that dominates NEET-SS preparation. The difference:

- NEET-SS asks: "Which marker is positive in angiosarcoma?" FRCPath asks: "A 58-year-old presents with a splenic mass after prior irradiation. The biopsy shows anastomosing vascular channels lined by atypical endothelial cells with high-grade nuclear features. Which immunohistochemical finding most supports this diagnosis?" — and you need to apply the marker knowledge, not just recall it.
- NEET-SS tests breadth across all pathology. FRCPath tests depth within histopathology, haematopathology, and oncopathology with an expectation of clinical integration.
- Image-based questions appear in both — but FRCPath's image scenarios are more complex, often linking histology to IHC to molecular to clinical outcome.

If you have done NEET-SS or INI-SS preparation, you already have the content foundation. What you need to develop is the **application layer** — the ability to reason through a clinical scenario rather than pattern-match to a memorised answer.

## The Hidden Trap: Stacking FRCPath With NEET-SS

Many Indian aspirants in 2026 are targeting both FRCPath Part 1 and NEET-SS Pathology. This is entirely achievable, but only if the timeline is planned explicitly.

**NEET-SS 2026 Pathology** is tentatively scheduled for December 11–12, 2026, with a possible shift to the 4th week of March 2027 per updated NBEMS calendar.

That gives you two scenarios:

**Scenario A — FRCPath (Sept/Oct 2026) → NEET-SS (Dec 2026):** 2–3 month gap. Tight, but viable. The knowledge built for FRCPath directly feeds NEET-SS content; the pivot is primarily about format (from BOF/EMQ style to 50-minute section format) and image-MCQ volume (NEET-SS has a higher proportion of digital pathology images than FRCPath Part 1).

**Scenario B — FRCPath (Sept/Oct 2026) → NEET-SS (March 2027):** 4–5 month gap. More comfortable. After FRCPath, you have time for a structured NEET-SS pivot — additional image drilling, sectional mock calibration, and a thorough PYQ analysis cycle.

The failure mode that ends both exams is preparing for neither properly because you are vaguely trying to do both at once. The solution is a phase-based plan with explicit switching points — which is what the section below provides.

The content overlap between FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology and NEET-SS Pathology (Oncopathology/Haematopathology) is approximately 70% — WHO 5th Edition tumour classification, IHC marker panels, molecular markers, haematopathology. One set of notes can serve both, supplemented by format-specific practice. For the full month-by-month dual exam roadmap, the [FRCPath Autumn 2026 dual exam plan](https://elearningfrcpath.in/blog/frcpath-autumn-2026-application-neet-ss-dual-plan/) covers this in detail.

## A Realistic 3-Month Plan: June to September 2026

This plan assumes you apply this week and receive entry confirmation by early July. The exam period begins 21 September 2026.

### June 2026 — Foundation and Application

**Week 1 (now):** Apply. Do not delay this for any reason. You can apply before your preparation is perfect — the application secures your seat in the sitting; preparation fills the gap.

**Preparation target: 1–2 hours/day**

- FRCPath-aligned MCQs: 15–20 questions/day with full explanation review (not just marking right/wrong)
- Image spotters: 10–15 H&E and IHC images/day — focus on pattern recognition, not label recall
- Start an error notebook: for every wrong MCQ, write the concept you missed, the correct pattern, and one anchor sentence you will remember. This notebook becomes your most valuable revision tool in September.

Content priority for June: **General Pathology** — cell injury, inflammation, neoplasia, haemodynamics. These underpin everything and have the highest question density in Part 1.

### July 2026 — Intensity Build

**Preparation target: 2–3 hours/day**

- Histopathology core content: systemic pathology — GI, breast, lung, liver, gynaecological, skin, CNS (prioritise in that order)
- Haematopathology: acute leukaemias (AML/ALL — morphology, IHC, cytogenetics), lymphomas (Hodgkin and NHL subtypes), myeloproliferative neoplasms, bone marrow patterns
- Oncopathology high-yield: gene fusions, WHO 5th edition classification updates, therapy-linked predictive markers
- Add 1–2 full-length timed practice tests per week (BOF + EMQ format) — do not skip timing; exam stamina is a physical skill
- Error notebook: review and update after every test session

**Application deadline: 3 July 2026.** If you have not applied yet, this is your final day.

### August 2026 — Peak Preparation

**Preparation target: 3–4 hours/day**

- Full-length timed practice tests: 2–3 times/week
- Image-drill sessions: 20–30 images/day; prioritise speed (pattern recognition in under 30 seconds) over laborious analysis
- Weak-topic sprint: identify your top 2–3 weak areas from error notebook analysis (commonly: soft tissue tumours, paediatric tumours, lymphoma subtype differentiation) and spend 1–2 dedicated weeks on targeted MCQs and images for each
- IHC panel rapid review: go through your marker panels once per week — marker → tumour → clinical implication (not just marker → tumour)
- NEET-SS parallel maintenance: 1–2 NEET-SS sectional mocks per week to maintain rhythm without derailing FRCPath momentum

### September 2026 — Final Phase

**From 1–10 September:** Lighter content — no new topics after 10 September. Only revision: error notebook, high-yield tables (tumour marker panels, WHO classification updates, molecular triads), image patterns you have struggled with.

**Final 5–7 days before exam:**
- 1–2 short timed sessions per day (20–30 questions, not full-length)
- Sleep rhythm: set your sleep schedule to match your exam-day start time at least 5 days before
- Logistics confirmed: venue, travel, ID documents, entry letter from RCPath

**After the exam:** Regardless of how you feel it went, begin the NEET-SS pivot within 48 hours. The FRCPath content is fresh; the pivot is primarily to format and sectional pacing.

## Exam Pearls: FRCPath Part 1 Specific

**Pearl 1:** BOF questions in FRCPath are more clinically anchored than NEET-SS-style MCQs. The distinguishing detail is usually in the clinical scenario — a specific location, patient age, prior condition, or associated finding. If you are pattern-matching on morphology alone and ignoring clinical context, you will fail roughly 20% of BOF questions that require context to disambiguate between two morphologically similar options.

**Pearl 2:** EMQs reward option-list reading first. Read all 10–12 answer options before reading the first stem. This activates your recall for the full concept space of the question group — then each stem has an activated retrieval path rather than a cold search.

**Pearl 3:** No negative marking in FRCPath Part 1. Every unanswered question scores zero; a guess has a 20% expected return. Never leave a question blank — eliminate what you can and choose.

**Pearl 4:** Error notebooks are compounding assets. A notebook maintained from June through August accumulates your personal failure patterns. Reviewing it in the final two weeks before the exam covers more actual exam risk than reviewing any textbook chapter, because it is calibrated exactly to where your knowledge has gaps.

**Mnemonic — APPLY for the Autumn 2026 decision:**
- **A** — Act this week (apply before 3 July)
- **P** — Plan the dual-exam timeline explicitly (FRCPath + NEET-SS pivot point)
- **P** — Practise in format (BOF timed, EMQ option-first, image spotters daily)
- **L** — Log every error (error notebook — non-negotiable)
- **Y** — Your cutoff is 3 July. Everything else is flexible.

## Practice MCQs — FRCPath Part 1 BOF Style

**Q1.** A 47-year-old woman undergoes excision of a 1.8 cm left axillary lymph node. Histology shows effacement of nodal architecture by a proliferation of large cells with prominent central nucleoli ("owl-eye" appearance), abundant pale cytoplasm, and scattered inflammatory cells including eosinophils, plasma cells, and small lymphocytes. Fibrous bands divide the node into nodules. IHC: CD30 positive, CD15 positive, CD45 negative, PAX5 weakly positive, CD20 negative, EBV (EBER) negative. What is the most likely diagnosis and the most common subtype of this disease in young adults?

- A) Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma — the most common aggressive lymphoma in adults; CD20 positive
- B) Nodular sclerosis Hodgkin lymphoma — the most common subtype in young adults, characterised by fibrous bands and lacunar cell variant RS cells
- C) Nodular lymphocyte-predominant Hodgkin lymphoma — CD20 positive, CD30 negative, CD15 negative; LP ("popcorn") cells
- D) T-cell/histiocyte-rich large B-cell lymphoma — CD20 positive, background histiocytes, no RS cells

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Nodular sclerosis Hodgkin lymphoma (NSHL)**

The histological features — architectural effacement, Reed-Sternberg cells with owl-eye nucleoli, mixed inflammatory background (eosinophils, plasma cells, small lymphocytes), and collagenous fibrous bands dividing the node into nodules — are pathognomonic of **Nodular Sclerosis Hodgkin Lymphoma (NSHL)**. The IHC profile confirms classical Hodgkin lymphoma: **CD30+, CD15+, CD45−, PAX5 weakly+, CD20−** — the RS cell is of B-cell lineage (PAX5 weak+) but has lost most B-cell markers (CD20−, CD45−). NSHL is the most common subtype of Hodgkin lymphoma overall and particularly in young adults and adolescents (15–35 years), with a predilection for mediastinal/supraclavicular involvement. The "lacunar cell" variant of RS cells (cells with retracted cytoplasm creating a lacunar space in formalin-fixed tissue) is characteristic of NSHL. EBV negativity is typical of NSHL (in contrast to Mixed Cellularity HL which is strongly EBV-associated). Option C (NLPHL) is characterised by CD20+/CD30−/CD15−/CD45+ LP cells ("popcorn cells") — completely different IHC profile. Option D (T-cell/histiocyte-rich LBCL) has CD20+ large B-cells; option A (DLBCL) is CD20+ throughout. Both options A and D are excluded by the CD20− result.

</details>

**Q2.** A 63-year-old male smoker with a 40 pack-year history undergoes CT-guided biopsy of a 3.5 cm right upper lobe mass. Histology shows a tumour with glandular structures, cribriform architecture, and cells with mucin-producing columnar morphology. IHC: TTF-1 positive, Napsin A positive, CK7 positive, p40 negative, CK5/6 negative, synaptophysin negative. Molecular testing is requested. Which molecular alteration is most likely to be found, and what is its direct therapeutic implication?

- A) KRAS G12C mutation — amenable to sotorasib or adagrasib targeted therapy
- B) ALK rearrangement — amenable to crizotinib, alectinib, or lorlatinib
- C) EGFR exon 19 deletion or exon 21 L858R mutation — amenable to osimertinib as first-line TKI
- D) ROS1 rearrangement — amenable to entrectinib or crizotinib

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — EGFR exon 19 deletion or exon 21 L858R mutation; osimertinib first-line**

The morphology (glandular/cribriform architecture, mucin-producing columnar cells) and IHC (TTF-1+, Napsin A+, CK7+, p40−, CK5/6−, synaptophysin−) confirm **lung adenocarcinoma**. Among lung adenocarcinomas, the most common targetable driver mutation globally is **EGFR mutation** — present in approximately 15% of non-Asian and 40–50% of East Asian adenocarcinoma patients. The two most common sensitising EGFR mutations are exon 19 deletion (in-frame deletion of amino acids 746–750) and exon 21 L858R point mutation; together they account for ~90% of EGFR-mutant NSCLC. **Osimertinib** (3rd generation EGFR TKI, targets sensitising mutations + T790M resistance mutation) is first-line standard of care based on FLAURA trial data (superior PFS vs 1st gen TKIs, also crosses BBB for CNS metastases). KRAS G12C (option A) is the most common KRAS mutation in lung adenocarcinoma and is now targetable with sotorasib/adagrasib — but KRAS and EGFR mutations are almost mutually exclusive in lung adenocarcinoma; the scenario most likely represents EGFR-mutant disease given the clinical context. ALK (option B) and ROS1 (option D) rearrangements are less common (3–5% and 1–2% respectively) — both are correct answers in the right scenario, but EGFR mutation is the highest-probability finding in a 63-year-old smoker with lung adenocarcinoma.

</details>

**Q3.** A 28-year-old woman presents with cervical lymphadenopathy. Node biopsy shows a follicular architecture with expanded follicles containing centrocytes and centroblasts. The mantle zones are attenuated. IHC: CD20 positive, CD10 positive, BCL2 positive, BCL6 positive, CD5 negative, cyclin D1 negative. FISH demonstrates t(14;18)(q32;q21). The patient has Stage II disease. Which of the following statements about this tumour's biology and clinical behaviour is most accurate?

- A) BCL2 overexpression from t(14;18) renders this tumour highly aggressive; intensive chemotherapy is required immediately regardless of disease burden
- B) Follicular lymphoma is an indolent B-cell lymphoma; BCL2 overexpression prevents apoptosis of germinal centre B-cells; Stage II low-tumour-burden disease may be managed with watch-and-wait
- C) Cyclin D1 negativity excludes t(14;18); this tumour requires reclassification as mantle cell lymphoma with follicular pattern
- D) CD10 positivity in this context indicates a DLBCL with germinal centre B-cell (GCB) origin by Hans algorithm; R-CHOP is first-line

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Follicular lymphoma, indolent; BCL2 prevents apoptosis; watch-and-wait appropriate for low-tumour-burden Stage II**

The morphology (follicular architecture with centrocytes and centroblasts, attenuated mantle zones), IHC (CD20+, CD10+, BCL2+, BCL6+, CD5−, cyclin D1−), and FISH (t(14;18)) are classic for **Follicular Lymphoma (FL)**. The t(14;18)(q32;q21) juxtaposes the BCL2 gene (18q21) next to the IGH enhancer (14q32), leading to constitutive BCL2 overexpression. BCL2 is an anti-apoptotic protein — its overexpression in germinal centre B-cells prevents normal programmed cell death, allowing accumulation of malignant cells. FL is the prototypical **indolent B-cell lymphoma** — most patients live with it for years to decades, and in low-tumour-burden disease (limited disease, no bulky mass, no B symptoms, preserved marrow function), **watch-and-wait** (active surveillance without immediate treatment) is the evidence-based standard, as treatment does not improve overall survival in this setting. Option A is incorrect: FL is not aggressive — immediate intensive chemotherapy is not standard for low-tumour-burden FL. Option C is incorrect: cyclin D1 negativity excludes mantle cell lymphoma (which requires cyclin D1 overexpression from t(11;14)), not t(14;18). BCL2 positivity in FL is from t(14;18), not t(11;14). Option D is incorrect: this is morphologically follicular lymphoma — DLBCL would show diffuse architecture with sheets of large cells; and while CD10+ / BCL6+ does characterise GCB-DLBCL by Hans algorithm, the follicular architecture here is diagnostic of FL, not DLBCL.

</details>

## FAQs

**I am in my 2nd year of DM Pathology in India. Am I eligible to apply for FRCPath Part 1 Autumn 2026?**

Eligibility for FRCPath Part 1 requires a primary medical qualification (MBBS or equivalent) and postgraduate pathology training — you do not need to have completed your DM to sit Part 1. The key eligibility requirement is that you hold a recognised primary medical degree and have sufficient postgraduate training to meet the syllabus. Candidates currently in DM programmes have successfully sat FRCPath Part 1 mid-programme. Confirm your eligibility directly on the RCPath examinations page before applying — eligibility requirements can have nuances for international candidates, and the official source is always authoritative over any third-party guide.

**What happens if I apply and then cannot sit the exam?**

The withdrawal deadline for a full refund is **17 July 2026**. If your circumstances change after applying and before that date, you can withdraw and receive your full examination fee back. After 17 July, withdrawal may result in partial or no refund depending on RCPath policy — check the current fee and refund schedule at the time of application. This safety valve means applying in the first week of June involves no financial risk up to mid-July if something changes.

**Is 3 months enough time to prepare for FRCPath Part 1?**

It depends on your starting point. Candidates who have already been preparing for NEET-SS or INI-SS — and therefore have a working knowledge of WHO 5th Edition tumour classification, haematopathology, IHC panels, and oncopathology — can close the gap to FRCPath readiness in 3 months. The adjustment is not primarily content (70% overlaps with NEET-SS preparation) but format: developing the clinical reasoning and application-layer thinking that FRCPath BOF/EMQ questions demand, plus consistent image-spotter practice. Candidates starting from near-zero would find 3 months insufficient. The [FRCPath Part 1 preparation course at eLearning FRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-histopathology-course) is designed to accelerate this pivot for NEET-SS-prepared candidates specifically.

**Where are the FRCPath Part 1 international examination centres in India?**

RCPath has approved international examination centres in India, with cities varying by sitting. The confirmed city list for the Autumn 2026 sitting will be published on the RCPath examinations page — check this when the application opens (it is already open). In recent sittings, centres have included major cities. Select your preferred centre at the time of application; centre allocation is subject to capacity.

**If I fail FRCPath Part 1 in Autumn 2026, what changes about the Spring 2027 sitting?**

Spring 2027 will use a new FRCPath examination format. RCPath has confirmed this. The specifics of the new format — question types, structure, timing, syllabus emphasis — will be published by RCPath in advance. Candidates who need to resit after Autumn 2026 will face a format they have not previously encountered, with preparation materials still being developed. This is not insurmountable, but it is an additional challenge relative to resitting the known current format. It reinforces the argument for sitting Autumn 2026 when you are at or near readiness rather than waiting for Spring 2027.
