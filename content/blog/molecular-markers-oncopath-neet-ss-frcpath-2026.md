By 2026, NEET-SS and FRCPath-style oncopathology questions are no longer primarily about H&E morphology. They are increasingly about which **molecular marker predicts therapy response, prognosis, or diagnosis** — and the shift is accelerating. A candidate who can identify the classic morphology of a lung adenocarcinoma but cannot map EGFR, KRAS, ALK, and ROS1 to their therapeutic and prognostic implications is prepared for the exam of five years ago, not the one they are sitting now. This post covers the molecular markers that have become single-best-answer anchors in NEET-SS 2026 and FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology MCQs, explains how to build the marker-tumour-drug triads that solve those questions, and gives you a structured 10-day prep strategy for integrating molecular marker knowledge into your mock routine.

---

## Why Molecular Markers Are Now Core to NEET-SS and FRCPath Oncopath MCQs

The change in exam pattern reflects the change in diagnostic practice. In contemporary pathology reporting, a diagnosis without its molecular profile is an incomplete diagnosis for most oncology-relevant tumours. Lung adenocarcinoma without EGFR/KRAS/ALK/ROS1/BRAF/MET/RET/NTRK status is not a complete report for clinical decision-making. Colorectal carcinoma without MSI/MMR and KRAS/NRAS/BRAF is incomplete. Glioblastoma without IDH and MGMT methylation status is now a classification error under WHO CNS 5th Edition.

This clinical reality has translated directly into the examination. NEET-SS 2026-pattern questions test molecular markers in three ways:

**Therapy-prediction MCQs:** "A patient with lung adenocarcinoma has EGFR exon 19 deletion. Which of the following is the most appropriate first-line therapy?" The H&E diagnosis is given; the question hinges entirely on the molecular implication.

**Diagnostic-classification MCQs:** "A diffuse large B-cell lymphoma with MYC rearrangement AND BCL2 rearrangement is best classified as?" This tests knowledge of WHO 5th edition molecular classification, not morphology.

**IHC-panel-style MCQs:** "A breast carcinoma is ER+, PR−, HER2 2+ on IHC. What is the next appropriate test, and what is the most likely HER2 status?" This tests understanding of IHC interpretation guidelines, equivocal scoring, and FISH reflex.

None of these questions can be answered from morphology alone. The [NEET-SS pathology preparation course at eLearning FRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology) is structured around this modern exam pattern — molecular and IHC content is not an appendix to clinical pathology; it is woven into every system.

## The High-Yield Molecular Markers: Tumour by Tumour

### PIK3CA — Breast, Colorectal, Endometrial Cancer

**PIK3CA** encodes the catalytic subunit of PI3-kinase. Activating mutations (most commonly in exons 9 and 20 — E545K and H1047R) drive constitutive activation of the PI3K/AKT/mTOR signalling axis, promoting cell survival, proliferation, and resistance to anti-hormonal therapy.

**Why it matters for exams:**
- In **HR+/HER2− breast cancer**, PIK3CA mutation is the most common somatic mutation (~40% of cases). It predicts response to **alpelisib** (a PI3Kα inhibitor), combined with fulvestrant in post-menopausal patients with disease progression on endocrine therapy.
- In **colorectal carcinoma**, PIK3CA mutations (particularly exon 20) are associated with resistance to aspirin chemoprevention and may be a predictor of poor outcome in certain molecular subtypes.
- In **endometrial carcinoma**, PIK3CA mutation is common in endometrioid type (low-grade, POLE-mutated, and microsatellite-unstable subtypes) and is part of the molecular landscape tested in WHO-aligned classification questions.

**Exam triad:** PIK3CA mutation → HR+/HER2− breast cancer → alpelisib + fulvestrant.

### EGFR, KRAS, BRAF, ALK, ROS1 — Lung and Colorectal

**EGFR** (epidermal growth factor receptor, chromosome 7p12) mutations are the most clinically actionable alteration in **lung adenocarcinoma**. The most common sensitising mutations are exon 19 deletions (~45%) and exon 21 L858R point mutation (~40%). These predict response to EGFR TKIs (gefitinib, erlotinib, osimertinib). T790M is the most common acquired resistance mutation, addressed by third-generation TKI osimertinib.

**KRAS** mutation (most commonly G12C, G12V, G12D at codon 12) is the most frequent oncogenic driver in lung adenocarcinoma and colorectal carcinoma. Critically for FRCPath and NEET-SS:
- In **colorectal carcinoma**, KRAS and NRAS mutations (codons 12, 13, 59, 61, 117, 146) are **predictors of non-response to anti-EGFR therapy** (cetuximab, panitumumab). KRAS mutation = no benefit from cetuximab. This is the most commonly tested KRAS-related question.
- In **lung adenocarcinoma**, KRAS G12C is now targeted by sotorasib and adagrasib.

**BRAF V600E** mutation is relevant in:
- **Colorectal carcinoma**: associated with microsatellite-unstable, right-sided, poorly differentiated tumours; CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP-high). BRAF V600E in CRC = poor prognosis; not typically responsive to BRAF inhibitors alone (unlike melanoma) due to EGFR feedback reactivation — combination BRAF + EGFR inhibition is used.
- **Melanoma**: BRAF V600E in ~50% of cutaneous melanomas; targeted by vemurafenib/dabrafenib (BRAF inhibitors) often combined with trametinib (MEK inhibitor).
- **Papillary thyroid carcinoma**: most common point mutation (~45%); associated with extrathyroidal extension and lymph node metastasis.
- **Hairy cell leukaemia**: BRAF V600E is pathognomonic and diagnostic — its presence distinguishes classic HCL from HCL-variant and splenic marginal zone lymphoma.

**ALK rearrangement** (chromosome 2p23) occurs in ~5% of non-small cell lung cancers, predominantly adenocarcinoma. EML4-ALK is the most common fusion. Predicts response to ALK inhibitors (crizotinib, alectinib, brigatinib). IHC for ALK (clone D5F3 with amplified detection) is used as a screening test; FISH or NGS confirms. An important diagnostic correlation: ALK-rearranged lung adenocarcinoma is mutually exclusive with EGFR and KRAS mutations.

**ROS1 rearrangement** occurs in ~1–2% of NSCLCs; morphologically similar to ALK-rearranged carcinomas. Responds to crizotinib and entrectinib. ROS1 IHC (clone D4D6) has high sensitivity as a screening tool; FISH or NGS confirms.

### MGMT Promoter Methylation — Glioblastoma and CNS Tumours

**MGMT** (O6-methylguanine-DNA methyltransferase) encodes a DNA-repair enzyme that removes alkyl adducts from the O6 position of guanine. Tumour cells with **MGMT promoter methylation** cannot repair the DNA damage caused by alkylating chemotherapy (temozolomide), making them more sensitive to it.

**Why it matters for exams:**
- MGMT promoter methylation is the **best predictive biomarker for response to temozolomide** in glioblastoma (GBM).
- Under **WHO CNS 5th Edition**, IDH-wildtype glioblastoma with MGMT promoter methylation has significantly better prognosis than MGMT-unmethylated GBM.
- Testing method: pyrosequencing or methylation-specific PCR. IHC for MGMT protein expression is **not** a reliable surrogate — this is a trap in MCQs.
- MGMT promoter methylation also occurs in low-grade IDH-mutant gliomas, anaplastic gliomas, and some other tumour types, but its clinical and therapeutic significance is highest in GBM.

**IDH1/IDH2 mutations** (isocitrate dehydrogenase) deserve mention alongside MGMT because WHO CNS 5th Edition makes IDH status the primary molecular classifier for adult diffuse gliomas:
- **IDH-mutant** = WHO Grade 2–3 astrocytoma or oligodendroglioma (better prognosis)
- **IDH-wildtype** = glioblastoma, IDH-wildtype (poor prognosis regardless of histological grade)

IDH1 R132H accounts for ~90% of IDH mutations; IHC with clone H09 detects it reliably.

### HER2 Amplification — Breast, Gastric, and Beyond

**HER2** (ERBB2, chromosome 17q12) amplification leads to overexpression of the HER2 receptor tyrosine kinase, driving cell proliferation and survival. Predicts response to anti-HER2 therapies: trastuzumab, pertuzumab, T-DM1, and T-DXd.

**IHC scoring (ASCO/CAP guidelines — breast):**
- **0** — No staining or incomplete faint membrane staining in ≤10% of cells → Negative
- **1+** — Incomplete faint/barely perceptible staining in >10% of cells → Negative
- **2+** — Weak-to-moderate complete membrane staining in >10% of cells → **Equivocal → FISH reflex required**
- **3+** — Strong complete membrane staining in >10% of cells → Positive

The **2+ equivocal** category is the most examined category in MCQs — the correct next step is FISH (or ISH) for HER2 amplification.

**HER2-low** (IHC 1+ or 2+/FISH non-amplified) is now a clinically recognised entity predicting response to trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd), which was previously classified simply as "HER2-negative." This is a high-yield 2025–2026 update for both NEET-SS and FRCPath.

**Gastric and gastro-oesophageal junction (GOJ) adenocarcinoma:** HER2 IHC scoring criteria differ from breast — incomplete membrane staining is acceptable as positive in gastric carcinoma due to the basolateral expression pattern. IHC 3+ or IHC 2+/FISH amplified predicts response to trastuzumab (ToGA trial data).

### BCL2, BCL6, MYC — Lymphoma Classification and the "Double-Hit" Concept

In **B-cell lymphomas**, three chromosomal rearrangements — **MYC**, **BCL2**, and **BCL6** — define some of the most clinically important molecular subgroups:

**MYC rearrangement** (chromosome 8q24) with immunoglobulin partners (IGH@, IGL@, IGK@) is the defining event in Burkitt lymphoma. In DLBCL, MYC rearrangement without BCL2 or BCL6 rearrangement carries intermediate prognosis.

**Double-hit lymphoma (DHL):** MYC + BCL2 rearrangement (most common) or MYC + BCL6 rearrangement. WHO 5th Edition designates these as **High-grade B-cell lymphoma with MYC and BCL2 rearrangements** — a distinct entity from DLBCL, with poor prognosis with standard R-CHOP chemotherapy.

**Triple-hit lymphoma (THL):** MYC + BCL2 + BCL6 rearrangements — worst prognosis.

**Why this matters for IHC in MCQs:**
- IHC "double-expressors" (MYC protein >40%, BCL2 protein >50%) are NOT the same as double-hit lymphoma — IHC expression reflects protein level, not gene rearrangement. The distinction is a classic MCQ trap.
- Double-hit lymphoma requires FISH or cytogenetic confirmation of both rearrangements.
- The morphological appearance is typically DLBCL-like (large cell), not Burkitt-like, distinguishing DHL from Burkitt lymphoma at the H&E level.

## How to Map Molecular Markers to MCQ Types

The same marker can appear in completely different MCQ types depending on what the stem is testing. Knowing a marker exists is not enough — you need to know what dimension of that marker is being examined.

**Therapy-prediction questions:** Stem gives diagnosis + molecular result → asks for best treatment. Master the triads in the table above. The distractor is always a plausible-sounding drug for the same tumour that does not match the molecular profile.

**Prognosis questions:** Stem gives molecular result → asks which option correctly describes prognosis. MGMT methylation in GBM, IDH mutation in glioma, BCR-ABL1 in CML — all carry specific prognostic implications tested in this format.

**Next-step / investigation questions:** Stem gives IHC result (e.g., HER2 2+) → asks for next appropriate investigation. The answer is FISH/ISH, not a treatment decision. This tests knowledge of the testing algorithm, not just the biomarker.

**Classification questions:** Stem gives molecular profile → asks for correct WHO 5th Edition classification. IDH status in glioma, MYC + BCL2 rearrangement in DLBCL, and DNMT3A/NPM1/FLT3 in AML all fall into this MCQ type.

## A 10-Day Molecular Marker Prep Strategy

### Days 1–3: Build your triad bank

Take the table above and convert it into a personal flash-card set — one card per marker, with three fields: tumour, drug/implication, and one exam trap. Do not write essays; write triads. Spend 10–15 minutes per day adding cards and testing the existing ones.

### Days 4–6: Practise IHC-panel MCQs

Pull 15–20 MCQs from your question bank that specifically involve IHC results in the stem. For each question, before reading the options, write down: what tumour type does this IHC panel suggest? What molecular test should follow? What therapy implication does the IHC result carry? Answer questions from these three angles before selecting from the options.

### Days 7–8: WHO 5th Edition classification consolidation

Focus specifically on three areas: CNS tumour classification (IDH, MGMT, 1p/19q codeletion), lymphoma classification (MYC/BCL2/BCL6 rearrangements, double-hit vs. double-expressor), and AML molecular subgroups (NPM1, FLT3-ITD, CEBPA, core-binding factor). These are the three highest-yield molecular classification areas for both NEET-SS and FRCPath in 2026.

### Days 9–10: Timed molecular marker section mock

Run two 20-question timed blocks focused exclusively on molecular/IHC questions. After each block, classify your errors: was the error a missing triad (knowledge gap), a question-type mismatch (knew the marker but misread what was being asked), or a trap error (confused two similar markers or confused IHC expression with genetic alteration)?

## Exam Pearls and Mnemonic

**Pearl 1:** In colorectal carcinoma, KRAS/NRAS mutation testing is about **excluding** candidates for anti-EGFR therapy, not about identifying candidates for targeted therapy. Mutant KRAS/NRAS = no cetuximab or panitumumab. Wildtype KRAS/NRAS + BRAF V600E wildtype = eligible. This directionality is tested explicitly.

**Pearl 2:** The IHC panel for **dMMR (mismatch repair deficiency)** uses four proteins: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2. Loss of MLH1 is most commonly due to promoter hypermethylation (sporadic) and is accompanied by PMS2 loss (MLH1 forms a heterodimer with PMS2). Loss of MSH2 is accompanied by MSH6 loss (MSH2 forms a heterodimer with MSH6). In Lynch syndrome: germline mutations most commonly in MLH1 or MSH2. Loss of isolated MSH6 or PMS2 = lower MSI rate than dual-loss patterns.

**Pearl 3:** **BRAF V600E in hairy cell leukaemia** is pathognomonic. This is the only haematological malignancy with BRAF V600E as a near-universal defining mutation. IHC with anti-BRAF V600E (clone VE1) can substitute for molecular testing in routine practice.

**Pearl 4:** **IDH-wildtype glioblastoma** is defined by grade-defining molecular features in WHO CNS 5th Edition — a diffuse astrocytic glioma with EGFR amplification, +7/−10 chromosome pattern, or TERT promoter mutation is classified as GBM regardless of histological grade. A Grade 2 or 3 histology does not exclude GBM if these molecular features are present.

**Mnemonic — METHK for the 5 most-tested molecular-drug triads:**
- **M** — MGMT methylation → Glioblastoma → Temozolomide sensitivity
- **E** — EGFR del19/L858R → Lung adeno → Osimertinib/TKIs
- **T** — T(15;17) / PML-RARA → APL → ATRA + ATO
- **H** — HER2 amplification → Breast/Gastric → Trastuzumab
- **K** — KRAS mutation → Colorectal → No anti-EGFR (cetuximab excluded)

## Practice MCQs

**Q1.** A 52-year-old non-smoker woman presents with a 3.2 cm right upper lobe lung mass. CT-guided biopsy confirms lung adenocarcinoma. Molecular testing shows: EGFR exon 20 T790M mutation, KRAS wildtype, ALK FISH negative. She previously received gefitinib as first-line therapy. **Which of the following is the most appropriate next-line targeted therapy?**

- A) Erlotinib
- B) Afatinib
- C) Osimertinib
- D) Alectinib

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Osimertinib**

EGFR T790M is the most common mechanism of acquired resistance to first- and second-generation EGFR TKIs (gefitinib, erlotinib — first generation; afatinib, dacomitinib — second generation). T790M substitution at exon 20 sterically hinders binding of first- and second-generation TKIs to the ATP-binding pocket. Osimertinib is a third-generation, irreversible EGFR TKI with specific activity against T790M-mutated EGFR. Options A (erlotinib) and B (afatinib) are ineffective against T790M-mediated resistance. Alectinib (option D) is an ALK inhibitor — irrelevant here as ALK FISH is negative. This question tests knowledge of acquired EGFR resistance mechanisms, a consistently high-yield topic in both NEET-SS and FRCPath.

</details>

---

**Q2.** A 65-year-old male with a known diagnosis of diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) is found on FISH testing to have MYC rearrangement and BCL2 rearrangement. IHC shows MYC protein expression in 55% of cells and BCL2 expression in 70% of cells. **Which of the following correctly classifies this tumour under WHO Classification of Haematopoietic Tumours, 5th Edition?**

- A) DLBCL with double-expressor phenotype
- B) Burkitt lymphoma
- C) High-grade B-cell lymphoma with MYC and BCL2 rearrangements
- D) Plasmablastic lymphoma

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — High-grade B-cell lymphoma with MYC and BCL2 rearrangements**

This is a high-yield WHO 5th Edition classification question. The presence of **both MYC and BCL2 gene rearrangements** (confirmed by FISH) defines "double-hit lymphoma," now formally classified as **High-grade B-cell lymphoma with MYC and BCL2 rearrangements** under WHO 5th Edition. This is a distinct entity from DLBCL. Option A (double-expressor) is the most common distractor — double-expressor refers to IHC overexpression of both MYC and BCL2 proteins WITHOUT gene rearrangements. Double-expressor DLBCL has worse prognosis than typical DLBCL but is NOT the same entity as double-hit lymphoma. Burkitt lymphoma has MYC rearrangement but typically lacks BCL2 rearrangement and has different morphology and IHC (CD10+, BCL6+, BCL2−, Ki-67 ~100%). Plasmablastic lymphoma is a plasma cell neoplasm.

</details>

---

**Q3.** A 70-year-old male with known colorectal carcinoma of the sigmoid colon is being evaluated for palliative systemic therapy. Tumour molecular testing returns: KRAS codon 12 mutation (G12D), BRAF wildtype, MSI-high/dMMR. **Which of the following treatment options is most appropriate based on this molecular profile?**

- A) Cetuximab monotherapy
- B) Vemurafenib
- C) Pembrolizumab
- D) Bevacizumab + FOLFOX

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Pembrolizumab**

This question tests two molecular results simultaneously. First, **KRAS G12D mutation** categorically excludes anti-EGFR therapy — cetuximab (option A) is contraindicated and ineffective in KRAS-mutant colorectal carcinoma. Second, **MSI-high/dMMR** status predicts response to immune checkpoint inhibition. Pembrolizumab (PD-1 inhibitor) is approved as first-line monotherapy for unresectable or metastatic MSI-high CRC (KEYNOTE-177 data). Vemurafenib (option B) is a BRAF V600E inhibitor — the BRAF status here is wildtype, so no BRAF inhibitor applies. Bevacizumab + FOLFOX (option D) is a standard chemotherapy option but is not the most targeted answer given the MSI-high status and the availability of a checkpoint inhibitor. The question rewards candidates who process both molecular results before selecting an answer, not just the one that is most prominently mentioned.

</details>

## FAQs

**Which molecular markers are most high-yield for NEET-SS 2026 oncopathology preparation?**

The highest-yield molecular markers for NEET-SS 2026 oncopathology are organised into five clinical clusters: lung cancer drivers (EGFR, KRAS, ALK, ROS1, BRAF, MET, RET, NTRK), colorectal carcinoma predictors (KRAS/NRAS, BRAF V600E, MSI/dMMR, PIK3CA), breast cancer biomarkers (HER2 IHC/FISH including HER2-low, PIK3CA, ER/PR), CNS tumour classifiers (IDH1/IDH2, MGMT, TERT promoter, 1p/19q codeletion), and lymphoma rearrangements (MYC, BCL2, BCL6 for double/triple-hit classification). Mastering the marker-tumour-drug triad for each of these clusters addresses the majority of molecular MCQs in the current NEET-SS exam pattern.

**What is the difference between a HER2 double-expressor and a HER2-amplified tumour?**

HER2 IHC measures protein overexpression on the cell membrane — a result of gene amplification or, occasionally, increased transcription without amplification. IHC 3+ (strong complete membrane staining in >10% of invasive tumour cells) is considered positive and does not require FISH confirmation. IHC 2+ (moderate complete membrane staining) is equivocal and requires reflex FISH/ISH testing. IHC 1+ is negative. A "double-expressor" in B-cell lymphoma context refers to IHC overexpression of BOTH MYC and BCL2 proteins (>40% and >50% respectively) — a different use of the term than in breast pathology. In lymphoma, double-expressor is not the same as genetic double-hit, because the protein overexpression can occur without gene rearrangements.

**How is MGMT methylation tested, and why is IHC for MGMT not a reliable substitute?**

MGMT promoter methylation is tested by methylation-specific PCR (MSP), pyrosequencing, or NGS-based methylation analysis. IHC detects MGMT protein expression in the nucleus — but there is only moderate correlation between protein loss on IHC and promoter methylation status. The methylation status that predicts temozolomide response is at the promoter (epigenetic silencing of transcription), not just protein level. False negatives and false positives are common with IHC alone. For clinical decision-making and exam purposes, methylation-specific PCR or pyrosequencing is the gold-standard method; IHC is not a validated surrogate for therapeutic prediction.

**What does it mean that IDH-wildtype glioblastoma can be diagnosed in a grade 2 histology tumour under WHO CNS 5th Edition?**

Under WHO CNS 5th Edition (2021), the classification of adult diffuse gliomas is hierarchically molecular-first, then histological. A diffuse astrocytic glioma that is IDH-wildtype AND harbours any ONE of three grade-defining molecular features — EGFR amplification, whole chromosome +7/−10 pattern, or TERT promoter mutation — is classified as **Glioblastoma, IDH-wildtype, WHO Grade 4**, regardless of whether the histology looks like a Grade 2 tumour. This replaced the old "secondary GBM" concept and eliminated the category of IDH-wildtype diffuse astrocytoma Grade 2 as a clinically meaningful entity. The practical implication: a biopsy with low-grade histology but IDH-wildtype molecular profile must be fully characterised before a low-grade classification is assigned.

**How do KRAS mutations affect anti-EGFR therapy eligibility in colorectal carcinoma, and which codons are tested?**

KRAS and NRAS mutations downstream of EGFR in the RAS-MAPK pathway render anti-EGFR monoclonal antibodies (cetuximab, panitumumab) ineffective — the pathway is constitutively activated regardless of EGFR blockade. Extended RAS testing (the current standard for CRC) includes KRAS codons 12, 13, 59, 61, 117, and 146, plus NRAS codons 12, 13, 59, 61, 117, and 146. Any mutation at these codons in either KRAS or NRAS excludes anti-EGFR therapy. BRAF V600E is additionally tested as it independently predicts poor prognosis and a distinct treatment approach. Only patients who are RAS-wildtype AND BRAF V600E-wildtype are eligible for anti-EGFR therapy in the first-line metastatic setting (in combination with chemotherapy).
