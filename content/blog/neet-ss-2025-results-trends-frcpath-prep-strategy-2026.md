## The NEET-SS 2025 Results: What the Numbers Tell Us

NEET-SS is conducted by NBEMS for super-speciality admission in centrally funded institutions. For Pathology, the relevant super-specialities are:

- **DM Oncopathology** (highly competitive, limited seats)
- **MD/DM Histopathology** (where available)
- **FNB Pathology** variants at some centres

### Cutoff Patterns: 2023–2025 Trajectory

| Category | NEET-SS Pathology Cutoff (approx. percentile / score range) |
|---|---|
| General (UR) | Progressively rising; 2025 cutoffs higher than 2024 in competitive DM branches |
| OBC-NCL | Approximately 4–8% below UR cutoff |
| SC/ST | Approximately 10–15% below UR cutoff |
| DM Oncopathology (UR) | Among the highest in Pathology group — effectively requires FRCPath-level oncopathology command |

**Key trend:** The gap between a "passing" score and a competitive score in DM Oncopathology has widened. In 2023, a candidate scoring around the 75th–80th percentile had a realistic chance at a DM Oncopath seat. By 2025, the effective competitive threshold for top institutions has pushed toward the 85th–90th percentile range.

**What this means for 2026 prep:** You cannot afford to leave marks in oncopathology, haematopathology, or molecular diagnostics — areas where questions have become more knowledge-intensive and where FRCPath-level understanding now clearly separates candidates.

## The Question That Changed Everything

Here is a pattern that consistently appears in NEET-SS Pathology papers from 2024 and 2025:

> "A 58-year-old female presents with a 3 cm thyroid mass. The IHC panel shows: TTF-1 +, PAX8 +, Tg −, Calcitonin −. The Ki-67 is 40%. BRAF V600E mutation detected. What is the most likely diagnosis and which WHO 5th edition classification change is relevant here?"

This type of question — combining **IHC panel interpretation**, **molecular markers**, and **WHO 5th edition classification updates** — cannot be answered from a memorised list. It requires the integrated, explanatory understanding that FRCPath preparation builds.

If you are approaching NEET-SS 2026 prep purely through rote MCQ drilling, you will lose marks exactly here — in the integrated, applied questions that now define the top percentile.

## Mapping NEET-SS 2025 High-Yield Topics to FRCPath Preparation

### Systems That Proved Difficult in 2025

Based on the post-exam discussions and result distributions from NEET-SS 2025, three systems stood out as consistently differentiating:

#### 1. Haematopathology

NEET-SS 2025 included several questions on:

- **WHO 5th edition AML classification** — specifically the new entity "AML with mutated NPM1" and the removal of the 20% blast threshold requirement (a major change from WHO 4th edition)
- **MDS/MPN overlap entities** — particularly CMML and how to distinguish it from atypical CML
- **Lymphoma classification updates** — distinguishing DLBCL subtypes, the new category of "large B-cell lymphoma with IRF4 rearrangement," and the reclassification of follicular lymphoma grade 3B

**FRCPath alignment:** Haematopathology is examined in depth in both FRCPath Part 1 (classification, morphology, IHC profiles) and Part 2 (integrated cases). Preparing haematopathology to FRCPath standard directly covers this NEET-SS domain.

**Practical revision focus:**
- AML: NPM1, FLT3-ITD, CEBPA, and the blast threshold rule change
- MDS subtypes: SF3B1 mutation in MDS with ring sideroblasts
- Lymphoma: Know MYC, BCL2, BCL6 rearrangements and their clinical implications

#### 2. CNS Pathology

Questions in 2025 specifically targeted:

- **WHO 5th edition glioma classification** — the shift from morphology-based to molecular-based classification (IDH mutation status, 1p/19q codeletion, TERT promoter mutation, CDKN2A/B homozygous deletion)
- **RELA fusion–positive ependymoma** — a paediatric CNS entity that appeared in at least one confirmed question
- **H3 K27-altered diffuse midline glioma** — distinguishing it from IDH-wildtype GBM by molecular criteria

**FRCPath alignment:** CNS pathology is a core Part 2 OSPE topic and appears in Part 1 in the context of WHO classification updates. Our detailed breakdown of WHO 5th edition CNS changes is available in the [FRCPath Part 1 course module on CNS pathology](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-histopathology-course).

#### 3. Oncopathology and Molecular Diagnostics

The DM Oncopathology competitive cutoff is essentially telling you: **you need FRCPath-level command of oncopathology MCQs.** This is not hyperbole. The questions in this domain from 2025 covered:

- **PD-L1 testing methodology** — which assay, which antibody clone, which tumour type, and what score is clinically actionable
- **MMR/MSI testing** — IHC panel interpretation (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2), when to reflexly test, and what patterns indicate germline vs somatic mutation
- **Predictive vs prognostic biomarkers** — a conceptually demanding distinction that appears directly in both NEET-SS and FRCPath papers
- **HER2 testing updates** — the new "HER2-low" category in breast cancer and its treatment implications (trastuzumab deruxtecan)

## Turning Results Into Strategy: The Practical Framework

Here is how to convert the NEET-SS 2025 result analysis into a working preparation schedule for 2026.

### Step 1: Classify Your Topics by Quadrant

Use a simple 2×2 framework:

|  | **High NEET-SS yield** | **Lower NEET-SS yield** |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong for you** | Consolidate — don't over-invest | Maintain, don't neglect |
| **Weak for you** | **Priority — fix these first** | Deprioritise for now |

Based on 2025 patterns, the **high NEET-SS yield, commonly weak** quadrant contains:
- WHO 5th edition molecular classification updates (CNS, haematolymphoid, renal, thyroid)
- IHC panel interpretation with predictive context
- MMR/MSI and PD-L1 testing principles
- Cytogenetics and FISH interpretation basics

### Step 2: Anchor Every Topic to a "Why" — Not Just a "What"

The shift from 2024 to 2025 NEET-SS papers was a shift from fact recall to applied reasoning. The questions no longer just ask "What is the marker for X?" — they ask "Why does this marker pattern indicate Y, and what does the latest WHO classification say about management?"

**FRCPath preparation naturally builds this "why" layer.** An FRCPath candidate studying oncopathology doesn't just learn that p53 is positive in serous carcinoma — they learn the TP53 mutation mechanism, why it causes diffuse strong positivity vs the aberrant null pattern, and how to apply that to a case with borderline morphology. That depth translates directly to the highest-difficulty NEET-SS questions.

### Step 3: Use NEET-SS–Style MCQs to Test FRCPath-Level Knowledge

Do not prepare for NEET-SS and FRCPath as separate tracks. They are converging. The most efficient preparation is:

1. **Study the topic to FRCPath depth** — use WHO 5th edition, updated RCPATH guidelines, and FRCPath-level explanations
2. **Test with NEET-SS–style MCQs** — high-difficulty, vignette-based, image-integrated questions
3. **Review explanations that cross-reference both exam formats** — so you understand how the same concept is tested differently in NEET-SS vs FRCPath Part 1 vs Part 2

This is the model used in the [ElearningFRCPath MCQ bank](https://elearningfrcpath.com/pathology-mcq-2026), where questions are graded by difficulty and linked to WHO 5th edition references and FRCPath curriculum topics. If you are specifically targeting NEET-SS, the dedicated [NEET-SS Pathology preparation track](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology) maps every topic to the NBEMS syllabus and includes past-paper style vignettes.

If you knew that, you have already answered one of the questions that separated top NEET-SS 2025 performers. If you didn't, you now know what to revise first.

## Exam Pearls & Mnemonics

**The "MOLECULAR FIRST" Rule for WHO 5th Edition AML:**

> **M**utated NPM1 → no blast threshold needed
> **O**nly 10% blasts required for molecularly defined AML
> **L**ymphoid context: blast threshold still 20% for AML-MRC
> **E**xclude prior MDS/therapy before applying molecular criteria
> **C**ytogenetics: t(8;21), inv(16)/t(16;16), t(15;17) → AML regardless of blast %
> **U**se WHO 5th 2022 for all current exams — NOT ICC 2022
> **L**ynch vs sporadic MMR loss: BRAF V600E distinguishes them
> **A**IM: Abnormal IHC → interpret as pairs (MLH1+PMS2, MSH2+MSH6)
> **R** — Remember: 1p/19q codeletion always co-occurs with IDH mutation in oligodendroglioma

**Mnemonic — MMR protein partners:** **"MoM & SMS"**
- **Mo**M = **ML**H1 partners with **P**MS2 (MoM pair; MLH1 loss destabilises PMS2)
- **SM**S = **MS**H2 partners with **MS**H6 (SMS pair; MSH2 loss destabilises MSH6)

If MLH1 and PMS2 are both lost → think MLH1 methylation (sporadic) or MLH1 germline mutation (Lynch). The key discriminator: **BRAF V600E positive → sporadic (methylation); negative → consider Lynch**.

**Pearl — CNS glioma hierarchy (WHO 5th edition, for exam speed):**
1. IDH-mutant + 1p/19q codeletion = **Oligodendroglioma** (any grade)
2. IDH-mutant + no 1p/19q codeletion = **Astrocytoma** (grade 2/3/4)
3. IDH-wildtype + TERT promoter mutation / EGFR amp / +7/−10 = **GBM** (grade 4) — even with low-grade morphology
4. H3 K27M-altered + midline location = **Diffuse midline glioma, H3 K27-altered** (grade 4)

## Practice MCQs

**Q1.** NEET-SS Pathology 2025–style. A 45-year-old male has WBC 28,000/µL with 18% blasts in peripheral blood. Bone marrow biopsy shows 18% myeloblasts. Cytogenetics: normal 46,XY. Molecular testing: NPM1 mutation detected, FLT3-ITD negative. According to WHO 5th edition (2022) classification, the correct diagnosis is:

- A) Myelodysplastic syndrome with excess blasts-2 (MDS-EB2)
- B) Acute myeloid leukaemia with mutated NPM1
- C) AML with myelodysplasia-related changes
- D) Cannot diagnose without 20% blasts on repeat biopsy

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Acute myeloid leukaemia with mutated NPM1**

This is one of the most exam-tested WHO 5th edition changes. In the 2022 WHO Classification of Haematolymphoid Tumours (5th edition), **AML with mutated NPM1** is a defined entity that does **not** require the 20% blast threshold. Any blast percentage ≥ 10% with NPM1 mutation (in the absence of prior MDS or therapy-related context) qualifies for this diagnosis. The old WHO 4th edition required ≥ 20% blasts for AML — that threshold has been removed for molecularly defined entities. Options A and D apply pre-2022 logic. Option C requires specific morphological/genetic features of MDS-related changes not described here.

</details>

---

**Q2.** An IHC panel on a colorectal adenocarcinoma shows: MLH1 −, PMS2 −, MSH2 +, MSH6 +. The most likely cause of this pattern is:

- A) MSH2 gene germline mutation (Lynch syndrome)
- B) MLH1 promoter hypermethylation (sporadic MSI-H)
- C) MSH6 gene somatic mutation
- D) Concurrent loss of all four MMR proteins indicating widespread epigenetic silencing

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — MLH1 promoter hypermethylation (sporadic MSI-H)**

MLH1 and PMS2 work as a heterodimer — when MLH1 is lost (whether by mutation or epigenetic silencing), PMS2 is destabilised and also lost. This pattern (MLH1 loss + PMS2 loss, MSH2 intact, MSH6 intact) is the classic pattern of **sporadic MSI-H colorectal cancer**, driven by MLH1 promoter hypermethylation, which accounts for approximately 80% of all MSI-H colorectal cancers. It is associated with BRAF V600E mutation (found in ~50% of sporadic MLH1-methylated cases) — test for BRAF V600E to distinguish sporadic from Lynch syndrome when MLH1 is lost. Lynch syndrome due to MSH2 mutation (option A) would show MLH1+, PMS2+, MSH2−, MSH6− (or MSH6 intact if only MSH2 is mutated).

</details>

**Q3.** Which of the following statements about PD-L1 testing in non-small cell lung cancer is CORRECT?

- A) All anti-PD-L1 antibody clones are interchangeable across tumour types and platforms
- B) A TPS (Tumour Proportion Score) ≥ 50% predicts response to first-line pembrolizumab monotherapy
- C) PD-L1 expression is exclusively assessed on tumour cells, never on immune cells, in NSCLC
- D) High PD-L1 expression guarantees response to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — A TPS ≥ 50% predicts response to first-line pembrolizumab monotherapy**

For NSCLC (non-squamous and squamous), a TPS ≥ 50% using the **22C3 antibody clone (PharmDx assay)** is the companion diagnostic for first-line pembrolizumab monotherapy (KEYNOTE-024). TPS (Tumour Proportion Score) counts PD-L1 expression on tumour cells as a percentage. Option A is false: antibody clones are NOT interchangeable — 22C3 (Merck/pembrolizumab), 28-8 (BMS/nivolumab), SP142 (Roche/atezolizumab), and SP263 (Roche/durvalumab) have different scoring systems and clinical thresholds. Option C is false: CPS (Combined Positive Score) includes immune cells and is used for some tumour types (gastric, cervical, TNBC). Option D is false: PD-L1 positivity predicts enrichment of response but does not guarantee it — some PD-L1-high tumours do not respond, and some PD-L1-low tumours do.

</details>

## FAQs

**Q: NEET-SS 2025 results are out — is it too late to start serious preparation for NEET-SS 2026?**

No — and the 2025 result data is precisely what makes starting now valuable. NEET-SS 2026 is expected in Q3–Q4 2026, giving candidates who begin a structured FRCPath-aligned preparation now approximately 6–8 months of focused study time. That is sufficient to achieve a competitive score if preparation is topic-prioritised based on the 2025 high-yield analysis above. The candidates who struggle are those who start 6–8 weeks before the exam with only MCQ drilling and no explanatory depth.

---

**Q: Can someone preparing for FRCPath Part 1 simultaneously prepare for NEET-SS 2026?**

Yes — and this is arguably the most efficient dual-track preparation possible. FRCPath Part 1 demands deep understanding of histopathology, molecular pathology, IHC principles, and WHO classification — exactly the domains that determine the top percentile in NEET-SS. The effort invested in understanding a topic to FRCPath standard provides more than enough depth for NEET-SS. The difference is in question style: NEET-SS uses single-best-answer MCQs, while FRCPath Part 1 uses EMQs and SBAs with detailed stems. Practising both formats is the only additional step needed for dual-track candidates.

---

**Q: Are NEET-SS and INI-SS Pathology papers the same or different?**

They are separate examinations. **NEET-SS** is conducted by NBEMS for centrally funded institutions (AIIMS, PGI, JIPMER, etc. — except those with separate exams). **INI-SS** (Institute of National Importance Super Speciality) is conducted for AIIMS New Delhi, PGIMER Chandigarh, JIPMER, and NIMHANS. The topic domains overlap significantly, but the paper styles and difficulty calibration can differ. Candidates targeting AIIMS or PGI for DM Oncopathology or Histopathology should prepare for both but be aware that INI-SS papers have historically had a higher proportion of image-based and case-vignette questions.

---

**Q: Which systems should I prioritise for NEET-SS 2026 based on 2025 trends?**

Based on the 2025 result analysis, the highest-priority systems for 2026 are: (1) **Haematopathology** — WHO 5th edition AML/MDS/lymphoma classification updates; (2) **CNS pathology** — molecular glioma classification; (3) **Oncopathology/molecular diagnostics** — predictive IHC, MMR/MSI, PD-L1, HER2; (4) **Gynaecological pathology** — FIGO staging updates and HPV-associated vs HPV-independent carcinoma classification. Historically stable systems (GI carcinoma morphology, basic IHC) remain important but are less likely to generate the high-difficulty questions that separate top scores.
