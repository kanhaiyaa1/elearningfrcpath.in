## What Preparation Actually Worked — Three Patterns Across the Success Stories

Reading across all of the above feedback, three preparation patterns appear consistently in first-attempt and successful reattempt candidates:

### 1. Multiple revision passes through structured notes — not just reading

Aswin Jenisha specifically mentions revising the notes "at least three times." This is not incidental. First-pass reading builds familiarity; second-pass revision builds retention; third-pass rapid review builds the automatic recall speed that the exam format demands. Candidates who read extensively but do not build in deliberate repetition cycles consistently underperform their content knowledge on exam day.

The [FRCPath histopathology course](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-histopathology-course) structures content into revision-ready modules for exactly this reason — each session is designed to be revisited, not just attended.

### 2. Mock examination practice as a distinct preparation phase — not a final check

Multiple students mention attempting "all mock question papers" as a specific activity, separate from content revision. This framing matters: candidates who use mocks as a diagnostic tool — identifying weak areas before the exam rather than confirming knowledge after it — use their preparation time far more efficiently than those who do mocks only in the final week.

The [FRCPath MCQ bank](https://elearningfrcpath.com/pathology-mcq-2026) provides 800+ exam-pattern questions with detailed explanations designed to function as this diagnostic tool, not just a question-and-answer list.

### 3. Exam-aligned teaching — not general pathology knowledge

Nidhi's feedback — "almost every topic encountered in the paper had been covered during the sessions" — reflects something specific about exam-oriented teaching: it is selective in a way that textbooks are not. General pathology textbooks cover everything. The FRCPath Part 1 paper covers specific high-yield areas, with predictable emphasis on WHO 5th edition updates, IHC marker panels, molecular diagnostics, and RCPath-dataset-aligned reporting criteria. Teaching that is explicitly mapped to this emphasis prepares candidates differently than unsorted content coverage.

## Exam Pearls for FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology

**Pearl 1:** WHO 5th Edition updates are the single highest-yield content area for new questions in recent FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology papers. Any entity added, reclassified, or renamed in the WHO 5th Edition Blue Books is a live examination target.

**Pearl 2:** IHC marker panels — not just individual markers — are tested. Know the full positive and negative profile for common tumours (e.g., the difference between NUT carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and adenocarcinoma IHC panels; the seminoma vs. embryonal carcinoma panel; the hepatocellular carcinoma vs. cholangiocarcinoma panel).

**Pearl 3:** RCPath histopathology datasets define reporting standards — and what is in a dataset is, by definition, examinable. The dataset for colorectal carcinoma, the dataset for breast pathology, and the dataset for lung carcinoma are the highest-yield starting points.

**Pearl 4:** The performance letter is your most valuable preparation tool for a reattempt. Do not design your next study plan until you have mapped the weak areas in that letter to the official syllabus. General revision after a failed attempt produces marginal improvement; targeted revision of specifically weak areas produces significantly better results.

**Mnemonic — the THREE-R revision cycle for FRCPath:**
- **Read** — first-pass systematic coverage (weeks 1–8)
- **Recall** — active recall + MCQs + mock tests (weeks 9–14)
- **Rapid** — third-pass revision of high-yield tables, IHC panels, and molecular markers only (weeks 15 to exam)

## Practice MCQs — FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology Level

**Q1.** A 55-year-old male presents with haemoptysis. CT chest shows a 4 cm hilar mass. Biopsy shows sheets of large cells with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm, distinct cell borders, and intercellular bridges. IHC: p40+, CK5/6+, TTF-1−, napsin A−. PD-L1 TPS is 75%. **What is the most likely diagnosis?**

- A) Large cell neuroendocrine carcinoma
- B) Adenocarcinoma of the lung
- C) Squamous cell carcinoma of the lung
- D) NUT carcinoma

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Squamous cell carcinoma of the lung**

The morphology (intercellular bridges, eosinophilic cytoplasm) and IHC (p40+, CK5/6+, TTF-1−, napsin A−) are defining features of squamous cell carcinoma. p40 is the most specific squamous marker — more specific than CK5/6, which can be positive in other carcinomas. Large cell neuroendocrine carcinoma would be synaptophysin+/chromogranin+ and negative for squamous markers. NUT carcinoma would require NUT (NUTM1) IHC and is typically seen in younger patients. Adenocarcinoma is TTF-1+/napsin A+. The PD-L1 figure is a clinical distractor — it does not change the histological diagnosis, though it is relevant for treatment decision-making.

</details>

---

**Q2.** A 40-year-old woman presents with a 2.5 cm breast mass. Core biopsy shows an invasive carcinoma with tubular formation in <10% of the tumour, nuclear pleomorphism grade 3, and mitotic count of 22/10HPF. ER 95%+, PR 20%+, HER2 IHC 1+. FISH is performed for HER2 amplification. **What Nottingham Histological Grade will this tumour be assigned?**

- A) Grade 1 (score 3–5)
- B) Grade 2 (score 6–7)
- C) Grade 3 (score 8–9)
- D) Grade cannot be assigned without HER2 FISH result

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Grade 3 (score 8–9)**

Nottingham grading (Elston-Ellis modification of Scarff-Bloom-Richardson) assigns 1–3 points each for: tubule formation, nuclear pleomorphism, and mitotic count. Here: tubule formation <10% = 3 points; nuclear pleomorphism grade 3 = 3 points; mitotic count 22/10HPF — the threshold for 3 points is ≥11/10HPF in a standard high-power field (field diameter 0.44mm, area 0.152mm²), so 22/10HPF = 3 points. Total = 9 → Grade 3. HER2 FISH result is required for treatment planning and HER2-low classification, but it does not affect Nottingham histological grading. This question tests both grading criteria and the trap of conflating IHC for HER2 with histological grading.

</details>

---

**Q3.** A 68-year-old male presents with a raised PSA of 18 ng/mL. Prostate biopsy shows two cores with infiltrating malignant glands: the dominant pattern shows well-formed glands (pattern 3), and a secondary pattern shows poorly formed glands and single cells comprising approximately 20% of the tumour. **What is the Grade Group and Gleason score for this tumour?**

- A) Grade Group 1, Gleason score 3+3=6
- B) Grade Group 2, Gleason score 3+4=7
- C) Grade Group 3, Gleason score 4+3=7
- D) Grade Group 4, Gleason score 4+4=8

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Grade Group 2, Gleason score 3+4=7**

The primary (dominant) pattern is Gleason 3 (well-formed glands) and the secondary pattern is Gleason 4 (poorly formed glands/single cells at approximately 20% of tumour). The Gleason score is primary + secondary = 3+4=7. Grade Group 2 corresponds to Gleason score 3+4=7. If the primary pattern had been pattern 4 and the secondary pattern 3 (pattern 4 dominant), the score would be 4+3=7, Grade Group 3 — a worse prognosis despite the same numerical Gleason score. The distinction between 3+4 and 4+3 for Gleason 7 tumours is an FRCPath-level question testing whether candidates understand that the primary pattern drives prognosis more than the secondary, and that Grade Groups were introduced precisely to capture this distinction.

</details>

## FAQs

**When were the FRCPath Part 1 Spring 2026 results declared?**

The FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology and FRCPath Part 2 Histopathology Spring 2026 results were declared on Friday, May 22, 2026. Based on recent RCPath practice, Part 1 results are emailed directly to candidates rather than published on the website. Candidates should check the email address registered with their RCPath application, including spam and junk folders. Detailed performance letters are expected in the week following the result email. For full information on the result date, verification process, and what to do next, see the [FRCPath Part 1 Result 2026 page](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-result-2026).

**What is the FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology pass rate?**

The most recently published official data (Spring 2025 RCPath Performance Report) shows an FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology pass rate of **67.17%** in that sitting, with an overall Part 1 pass rate of 70.11% across all specialties. Spring 2026 pass rate data will not be available until RCPath publishes the corresponding performance report, typically several weeks after the result declaration. Approximately 1 in 3 candidates in a typical sitting do not pass, which makes structured, exam-aligned preparation essential rather than optional.

**Is Autumn 2026 really the last sitting in the current FRCPath format?**

Yes. RCPath has confirmed that the Autumn 2026 session will be the final sitting in the current FRCPath examination format. A new format commences from Spring 2027. Applications for Autumn 2026 open by 5pm on Friday, 29 May 2026 and close on Friday, 3 July 2026. Candidates who are planning their next attempt — whether a first attempt or a reattempt — should apply early and begin preparation without waiting for the application deadline.

**If I did not pass Spring 2026, do I need to reapply online for Autumn 2026?**

Based on recent RCPath guidance, candidates resitting after an unsuccessful attempt typically do not need to reapply online — they can notify the Examinations team in writing. However, you should verify the exact process on the RCPath applications page for your specialty, as procedures may vary. Regardless of the application method, the deadline of Friday, 3 July 2026 applies to all candidates. Begin preparation now, not after you have confirmed your application.

**What preparation worked for first-attempt passers in FRCPath Spring 2026?**

Across the Spring 2026 success stories, three consistent preparation patterns emerged: (1) multiple systematic revision passes through structured notes — not single-pass reading; (2) mock examination practice used as a diagnostic tool throughout preparation, not just a final rehearsal; and (3) exam-aligned teaching that specifically covers high-yield areas (WHO 5th Edition updates, IHC panels, RCPath datasets) rather than general pathology at textbook depth. Candidates who combined all three consistently described their preparation as "targeted" and "covered what actually appeared in the paper."
