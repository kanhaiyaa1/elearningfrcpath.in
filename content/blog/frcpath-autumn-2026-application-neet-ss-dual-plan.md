If you are an MD-Pathology graduate in India targeting FRCPath Part 1 alongside NEET-SS 2026, you are in a critical window right now. **FRCPath Autumn 2026 applications opened on 29 May 2026 and close on 3 July 2026** — and the Part 1 examination sits in September or October 2026, the same 6-month cycle as NEET-SS Pathology, which is tentatively scheduled for December 2026 or March 2027. This post gives you the exact dates you need, explains what the last-sitting-in-current-format status means for your decision, and lays out a month-by-month dual-exam roadmap from June 2026 through to either the December or March NEET-SS sitting.

## FRCPath Autumn 2026: The Complete Deadline Timeline

Before planning preparation, you need the dates locked. Here is every deadline that matters for the Autumn 2026 session, with conversions to IST (Indian Standard Time — UTC+5:30) for candidates applying from India. The UK is currently on BST (British Summer Time, UTC+1), so IST = BST + 4:30.

| Event | UK Date & Time | IST Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Applications open | Friday 29 May 2026, 5:00pm BST | Friday 29 May 2026, 9:30pm IST |
| Applications close | Friday 3 July 2026, midnight BST | Saturday 4 July 2026, 4:30am IST |
| Withdrawal deadline (full refund) | Friday 17 July 2026 | Same date, check RCPath for time |
| Reasonable adjustments requests | By Friday 17 July 2026 | Same |
| Entry confirmation from RCPath | By 5pm Friday 31 July 2026 | 31 July, 9:30pm IST |
| Examination period | 21 September – 30 October 2026 | — |
| Part 1 exams | First week of exam period | ~21–25 September 2026 |
| Results declared | Friday 20 November 2026 | 20 November, evening IST |

**For India-based candidates:** The practical application close deadline is **3 July 2026 midnight BST**, which translates to **4 July 2026, 4:30am IST**. Do not cut it to the last hour — online application portals can be slow around deadlines and timezone confusion is common.

### The Last-Sitting-in-Current-Format Factor

This is not a routine application deadline. **RCPath has confirmed that Autumn 2026 is the final sitting in the current FRCPath examination format.** From Spring 2027, a new format commences. What this means practically:

- If you miss the July 3 deadline, your next opportunity is Spring 2027 in an unfamiliar format — with no accumulated preparation advantage from studying to the current format.
- Candidates who are 1–2 attempts into the current format, or who have been building preparation around current-format MCQ banks and notes, are at a relative disadvantage if they defer to Spring 2027.
- The "I'll do the next sitting" logic does not apply here the way it normally would. **This is the last current-format sitting.**

For full information on result timelines, pass rates, and reattempt eligibility, the [FRCPath Part 1 Result 2026 page](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-result-2026) has the complete guide.

## How to Apply from India — Practical Steps

**Step 1:** Go to the RCPath Examinations page for your specialty and locate the application form link, which is made live when the window opens. The application is done online through the RCPath candidate portal.

**Step 2:** Have your GMC/RCPath registration number ready (if applicable), your current employer details, and the examination fee. Payment is made by card at the time of application. The fee schedule is published on the RCPath website — verify the current amount before applying.

**Step 3:** International candidates sitting the examination at an international centre need to select their preferred centre at application. Available international centres vary by sitting — confirm on the RCPath examinations page which centres are available for Autumn 2026 and in which country they are located.

**Step 4:** If you require reasonable adjustments (extra time, rest breaks, etc.), your request must be submitted by **17 July 2026** — the same as the withdrawal deadline.

## NEET-SS 2026 Pathology: Dates and Pattern

NEET-SS 2026 Pathology is tentatively scheduled for **December 11–12, 2026** per the NBEMS tentative calendar. Some updated schedules indicate the possibility of a **fourth-week-of-March 2027** sitting. As of the current date, candidates should plan for the December window as the primary target and treat March 2027 as a contingency.

The current NEET-SS 2026 Pathology examination pattern:

- **3 time-bound sections**, 50 questions each, 50 minutes per section
- **+4 marks** for correct answers; **−1 mark** for wrong answers; **0** for unattempted
- Total: 150 questions, 150 minutes across 3 sections
- The 3-section format means each section is independent — time overrun in one section cannot be recovered in another
- Image-based MCQs (digital pathology images) form a significant share of questions, particularly in oncopathology and histopathology sections

The NEET-SS examination pattern is covered in depth in the [NEET-SS pathology preparation course at eLearning FRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology), which includes 50-minute sectional mocks matching the exam format exactly.

## The Dual-Exam Reality: What You Are Actually Managing

Most India-based aspirants in this window face one of two timelines:

**Path A — Tight gap:** FRCPath Part 1 in September/October 2026 → NEET-SS December 2026
This is a **2–3 month gap** between the two exams. The advantage: momentum. If you are well-prepared and in exam-mode in September, you can pivot to NEET-SS-specific sectional mocking in October and November without losing the knowledge base. The risk: if FRCPath Part 1 preparation has left gaps in oncopathology or haematopathology, there is limited time to address them before NEET-SS.

**Path B — Comfortable gap:** FRCPath Part 1 in September/October 2026 → NEET-SS March 2027
This is a **4–5 month gap**. The advantage: there is time for a proper NEET-SS pivot, including rebuilding weak areas, adding image-based MCQ practice, and running the full stopping-camera and sectional-pacing drills the 2026 pattern demands. The risk: FRCPath knowledge can fade over 4–5 months if not actively maintained, and some aspirants lose study momentum after a major exam.

**The overlap advantage:** The content overlap between FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology and NEET-SS Pathology (Oncopathology / Haematopathology track) is substantial — WHO 5th Edition updates, IHC marker panels, molecular markers, histological classification of tumours. Preparation done for FRCPath Part 1 directly supports NEET-SS preparation. The primary differences are in **exam format** (FRCPath uses EMQs/SBAs; NEET-SS uses the 3-section 50-minute format with different pacing demands) and **emphasis** (FRCPath tests UK clinical practice alignment; NEET-SS tests applied oncopathology in an Indian DM-entry context).

## Month-by-Month Dual-Exam Roadmap: June 2026 → November 2026

This plan assumes you are applying for FRCPath Autumn 2026 immediately and targeting NEET-SS December 2026. If NEET-SS is March 2027, extend the post-FRCPath phase with 3 additional months of dedicated NEET-SS preparation.

### June 2026: Foundation and Registration

**FRCPath focus:**
- Apply for Autumn 2026 before 3 July — do this in the first week of June, not the last week.
- Begin systematic FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology content coverage: system-by-system, WHO 5th Edition-aligned. Target 2 systems per week.
- Start an MCQ bank (800+ questions minimum); do 20 questions per day with full explanation review.
- IHC marker panel flash-cards: build the triad set (marker → tumour → drug/diagnosis) from the first week.

**NEET-SS focus:**
- Run one 50-minute sectional mock per week — not for content assessment at this stage, but to build the sectional-rhythm habit. Use the stopping-camera rules from day one.
- Begin an error-notebook: classify every wrong answer by type (content gap, stem misread, question-type mismatch, pacing error).

### July 2026: Building Momentum

**FRCPath focus:**
- Increase MCQ volume to 30–40 per day.
- Add image-spotter sessions: 3 times per week, 20-minute blocks of H&E image recognition (carcinoma subtypes, sarcomas, lymphomas, haematopathology).
- Haematopathology deep dive: acute leukaemia classification (WHO 2022), lymphoma classification (WHO 5th Edition), myeloproliferative neoplasms.

**NEET-SS focus:**
- Increase to two 50-minute sectional mocks per week. Now analyse error notebooks for content patterns — use these to direct FRCPath content coverage simultaneously.
- Add PYQ (previous year question) analysis sessions: 30 minutes, 3 times per week, to calibrate NEET-SS question difficulty and style.

**Application deadline:** 3 July 2026. If you have not applied yet, this is your final day. The withdrawal deadline (17 July) allows for a refund if your circumstances change after applying.

### August 2026: Peak FRCPath Preparation

**FRCPath focus:**
- Full-length timed practice tests (125 questions, 180 minutes) at least twice per week.
- Oncopathology consolidation: breast, lung, colorectal, prostate, lymphoma — each with morphology, IHC panel, molecular marker, and RCPath dataset awareness.
- Rapid revision of IHC flash-cards and molecular triads: one full pass per week.
- Weak-area targeted sprint: use error patterns from mock tests to identify the 3–4 highest-miss topics and dedicate 30 minutes daily to these specifically.

**NEET-SS focus:**
- Two to three 50-minute sectional mocks per week. Shift to image-heavy mock blocks to build digital pathology image recognition speed.
- NEET-SS-specific pattern drills: stopping-camera, 90-second image-MCQ threshold, question-type tagging.

### September 2026: FRCPath Exam Month

**FRCPath focus:**
- Final 2-week revision: rapid, not new. No new topics after 10 September. Only revision of existing notes, IHC flash-cards, and molecular triads.
- Final-week routine: light daily practice (20 MCQs), sleep rhythm reset, exam logistics confirmation (venue, travel, documents).
- **FRCPath Part 1 examination: approximately 21–25 September 2026.**

**NEET-SS focus:** Minimal during FRCPath exam week. One sectional mock mid-month to maintain rhythm; resume full NEET-SS preparation from the day after FRCPath exam.

### October 2026: NEET-SS Pivot

- FRCPath preparation is complete. Pivot fully to NEET-SS.
- Increase NEET-SS sectional mocks to 3–4 per week.
- Add oncopathology and haematopathology image-drill blocks (NEET-SS has a higher image-MCQ share than FRCPath).
- Full error-notebook review: categorise accumulated errors from June–August. Address the top 5 content-gap areas first.
- Sectional-pacing refinement: calibrate your personal stopping-camera thresholds based on October mock data.

### November 2026 (if NEET-SS December 2026)

- Final NEET-SS sprint: PYQ revision, high-yield topic consolidation, image-pattern drilling.
- Final 2 weeks: only revision — rapid-pass through notes, IHC panels, molecular triads. No new topics.
- Mock rhythm: 2 full 3-section mocks per week in the first 3 weeks; taper to 1 per week in the final week.
- Final week: light practice, rest, logistics. Review stopping-camera rules and pacing thresholds one last time.

If NEET-SS is **March 2027**, use December 2026–February 2027 for a deeper content rebuild and extended mock series, following the same October–November pattern across 5 months instead of 2.

## Exam Pearls for Dual-Exam Candidates

**Pearl 1:** The 70% content overlap between FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology and NEET-SS Oncopathology is your strategic asset. Never prepare one exam in isolation — every WHO 5th Edition topic, every IHC marker panel, every molecular triad you master for FRCPath is simultaneously building NEET-SS readiness. Structure your notes so they serve both.

**Pearl 2:** The format difference is the primary adjustment required, not the content difference. FRCPath does not impose a per-section time firewall; NEET-SS does. Practise NEET-SS's 50-minute per-section format separately and explicitly — it is a different cognitive skill from FRCPath-style sequential answering.

**Pearl 3:** Apply for FRCPath Autumn 2026 immediately — do not wait until late June or early July. Portal slowdowns around deadlines are common, and timezone confusion (BST vs IST) has caught out candidates in previous cycles.

**Pearl 4:** If you do not pass FRCPath Part 1 in Autumn 2026, the Spring 2027 sitting will be in a new format. Begin familiarising yourself with the new format details as soon as RCPath publishes them — do not wait for a failed result to start planning the next attempt.

**Mnemonic — CMAP for dual-exam candidate priorities:**
- **C** — Calendar: lock in FRCPath application (by 3 July); lock in NEET-SS date (Dec or March)
- **M** — Merge content preparation (one set of notes serving both exams)
- **A** — Adapt format practice separately (FRCPath mock format ≠ NEET-SS 50-min section format)
- **P** — Pivot deliberately (after FRCPath exam, clean pivot to NEET-SS pacing drills within 48 hours)

## Practice MCQs — High-Yield Overlap Topics (FRCPath and NEET-SS Relevant)

**Q1.** A 58-year-old male undergoes colectomy for a sigmoid adenocarcinoma. Histology confirms invasive poorly differentiated adenocarcinoma, T3N1M0. MMR IHC panel shows: MLH1 absent, PMS2 absent, MSH2 intact, MSH6 intact. **What is the most likely underlying mechanism for this MMR-deficient tumour, and what is the clinical implication?**

- A) Germline MSH2 mutation — Lynch syndrome; screen first-degree relatives
- B) Somatic MLH1 promoter hypermethylation — sporadic MSI-H; BRAF V600E testing recommended to confirm sporadic origin
- C) Germline MLH1 mutation — Lynch syndrome; screen first-degree relatives
- D) Somatic PMS2 mutation — sporadic MSI-H; no further germline testing required

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Somatic MLH1 promoter hypermethylation; BRAF V600E testing recommended**

The IHC pattern of **MLH1 absent + PMS2 absent, MSH2 intact + MSH6 intact** reflects selective MLH1 loss. MLH1 forms a heterodimer with PMS2 — when MLH1 is lost, PMS2 is also lost (PMS2 is unstable without its partner). The most common cause of this pattern in colorectal carcinoma is **somatic MLH1 promoter hypermethylation** (sporadic MSI-H CRC), accounting for ~15% of all CRC. Lynch syndrome (germline MLH1 mutation) also produces this pattern, making the distinction critical. BRAF V600E mutation and CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP) are strongly associated with sporadic MLH1 methylation — **BRAF V600E testing** is the recommended next step to help distinguish sporadic from Lynch syndrome: a BRAF V600E mutation in the tumour essentially excludes Lynch syndrome (germline MLH1 mutations are not associated with BRAF V600E). Option C would also show MLH1/PMS2 absence but the mechanism is germline, not methylation — distinguished by BRAF testing and germline testing. Option A (germline MSH2) would show MSH2 absent + MSH6 absent, not the pattern described.

</details>

**Q2.** A 32-year-old woman presents with a 4.5 cm mass in the right ovary. Frozen section during laparoscopy shows a surface epithelial tumour with papillary architecture, serous-type ciliated cells, mild nuclear atypia, and no stromal invasion. Mitoses are rare. The surgeon asks: **"What is the most likely diagnosis and how should I plan this surgery?"**

- A) High-grade serous carcinoma — perform staging laparotomy immediately
- B) Serous borderline tumour (atypical proliferative serous tumour) — conservative surgery appropriate if fertility desired
- C) Low-grade serous carcinoma — complete surgical staging required
- D) Serous cystadenoma — ovarian cystectomy is sufficient; no further staging

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Serous borderline tumour; conservative surgery appropriate if fertility desired**

Serous borderline tumour (SBT), also called atypical proliferative serous tumour (APST) in WHO classification, is defined by papillary epithelial proliferation with stratification and tufting, cellular atypia, increased mitoses, but **absence of destructive stromal invasion**. This absence of invasion distinguishes it from both low-grade and high-grade serous carcinoma. In young women desiring fertility, unilateral salpingo-oophorectomy with peritoneal staging is acceptable — contralateral ovarian cystectomy rather than oophorectomy can preserve fertility. SBT has an excellent prognosis when confined to the ovary (5-year survival >95%). High-grade serous carcinoma (option A) has high-grade nuclear atypia, numerous mitoses, and destructive invasion; it is WT1+/p53 abnormal/p16 block-positive and requires immediate staging. Low-grade serous carcinoma (option C) shows invasion but is distinct from SBT and HGSC — it is WT1+/p53 wild-type/KRAS/BRAF mutated; staging is required. Serous cystadenoma (option D) lacks the papillary proliferation and stratification; simple cystectomy suffices.

</details>

**Q3.** A FRCPath-style EMQ scenario: A 70-year-old male presents with fatigue, weight loss, and splenomegaly. Full blood count: WBC 120 × 10⁹/L (predominantly neutrophils and myelocytes), Hb 9.8 g/dL, platelets 680 × 10⁹/L. BCR-ABL1 by RT-PCR is positive (p210 isoform). He is started on imatinib. Six months later, BCR-ABL1 transcript levels have risen from 0.1% to 3.2% (IS). Kinase domain mutation testing shows T315I mutation. **Which of the following is the most appropriate next targeted therapy?**

- A) Dasatinib (second-generation TKI)
- B) Nilotinib (second-generation TKI)
- C) Ponatinib (third-generation TKI)
- D) Bosutinib (second-generation TKI)

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — Ponatinib (third-generation TKI)**

The **T315I ("gatekeeper") mutation** in BCR-ABL1 is the most important kinase domain resistance mutation in CML. It is called the gatekeeper mutation because the threonine-to-isoleucine substitution at position 315 eliminates the critical hydrogen bond used by all first- and second-generation TKIs (imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib) to bind the ATP-binding pocket — none of them have clinical activity against T315I. **Ponatinib** (AP24534) is the only approved third-generation TKI with activity against T315I-mutant BCR-ABL1. It uses a carbon-carbon triple bond that bypasses the steric block created by the isoleucine substitution. Options A (dasatinib), B (nilotinib), and D (bosutinib) are all second-generation TKIs; all are inactive against T315I. This question is tested in both FRCPath (haematopathology molecular) and NEET-SS (haematopathology oncopath) in the form of "resistance mutation → appropriate TKI switch" clinical reasoning.

</details>

## FAQs

**When exactly do FRCPath Autumn 2026 applications close for India-based candidates?**

The application window closes at midnight BST on Friday, 3 July 2026 — which is **4:30am IST on Saturday, 4 July 2026**. In practice, candidates should aim to complete and submit their application by **2 July 2026 IST** to avoid last-minute technical issues, portal congestion, and timezone calculation errors. The withdrawal deadline (for a full refund if you need to pull out after applying) is 17 July 2026. Applications should be submitted through the RCPath online candidate portal — check the RCPath Examinations by Specialty page for the direct application link.

**Is Autumn 2026 really the last sitting in the current FRCPath format?**

Yes. RCPath has officially confirmed that Autumn 2026 is the final sitting in the current FRCPath examination format, with a new format beginning from Spring 2027. Candidates who are mid-way through their attempt sequence, who have been preparing with current-format study materials, or who have one attempt remaining under the current regulations are at a significant disadvantage if they defer to Spring 2027. The format change is also likely to require an adjustment period — preparation materials, question banks, and teaching resources for the new format will take time to develop. Sitting Autumn 2026 is strategically sound for any candidate who is ready or near-ready.

**How much content overlaps between FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology and NEET-SS 2026 Pathology?**

Approximately 70% of the core content overlaps — specifically WHO 5th Edition tumour classification, IHC marker panels, molecular markers and their diagnostic/therapeutic implications, histological pattern recognition, and haematopathology. The primary differences are in emphasis and clinical alignment: FRCPath expects UK-practice alignment (NICE guidelines, RCPath datasets, UK reporting standards); NEET-SS focuses on applied oncopathology in the DM-entry context, with a higher proportion of image-based digital pathology MCQs. Candidates can legitimately prepare from a single set of notes — WHO 5th Edition-aligned, IHC-panel-organised notes serve both exams when supplemented by format-specific mock practice.

**How should a dual-exam candidate split their daily study time?**

A practical split for the June–August peak preparation phase is: 60–70% FRCPath-focused content and MCQs, 30–40% NEET-SS format practice (sectional mocks and error analysis). The content studied for FRCPath is simultaneously building NEET-SS knowledge — the split is in format practice, not in content coverage. After the FRCPath exam (late September), shift entirely to NEET-SS format: 3–4 sectional mocks per week, image-drill blocks, and NEET-SS-specific pacing and stopping-camera drills. The [FRCPath exam guide at eLearning FRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-exam-guide-2026) and [NEET-SS pathology course](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology) are designed to be used together for exactly this dual-exam preparation path.

**What should a dual-exam candidate prioritise if they only have 4 hours per day to study?**

With 4 hours per day in the June–August phase, a practical daily split is: 2 hours on FRCPath content (system-by-system notes + MCQs), 1 hour on IHC and molecular marker flash-card revision (this serves both exams simultaneously), and 1 hour on NEET-SS format — one 50-minute sectional mock followed by 10 minutes of error-sheet analysis. This maintains dual-exam readiness without overloading either preparation track. After FRCPath Part 1 (September), shift all 4 hours to NEET-SS preparation: 2 hours content revision, 1 hour image-MCQ drilling, 1 hour sectional mock or PYQ analysis.
