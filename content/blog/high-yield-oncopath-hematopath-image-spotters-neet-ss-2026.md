Every NEET-SS pathology aspirant faces the same dilemma: the syllabus is vast, time is finite, and not all topics carry equal weight. After analysing previous year questions, expert breakdowns, and recent exam patterns, three domains consistently dominate the high-yield zone—**oncopathology, hematopathology, and image-based "spotter" questions**. If you're preparing for NEET-SS 2026 pathology (or FRCPath Part 1), understanding which topics to prioritise can transform scattered revision into focused, strategic preparation.

This isn't about shortcuts. It's about recognising that certain pathology domains—particularly cancer classification, blood disorders, and visual diagnosis—appear repeatedly in NEET-SS papers, often accounting for 40–50% of the pathology section. Let's break down exactly which topics deserve your prime study hours and why.

---

## Why Oncopathology & Hematopath Dominate NEET-SS Pathology

NEET-SS pathology questions aren't evenly distributed. Analysis of the last three years shows:

- **Oncopathology (solid tumours + molecular markers):** 25–30% of pathology questions
- **Hematopathology (leukemias, lymphomas, coagulation):** 15–20% of pathology questions
- **Image-based spotters (histology, gross, IHC):** 20–25% of pathology questions

Combined, these three domains represent nearly **60–75% of the pathology section**. The reason? They're clinically relevant, visually testable, and align with postgraduate competencies. NEET-SS examiners favour topics where morphology meets molecular updates—exactly where oncopath and hematopath intersect.

## High-Yield Oncopathology Topics for NEET-SS 2026

### 1. Breast Pathology: HER2-Low, Molecular Subtypes, Grading

Breast cancer questions appear in almost every NEET-SS paper. Focus areas:

- **HER2-low breast cancer** (IHC 1+ or IHC 2+/ISH-negative)—new therapeutic category, frequently tested
- **Nottingham grading** (tubule formation, nuclear pleomorphism, mitotic count)
- **Molecular subtypes** (Luminal A/B, HER2-enriched, triple-negative)
- **Lobular vs ductal carcinoma** morphology and IHC (E-cadherin loss in lobular)

**Exam Pearl:** HER2-low is not HER2-negative. It's a distinct category with emerging targeted therapy (trastuzumab deruxtecan). Expect case-based questions showing IHC 1+ staining.

### 2. Lung Carcinoma: Adenocarcinoma Patterns & Driver Mutations

Lung pathology is a perennial favourite:

- **Adenocarcinoma growth patterns** (lepidic, acinar, papillary, micropapillary, solid)
- **Driver mutations** (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, KRAS, BRAF)—know which are mutually exclusive
- **Small cell vs non-small cell** distinction (TTF-1, synaptophysin, chromogranin)
- **PD-L1 scoring** (tumour proportion score for immunotherapy eligibility)

**Mnemonic for adenocarcinoma patterns:** "LAMPS" — Lepidic, Acinar, Micropapillary, Papillary, Solid.

### 3. Colorectal Carcinoma: MMR Status, Grading, Polyps

Colorectal pathology integrates morphology and molecular markers:

- **Mismatch repair deficiency** (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 loss)—Lynch syndrome vs sporadic
- **Adenoma-carcinoma sequence** (tubular, tubulovillous, villous adenomas)
- **Tumour budding** and poorly differentiated clusters (prognostic markers)
- **Serrated polyps** (sessile serrated lesion vs traditional serrated adenoma)

**High-Yield Table: MMR Status Interpretation**

| MLH1 | PMS2 | MSH2 | MSH6 | Interpretation |
|------|------|------|------|----------------|
| Lost | Lost | Intact | Intact | MLH1/PMS2 deficiency (check BRAF/MLH1 promoter methylation) |
| Intact | Intact | Lost | Lost | MSH2/MSH6 deficiency (Lynch syndrome likely) |
| Intact | Intact | Intact | Lost | Isolated MSH6 deficiency (Lynch syndrome possible) |
| Intact | Lost | Intact | Intact | Isolated PMS2 deficiency (rare, Lynch syndrome possible) |

### 4. Renal Cell Carcinoma: WHO 5th Edition Updates

The WHO 5th edition introduced new RCC entities—prime exam material:

- **Clear cell RCC** (VHL mutation, carbonic anhydrase IX positive)
- **Papillary RCC** (Type 1 vs Type 2)
- **Chromophobe RCC** (Hale's colloidal iron positive)
- **New entities:** ALK-rearranged RCC, TFEB-rearranged RCC, fumarate hydratase-deficient RCC

**Exam Pearl:** TFEB-rearranged RCC shows strong nuclear TFEB immunostaining and occurs in younger patients. It's a translocation-associated carcinoma.

### 5. Soft Tissue Tumours: Molecular Fusions

Sarcoma questions often hinge on molecular markers:

- **Ewing sarcoma** (EWSR1-FLI1 fusion, CD99 positive)
- **Synovial sarcoma** (SS18-SSX fusion, TLE1 positive)
- **Alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma** (PAX3/7-FOXO1 fusion)
- **Dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans** (COL1A1-PDGFB fusion, CD34 positive)

## High-Yield Hematopathology Topics for NEET-SS 2026

### 1. Acute Leukemias: WHO 5th Edition Classification

Acute leukemia classification has shifted toward molecular markers:

- **AML with NPM1 mutation** (no longer requires 20% blasts in WHO 5th edition)
- **AML with RUNX1-RUNX1T1** (t(8;21), Auer rods, myeloperoxidase positive)
- **APL (acute promyelocytic leukemia)** (PML-RARA fusion, t(15;17), ATRA therapy)
- **ALL immunophenotyping** (B-ALL: CD19, CD10, TdT; T-ALL: CD3, CD7, TdT)

**Exam Pearl:** NPM1-mutated AML can be diagnosed even with <20% blasts if NPM1 mutation is confirmed. This is a major WHO 5th edition change.

### 2. Lymphomas: Hodgkin vs Non-Hodgkin, Key Entities

Lymphoma questions test morphology and immunophenotype:

- **Classical Hodgkin lymphoma** (Reed-Sternberg cells, CD15+/CD30+, CD20−)
- **Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma** (CD20+, CD10 variable, BCL6+)
- **Follicular lymphoma** (t(14;18), BCL2+, CD10+, BCL6+)
- **Burkitt lymphoma** (MYC translocation, "starry sky" pattern, Ki-67 ~100%)
- **Mantle cell lymphoma** (t(11;14), cyclin D1+, CD5+/CD20+)

**Mnemonic for Reed-Sternberg cell markers:** "15-30 Club" — CD15+, CD30+.

### 3. Myeloproliferative Neoplasms: JAK2, CALR, MPL

MPNs are molecular-driven diagnoses:

- **Polycythemia vera** (JAK2 V617F in >95%, raised Hb/Hct)
- **Essential thrombocythemia** (JAK2, CALR, or MPL mutations, platelets >450,000)
- **Primary myelofibrosis** (JAK2, CALR, or MPL mutations, bone marrow fibrosis)
- **CML** (BCR-ABL1 fusion, t(9;22) Philadelphia chromosome)

**High-Yield Table: MPN Molecular Markers**

| Disease | JAK2 V617F | CALR | MPL | BCR-ABL1 |
|---------|------------|------|-----|----------|
| Polycythemia vera | 95% | Rare | Rare | Negative |
| Essential thrombocythemia | 50–60% | 20–30% | 5% | Negative |
| Primary myelofibrosis | 50–60% | 20–30% | 5% | Negative |
| CML | Negative | Negative | Negative | Positive |

### 4. Coagulation Disorders: Hemophilia, VWD, DIC

Coagulation questions integrate lab values and pathophysiology:

- **Hemophilia A** (Factor VIII deficiency, prolonged aPTT, normal PT)
- **Hemophilia B** (Factor IX deficiency, prolonged aPTT, normal PT)
- **Von Willebrand disease** (prolonged bleeding time, low VWF, variable aPTT)
- **DIC** (low platelets, low fibrinogen, raised D-dimer, schistocytes)

## Image-Based "Spotter" Questions: What to Expect

Image-based MCQs are the fastest-growing question type in NEET-SS pathology. They test visual pattern recognition across:

### 1. Histopathology Spotters

- **Signet-ring cells** (gastric adenocarcinoma, lobular breast carcinoma)
- **Psammoma bodies** (papillary thyroid carcinoma, serous ovarian carcinoma, meningioma)
- **Orphan Annie nuclei** (papillary thyroid carcinoma)
- **Starry sky pattern** (Burkitt lymphoma)
- **Indian filing** (lobular breast carcinoma)

### 2. Gross Pathology Spotters

- **Krukenberg tumour** (bilateral ovarian metastases, signet-ring cells)
- **Chocolate cyst** (endometrioma)
- **Nutmeg liver** (chronic passive congestion)
- **Currant jelly sputum** (Klebsiella pneumonia)

### 3. IHC Spotters

- **Membranous HER2 staining** (breast/gastric carcinoma)
- **Nuclear ER/PR staining** (breast carcinoma)
- **Cytoplasmic CK7/CK20 patterns** (adenocarcinoma origin)
- **Nuclear loss of MMR proteins** (colorectal carcinoma)

**Exam Pearl:** Practice with high-resolution digital pathology images. Platforms like [eLearning FRCPath's MCQ bank](https://elearningfrcpath.com/pathology-mcq-2026) include annotated image-based questions with zoom functionality.

## Strategic Prioritisation: How to Allocate Study Time

Not all high-yield topics require equal time investment. Here's a practical allocation for the final 3 months before NEET-SS 2026:

1. **Oncopathology (40% of pathology study time):** Focus on breast, lung, colorectal, renal, and soft tissue tumours. Master molecular markers and IHC interpretation.

2. **Hematopathology (30% of pathology study time):** Prioritise acute leukemias, lymphomas, and MPNs. Memorise immunophenotypes and molecular fusions.

3. **Image-based spotters (20% of pathology study time):** Daily practice with histology and IHC images. Build visual memory for classic patterns.

4. **Other pathology domains (10% of pathology study time):** Infectious diseases, autopsy, forensic pathology—lower yield but still testable.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Ignoring Molecular Updates

Many candidates focus solely on morphology and miss molecular markers. NEET-SS 2026 will test WHO 5th edition updates—NPM1-mutated AML, HER2-low breast cancer, and RCC molecular subtypes are all fair game.

### 2. Neglecting Image Practice

Reading about "starry sky pattern" isn't enough. You need to recognise it in a low-power histology image under exam pressure. Dedicate time to image-based MCQs.

### 3. Overloading on Low-Yield Topics

Autopsy protocols and forensic toxicology are part of the syllabus, but they rarely appear in NEET-SS. Don't let them consume time better spent on oncopath and hematopath.

## Exam Pearls & Mnemonics

**Mnemonic for HER2 IHC scoring:**
- **0:** No staining or <10% incomplete membrane staining
- **1+:** Faint incomplete membrane staining in >10% cells (HER2-low)
- **2+:** Weak-moderate complete membrane staining in >10% cells (needs ISH)
- **3+:** Strong complete membrane staining in >10% cells (HER2-positive)

**Mnemonic for CK7/CK20 patterns:**
- **CK7+/CK20−:** Lung, breast, ovary, endometrium
- **CK7−/CK20+:** Colorectal, Merkel cell
- **CK7+/CK20+:** Urothelial, pancreas, stomach
- **CK7−/CK20−:** Hepatocellular, renal, prostate

**Mnemonic for Reed-Sternberg cell variants:**
- **Classical RS:** Bilobed "owl's eye" nuclei
- **Lacunar cells:** Retracted cytoplasm (nodular sclerosis HL)
- **Mummified cells:** Pyknotic nuclei (lymphocyte-depleted HL)
- **Popcorn cells:** Multilobated nuclei (nodular lymphocyte-predominant HL)

## Practice MCQs

**Q1.** A 45-year-old woman undergoes core needle biopsy for a 2.5 cm breast mass. Histology shows invasive ductal carcinoma, grade 2. IHC: ER 80%, PR 60%, HER2 IHC 1+, Ki-67 15%. What is the molecular subtype?

- A) Luminal A
- B) Luminal B (HER2-negative)
- C) HER2-enriched
- D) Triple-negative

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: A — Luminal A**

This tumour is ER/PR-positive, HER2-negative (IHC 1+ is HER2-low, not HER2-positive), and has low Ki-67 (<20%). These features define Luminal A subtype, which has the best prognosis among breast cancer subtypes. Note that HER2 IHC 1+ is now recognised as "HER2-low" and may be eligible for HER2-targeted ADCs like trastuzumab deruxtecan, but it's still classified as HER2-negative for molecular subtyping.

</details>

**Q2.** A 60-year-old man with chronic cough undergoes lung biopsy. Histology shows malignant cells with lepidic growth pattern along alveolar walls. IHC: TTF-1+, napsin A+, p40−. What is the diagnosis?

- A) Squamous cell carcinoma
- B) Adenocarcinoma, lepidic-predominant
- C) Small cell carcinoma
- D) Large cell neuroendocrine carcinoma

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Adenocarcinoma, lepidic-predominant**

Lepidic growth (tumour cells growing along pre-existing alveolar walls without stromal invasion) is a characteristic pattern of lung adenocarcinoma. TTF-1 and napsin A are positive in most lung adenocarcinomas. p40 negativity rules out squamous cell carcinoma (which would be p40+, TTF-1−). Lepidic-predominant adenocarcinoma has a better prognosis than other adenocarcinoma patterns.

</details>

**Q3.** A 35-year-old man presents with leukocytosis (WBC 80,000/μL) and splenomegaly. Bone marrow shows 15% blasts with Auer rods. Molecular testing reveals NPM1 mutation. According to WHO 5th edition, what is the diagnosis?

- A) AML with myelodysplasia-related changes
- B) AML with NPM1 mutation
- C) Myelodysplastic syndrome with excess blasts-2
- D) Acute leukemia of ambiguous lineage

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — AML with NPM1 mutation**

WHO 5th edition (2022) removed the 20% blast threshold for AML with defining genetic abnormalities, including NPM1 mutation. Even with 15% blasts, the presence of NPM1 mutation is sufficient for AML diagnosis. Auer rods (azurophilic needle-like inclusions) confirm myeloid lineage. NPM1-mutated AML has a favourable prognosis, especially when FLT3-ITD is absent.

</details>

## FAQs

**Q: How much weightage does oncopathology carry in NEET-SS 2026 pathology?**

Oncopathology (solid tumours, molecular markers, grading systems) accounts for approximately 25–30% of the pathology section in NEET-SS. This includes breast, lung, colorectal, renal, and soft tissue tumours. Combined with hematologic malignancies (another 15–20%), cancer-related pathology represents nearly half of all pathology questions. Prioritising oncopath is not optional—it's essential for a competitive score.

**Q: Are image-based spotter questions increasing in NEET-SS pathology?**

Yes. Analysis of recent NEET-SS papers shows a steady rise in image-based MCQs, now comprising 20–25% of pathology questions. These include histopathology slides, gross specimens, and IHC staining patterns. The shift reflects the move toward competency-based assessment, where visual diagnosis is a core skill. Daily practice with annotated digital pathology images is now as important as reading textbooks.

**Q: Should I focus on WHO 5th edition updates for NEET-SS 2026?**

Absolutely. NEET-SS 2026 will test WHO 5th edition (2022) classification changes, particularly in hematopathology (NPM1-mutated AML without 20% blasts) and solid tumours (new RCC entities, HER2-low breast cancer). Examiners favour recent updates because they test whether candidates are current with evolving diagnostic criteria. Dedicate time to understanding what changed from WHO 4th to 5th edition.

**Q: How do I improve at IHC interpretation for NEET-SS?**

IHC interpretation requires pattern recognition: nuclear (ER, PR, TTF-1), cytoplasmic (CK7, CK20, chromogranin), or membranous (HER2, E-cadherin). Practice with annotated IHC images, focusing on staining intensity (0/1+/2+/3+) and distribution (focal vs diffuse). Create flashcards for common IHC panels (e.g., adenocarcinoma of unknown primary: CK7, CK20, TTF-1, CDX2). Platforms with zoom-enabled IHC images are invaluable for exam-style practice.

**Q: Is hematopathology harder than solid tumour pathology for NEET-SS?**

Hematopathology has a steeper learning curve due to immunophenotyping complexity and molecular markers, but it's highly pattern-based once you master the fundamentals. Acute leukemias, lymphomas, and MPNs follow predictable diagnostic algorithms. Solid tumour pathology requires broader anatomical knowledge but often tests morphology and grading systems. Both domains are high-yield; allocate time proportionally (40% oncopath, 30% hematopath) based on your baseline comfort level.

- **Oncopathology, hematopathology, and image-based spotters** account for 60–75% of NEET-SS pathology questions—prioritise these domains.
- **WHO 5th edition updates** (NPM1-mutated AML, HER2-low breast cancer, new RCC entities) are prime exam material for NEET-SS 2026.
- **Molecular markers and IHC interpretation** are no longer optional—they're core competencies tested in most oncopath and hematopath questions.
- **Image-based MCQs** are rising in frequency; daily practice with histology, gross, and IHC images is essential.
- **Strategic time allocation** (40% oncopath, 30% hematopath, 20% image spotters, 10% other) maximises yield in the final months before NEET-SS 2026.
