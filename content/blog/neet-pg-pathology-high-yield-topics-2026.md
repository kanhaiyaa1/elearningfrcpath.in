NEET PG Pathology is one of the highest-scoring subjects if approached with deliberate prioritization. The 15–20 questions that appear from pathology each year are not randomly distributed — they cluster around recurring themes, predictable traps, and a set of clinical vignette types that the exam has returned to reliably across multiple years. Done right, pathology can shift your rank by thousands. Done wrong — covering every chapter uniformly — and you end up with 60% of the knowledge needed to answer 40% of the questions.

This guide is built from analysis of NEET PG papers from 2018 through 2024, current Robbins 10th edition syllabus alignment, and the question patterns that characterise the National Board Examinations' current approach to pathology. Use it as your roadmap, not a supplement.

---

## How Many Questions Come from Pathology in NEET PG?

NEET PG typically allocates 15–20 questions from Pathology across General Pathology, Systemic Pathology, and Haematology. The distribution across recent papers has been approximately:

| Section | Expected Questions |
|---|---|
| General Pathology | 6–8 |
| Systemic Pathology | 5–7 |
| Haematology | 3–5 |
| Clinical Pathology / Lab Medicine | 1–2 |

The General Pathology questions tend toward mechanism-based clinical vignettes — a patient scenario followed by "what is the pathological process?" rather than simple recall. Systemic Pathology questions are often image-free but present a defining clinical feature (Sister Mary Joseph nodule, Councilman bodies, Aschoff nodule) and test whether you know the diagnosis. Haematology questions routinely test lab parameter comparisons — particularly the iron deficiency vs anaemia of chronic disease table and leukaemia classification by surface markers.

## Tier 1: Highest-Yield Chapters (Do These First)

### 1. Cell Injury and Death

This chapter generates more NEET PG questions per page of content than almost any other in pathology. Examiners particularly favour clinical vignette-based necrosis identification — a scenario describing the clinical context, and you identify the necrosis type from the mechanism.

**What to master:**
- **Types of necrosis** — coagulative (solid organ infarction except brain), liquefactive (brain infarction, abscess), caseous (TB — cheese-like, no architecture preserved), fat necrosis (pancreatic enzymatic, traumatic breast), fibrinoid (immune complex deposition in vessel walls), gangrenous (coagulative + liquefactive, wet vs dry)
- **Reversible vs irreversible injury markers** — reversible: cell swelling, ER dilatation, ribosome detachment; irreversible: nuclear changes (pyknosis → karyorrhexis → karyolysis), membrane disruption, mitochondrial flocculent densities
- **Apoptosis vs necrosis** — single-cell process (apoptosis) vs group necrosis; caspase cascade, intrinsic (mitochondrial, cytochrome C, BAX/BCL-2 balance) and extrinsic (death receptor, Fas-FasL, TRAIL) pathways; no inflammation in apoptosis (key exam point)
- **Free radical injury** — superoxide, hydroxyl radical, hydrogen peroxide; antioxidants (superoxide dismutase, catalase, glutathione peroxidase, vitamins C and E)
- **Reperfusion injury** — re-entry of oxygen generates reactive oxygen species in already-compromised cells; calcium overload; increasingly tested in recent papers

**Mnemonic for irreversible injury signs — CALM:** Calcium influx, ATP depletion (severe), Lysosome rupture, Membrane phospholipid loss

---

### 2. Inflammation

Inflammation generates a dense cluster of MCQs, particularly around chemical mediators (every exam has at least one mediator question), granulomatous diseases, and the healing/repair chapter which is frequently paired with trap questions about keloids.

**What to master:**
- **Acute inflammation cellular sequence** — neutrophils first (24 hrs), then monocytes/macrophages (48–72 hrs); endothelial changes, margination, rolling, adhesion, transmigration (ICAM-1, VCAM-1, selectins)
- **Chemical mediators** — histamine (mast cells, basophils, platelets; immediate phase), serotonin (platelets), bradykinin (pain, vascular permeability, oedema), complement (C3a/C5a = anaphylatoxins; C5a = most potent chemotactic), prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2 = vasodilation + pain), leukotrienes (LTC4/D4/E4 = bronchoconstriction; LTB4 = chemotaxis)
- **Granulomatous inflammation** — caseating (TB, histoplasmosis, coccidioidomycosis) vs non-caseating (sarcoidosis, Crohn's, berylliosis, foreign body). Epithelioid macrophages are the defining cell. Langhans giant cells (horseshoe nucleus arrangement) = TB; Touton cells (foamy cytoplasm wreath) = fat necrosis/xanthoma; Schaumann bodies + asteroid bodies = sarcoidosis
- **Wound healing** — primary intention (clean sutured wound, minimal granulation tissue), secondary intention (open wound, more granulation, contraction by myofibroblasts); factors impairing healing: infection, foreign body, poor nutrition (vitamin C deficiency — collagen cross-linking), steroids (impair fibroblast activity), diabetes (impaired angiogenesis, neuropathy, infection risk)
- **Keloid vs hypertrophic scar** — keloid extends beyond wound margins, does not regress, common in dark-skinned individuals; hypertrophic scar stays within margins and tends to regress over time

### 3. Neoplasia (Very High Yield)

Neoplasia is the single most tested topic in General Pathology in NEET PG, and it also connects directly to Systemic Pathology questions in oncology-themed scenarios. Tumour markers appear in at least one question per paper — often as a "which marker is most appropriate to monitor recurrence" clinical scenario.

**What to master:**
- **Tumour markers** — CEA (colorectal, gastric, pancreatic monitoring), AFP (hepatocellular carcinoma, yolk sac tumour, neural tube defects), CA-125 (ovarian carcinoma, monitoring), CA 19-9 (pancreatic carcinoma), PSA (prostate — total and free), HCG (choriocarcinoma, gestational trophoblastic disease, testicular), LDH (non-specific but used in lymphoma, Ewing sarcoma)
- **Carcinogenesis** — chemical (initiators + promoters; direct-acting like nitrogen mustard vs indirect-acting like polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons requiring metabolic activation); radiation (UV → pyrimidine dimers → p53 mutation; ionising → double-strand DNA breaks); viral (HPV 16/18 → E6/E7 → p53/Rb inactivation; EBV → Burkitt lymphoma, NPC, Hodgkin lymphoma, EBV+ gastric carcinoma; HBV/HCV → HCC; HTLV-1 → adult T-cell leukaemia)
- **Oncogenes and tumour suppressors** — RAS (point mutation, GTPase, most common oncogene); MYC (translocation; Burkitt t8:14); HER2/NEU (amplification; breast, gastric); RB (retinoblastoma — two-hit hypothesis, also osteosarcoma); p53 (most commonly mutated in human cancers; Li-Fraumeni syndrome); APC (familial adenomatous polyposis, colorectal); BRCA1/2 (hereditary breast/ovarian)
- **Paraneoplastic syndromes** — ACTH (small cell lung Ca → Cushing's); ADH (small cell → SIADH); PTHrP (squamous cell lung Ca → hypercalcaemia); erythropoietin (renal cell Ca → polycythaemia); Eaton-Lambert (small cell → antibodies against presynaptic Ca channels)
- **Grading vs Staging** — grading = degree of differentiation (histological; I–III or I–IV); staging = extent of spread (clinical + pathological; TNM); staging has greater prognostic significance

**Mnemonic for Psammoma bodies — PMMS:** Papillary thyroid carcinoma, Meningioma, Mesothelioma, Serous papillary ovarian carcinoma

---

### 4. Haemodynamic Disorders

This chapter generates fewer total questions but very high-reliability questions — particularly Virchow's triad, embolism classification, and infarct colour. Never skip it.

**What to master:**
- **Oedema mechanisms** — increased hydrostatic pressure (heart failure), decreased oncotic pressure (nephrotic syndrome, liver failure, malnutrition), lymphatic obstruction (filariasis, post-mastectomy), sodium/water retention, increased vascular permeability (inflammation)
- **Virchow's triad** — endothelial injury + stasis/turbulence + hypercoagulability; classic MCQ: which component is most important in DVT? (stasis + hypercoagulability; endothelial injury less prominent in DVT)
- **Embolism types** — pulmonary (most common; from DVT of lower limb deep veins), fat (long bone fracture, adipose trauma; marrow fat globules; petechiae, respiratory distress 24–72 hrs post-injury), air/gas (iatrogenic, decompression sickness; >100 mL air lethal), amniotic fluid (most lethal obstetric emergency; fetal squames, mucin in maternal pulmonary capillaries)
- **Infarction** — red (haemorrhagic): loose tissue or dual blood supply (lung, liver, intestine, testis); white (anaemic): solid organs with single blood supply (heart, spleen, kidney). Brain infarct = liquefactive necrosis (not coagulative — important trap)
- **DIC** — consumptive coagulopathy; triggers: sepsis (most common), obstetric complications, malignancy, transfusion; labs: low platelets, prolonged PT/aPTT, low fibrinogen, elevated D-dimer/FDP, fragmented RBCs (microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia)

## Tier 2: Systemic Pathology (High Yield)

### 5. Cardiovascular Pathology

**MI timeline** — the most reliably tested topic in cardiovascular pathology:

| Time | Gross Change | Microscopic Change |
|---|---|---|
| 0–6 hours | None visible | None (earliest: wavy fibres) |
| 6–24 hours | Pallor | Coagulative necrosis, neutrophil infiltration begins |
| 1–3 days | Pale, soft | Dense neutrophil infiltration |
| 3–7 days | Yellow-tan, softest | Macrophages begin, granulation tissue starts |
| 1–3 weeks | Granulation tissue | Vascular granulation, collagen laid down |
| Months | Grey-white scar | Dense collagenous scar |

**Most common cause of death in MI:** Arrhythmia (not cardiac failure) — this is a guaranteed trap question. Cardiac failure becomes the leading cause only after 48 hours in those who survive the acute arrhythmic phase.

**Rheumatic heart disease** — Aschoff nodule (pathognomonic; fibrinoid necrosis + Anitschkow cells/caterpillar cells + Aschoff giant cells); Jones criteria (2 major or 1 major + 2 minor); pancarditis (all three layers); mitral stenosis is the most common long-term valvular lesion.

**Infective endocarditis** — vegetation comparison: IE (large, irregular, on valve leaflet closure line), NBTE non-bacterial thrombotic endocarditis (small, sterile, on closure line), Libman-Sacks (SLE; both surfaces of valve, small).

---

### 6. Respiratory Pathology

**Lung cancer** — most tested malignancy in respiratory pathology:
- Adenocarcinoma: most common overall; peripheral; TTF-1 positive; associated with non-smokers (especially in women); lepidic growth pattern (BAC-like); EGFR, ALK, ROS1 mutations
- Squamous cell carcinoma: central (hilar); Pancoast tumour (superior sulcus); PTHrP → hypercalcaemia; cavitation common; p63/CK5/6 positive
- Small cell carcinoma: central; neuroendocrine origin; ACTH (Cushing's), ADH (SIADH), Eaton-Lambert; most aggressive; responds to chemo but recurs; TdT negative, CD56/synaptophysin/chromogranin positive
- Large cell carcinoma: peripheral; poorly differentiated; diagnosis of exclusion

**COPD — emphysema types:** Centriacinar (centrilobular) = smoking; Panacinar (panlobular) = alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency; Paraseptal (distal acinar) = spontaneous pneumothorax in young adults.

**Sarcoidosis** — non-caseating granulomas; bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy; Schaumann bodies (calcified inclusions) + asteroid bodies (stellate inclusions) in giant cells; serum ACE elevated; CD4/CD8 ratio reversed in BAL.

---

### 7. GIT Pathology

**UC vs Crohn's comparison** — perennial MCQ staple:

| Feature | Ulcerative Colitis | Crohn's Disease |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Rectum → proximal (continuous) | Any part GI tract; skip lesions |
| Wall involvement | Mucosa + submucosa | Transmural |
| Granulomas | No | Yes (non-caseating) |
| Fistulae / abscesses | Rare | Common |
| Cancer risk | High (pancolitis) | Lower |
| Pathognomonic feature | Pseudopolyps, crypt abscesses | Cobblestone mucosa, creeping fat, rose-thorn ulcers |

**Gastric carcinoma:** Signet ring cells (intracytoplasmic mucin displacing nucleus); diffuse type (Borrmann); spreads to ovaries = Krukenberg tumour; left supraclavicular node = Virchow's node; periumbilical nodule = Sister Mary Joseph nodule.

---

### 8. Liver Pathology

**Hepatitis serology** — the HBV serological timeline is asked almost every year:
- **HBsAg** — first marker to appear; persists in chronic infection
- **Anti-HBc IgM** — acute infection marker; also present in window period
- **Window period** — HBsAg cleared, anti-HBs not yet appeared; only anti-HBc IgM is positive
- **HBeAg** — marker of active replication and high infectivity
- **Anti-HBs** — immunity marker; appears after vaccination or recovery

**Hepatocellular carcinoma:** AFP elevated (>400 ng/mL highly specific); predisposing factors: HBV/HCV cirrhosis, aflatoxin (HBV + aflatoxin synergistic), haemochromatosis, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, alcohol.

## Tier 3: Haematology (Don't Skip)

### 9. Anaemias

**Iron deficiency vs Anaemia of Chronic Disease** — the comparison table is a guaranteed question in most NEET PG papers:

| Parameter | Iron Deficiency | ACD |
|---|---|---|
| Serum iron | Low | Low |
| TIBC | High | Low/Normal |
| Serum ferritin | Low | High/Normal |
| Transferrin saturation | Low | Low |
| Bone marrow iron | Absent | Present (increased) |

**Megaloblastic anaemia:** B12 and folate deficiency both cause macrocytic anaemia, hypersegmented neutrophils, and megaloblastic marrow. Key distinction: B12 deficiency causes subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord (posterior and lateral columns); folate deficiency does not affect the nervous system. Schilling test distinguishes B12 malabsorption from dietary deficiency.

**Haemolytic anaemia markers:** Intravascular haemolysis = haemoglobinuria, haemoglobinaemia, haemosiderinuria, low haptoglobin, elevated LDH. Extravascular = splenomegaly, raised unconjugated bilirubin, normal haptoglobin (haemolysis occurs in reticuloendothelial system).

---

### 10. Leukaemias and Lymphomas

**ALL vs AML:**
- ALL: TdT+, CD10+ (CALLA); most common childhood malignancy; lymphoblasts (fine chromatin, scant cytoplasm); t(12:21) = good prognosis; t(9:22) Philadelphia = poor prognosis in ALL
- AML: Auer rods (pathognomonic; fused primary granules in blast cytoplasm); myeloperoxidase+; AML-M3 (APML) = t(15:17), PML-RARA, DIC risk, treated with ATRA

**CML:** Philadelphia chromosome t(9:22); BCR-ABL p210 fusion; basophilia prominent; blast crisis = transformation to acute leukaemia; imatinib (TKI) is first-line.

**Hodgkin Lymphoma:** Reed-Sternberg cells (owl-eye nucleoli, CD15+, CD30+, CD45−); subtypes — NLPHL (CD20+, CD15−, CD30−, best prognosis), NSHL (most common in young adults; birefringent collagen bands; lacunar cells), MCHL (many RS cells, mixed infiltrate; EBV strongly associated), LDHL (worst prognosis).

**NHL high-yield subtypes:**
- Burkitt lymphoma: c-MYC translocation t(8:14); starry sky pattern (macrophages clearing apoptotic tumour cells); jaw mass in endemic (Africa, EBV-associated) vs sporadic (abdominal, North America)
- Follicular lymphoma: BCL-2 overexpression, t(14:18); CD10+; indolent but incurable; transforms to DLBCL
- Mantle cell lymphoma: BCL-1 (cyclin D1) overexpression, t(11:14); CD5+CD23−; aggressive

## Top MCQ Traps in Pathology (Reliably Missed)

These appear in the exam repeatedly and consistently catch candidates who have read the topic but not anticipated the specific framing:

- **Most common cause of death in MI** → Arrhythmia (not pump failure — cardiac failure becomes dominant only >48 hours)
- **Brain infarct necrosis type** → Liquefactive (not coagulative — brain lacks supporting stroma, so necrotic tissue liquefies)
- **Councilman bodies** → Yellow fever (not hepatitis A/B/C — they are apoptotic hepatocytes in yellow fever)
- **Dutcher bodies vs Russell bodies** → Dutcher = intranuclear inclusions (Waldenström's/myeloma); Russell = intracytoplasmic Ig accumulations (plasma cells)
- **Most common leukaemia in adults** → CLL; in children → ALL
- **Reinke crystals** → Leydig cell tumour (not Sertoli — Sertoli cell tumours have Charcot-Böttcher filaments)
- **Signet ring cells** → Gastric carcinoma (primary); also lobular breast carcinoma (secondary presentation — both are testable)
- **Most common paediatric renal tumour** → Wilms' tumour (nephroblastoma); most common renal tumour in adults → Renal cell carcinoma (clear cell type)
- **Psammoma bodies — PMMS** → Papillary thyroid carcinoma, Meningioma, Mesothelioma, serous papillary ovarian carcinoma

## Exam Pearls and Smart Revision

**Pearl 1:** For every tumour marker question, the clinical scenario will specify a context — monitoring recurrence, initial diagnosis, or prognosis. AFP in HCC is for monitoring; PSA total + free ratio helps distinguish BPH from prostate cancer. Learning markers in context (not just lists) is what separates 85th percentile answers from 95th percentile ones.

**Pearl 2:** The Jones criteria for rheumatic fever can appear as a spot-the-major-criterion question within a case vignette. Major criteria: carditis, polyarthritis, chorea, erythema marginatum, subcutaneous nodules. Fever and elevated CRP/ESR are minor criteria.

**Pearl 3:** Wilson's disease and haemochromatosis appear together or separately in liver-metabolism questions. Wilson's = copper (reduced ceruloplasmin, elevated urine copper, Kayser-Fleischer rings, liver + basal ganglia); Haemochromatosis = iron (elevated serum iron, ferritin, transferrin saturation, HFE gene mutation; "bronze diabetes" — liver + pancreas + skin + heart + gonadal).

**Mnemonic for NEET PG Pathology High-Yield Priority — CHIN:**
- **C** — Cell injury and inflammation (Tier 1; do first)
- **H** — Haematology (anaemia comparisons, leukaemia markers)
- **I** — Innate to neoplasia (tumour markers, carcinogenesis, oncogenes)
- **N** — Necrosis types + systemic organopathology (CVS, lung, GIT, liver)

**60-Day Smart Revision Plan:**

| Days | Focus |
|---|---|
| 1–20 | General Pathology: Cell injury → Inflammation → Neoplasia → Haemodynamics |
| 21–40 | Systemic Pathology: CVS → Respiratory → GIT → Liver → Kidney → Breast |
| 41–50 | Haematology: Anaemias → WBC disorders → Coagulation / Bleeding disorders |
| 51–60 | Full revision: MCQ banks + PYQ 2018–2024, trap question review |

Daily minimum: 50 MCQs timed — without timing discipline, you will be unable to pace yourself in the actual exam. The [NEET-SS Pathology preparation course](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology) includes timed section modules useful for NEET PG practice rhythm as well, given the content overlap in General Pathology and Haematology.

## Practice MCQs — NEET PG Pathology 2026

**Q1.** A 52-year-old male presents with 3 weeks of fatigue, 4 kg weight loss, and early satiety. Endoscopy shows a poorly differentiated gastric tumour. Histology reveals cells with a single large intracytoplasmic vacuole displacing the nucleus peripherally. The tumour is diffusely infiltrating without gland formation. What is the most likely diagnosis, and which ovarian metastasis pattern is classically associated with this histological type?

- A) Intestinal-type gastric adenocarcinoma — Brenner tumour
- B) Diffuse-type gastric adenocarcinoma (signet ring cell carcinoma) — Krukenberg tumour
- C) Mucinous gastric adenocarcinoma — pseudomyxoma peritonei
- D) Squamous cell carcinoma of the stomach — direct ovarian extension

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Diffuse-type gastric adenocarcinoma (signet ring cell); Krukenberg tumour**

The histological description — cells with large intracytoplasmic mucin vacuole pushing the nucleus to the periphery — is the defining appearance of **signet ring cell carcinoma**, which is classified under the **diffuse-type** of gastric adenocarcinoma (Lauren classification). Diffuse-type gastric carcinoma lacks gland formation, infiltrates as single cells or small clusters through the stroma (linitis plastica pattern when the whole wall is involved), and is driven by CDH1 (E-cadherin) loss. Intestinal-type (option A) has gland formation and is associated with H. pylori, intestinal metaplasia, and the Correa cascade — it is more common in older males in high-incidence regions. **Krukenberg tumour** is bilateral ovarian metastasis characterised histologically by signet ring cells in a cellular ovarian stroma — it most classically arises from gastric primary (diffuse type), though breast (lobular carcinoma, which also has signet ring morphology) and colorectal primaries can also produce it. Pseudomyxoma peritonei (option C) is associated with appendiceal mucinous tumours, not signet ring gastric carcinoma.

</details>

**Q2.** A 7-year-old boy presents with pallor, hepatosplenomegaly, and recurrent infections. CBC shows WBC 85,000/mm³ with 90% blasts. The blasts are TdT positive, CD10 positive, CD19 positive, and myeloperoxidase negative. Cytogenetics shows t(12;21) translocation. Which of the following statements about this patient's prognosis and treatment is most accurate?

- A) t(12;21) is associated with poor prognosis in ALL; allogeneic stem cell transplant is first-line
- B) t(12;21) (ETV6-RUNX1 fusion) is associated with favourable prognosis in paediatric B-ALL; standard chemotherapy has >90% remission rate
- C) TdT positivity indicates this is AML-M1; Auer rods would be present on bone marrow examination
- D) CD10 positivity in this context indicates a mature B-cell lymphoma requiring rituximab

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — t(12;21) ETV6-RUNX1 is the most common and best-prognosis translocation in paediatric B-ALL**

The immunophenotype — TdT+, CD10+ (CALLA), CD19+, MPO− — is classic for **B-cell precursor ALL** (B-ALL), the most common childhood malignancy. TdT (terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase) marks immature lymphoid precursors — it is NOT a myeloid marker and does not indicate AML (option C is incorrect; AML blasts are TdT−, MPO+, and may show Auer rods). **t(12;21) creates the ETV6-RUNX1 (TEL-AML1) fusion** — this is the single most common translocation in paediatric B-ALL and carries an excellent prognosis: >90% complete remission with standard multiagent chemotherapy, 5-year OS >95%. It represents the good-risk end of the cytogenetic spectrum, in contrast to t(9;22) (Philadelphia chromosome, BCR-ABL) which carries poor prognosis in ALL (though tyrosine kinase inhibitors have improved this). CD10 in this context is a B-precursor marker, not a mature B-cell marker — mature B-cell lymphomas (option D) are TdT−, surface Ig+.

</details>

**Q3.** A 45-year-old woman undergoing laparotomy for acute abdomen has a 4 × 5 cm area of the small intestine that appears dark red, haemorrhagic, and demarcated. The mesenteric vessels supplying this segment are thrombosed. A biopsy of the affected segment taken 18 hours after symptom onset shows preserved outlines of necrotic cells without nuclei, coagulative architecture maintained, with diffuse haemorrhage into the wall. What is the most accurate description of this type of infarction, and why is the haemorrhage present?

- A) White (anaemic) infarct — single blood supply; haemorrhage from vessel rupture
- B) Red (haemorrhagic) infarct — loose tissue texture of intestine and dual/collateral blood supply allows re-entry of blood into necrotic zone
- C) Liquefactive necrosis — intestine undergoes liquefactive necrosis like brain tissue
- D) Caseous necrosis — transmural bowel involvement in TB produces caseous changes

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Red (haemorrhagic) infarct due to loose intestinal tissue and collateral blood re-entry**

Infarct colour depends on two factors: tissue texture and blood supply pattern. **Red (haemorrhagic) infarcts** occur in: (1) **loose/spongy tissues** — lung, intestine — where blood can re-enter from adjacent vessels into the necrotic zone; (2) tissues with **dual blood supply** — liver (hepatic artery + portal vein), lung (pulmonary artery + bronchial artery); (3) **venous outflow obstruction** — e.g., testicular torsion, ovarian torsion. The intestine qualifies on criterion 1 (loose wall texture) and criterion 3 (mesenteric venous thrombosis traps blood). **White (anaemic) infarcts** occur in solid organs with a single end-arterial supply — heart, spleen, kidney — where the dense stroma prevents re-entry of blood. The histology described — coagulative necrosis with preserved cell outlines but no nuclei, at 18 hours — is characteristic of all infarcts except brain. Brain infarcts undergo **liquefactive necrosis** (option C is incorrect for intestine) because the CNS lacks the structural stroma that maintains coagulative architecture in other organs. Caseous necrosis (option D) is a feature of TB, not ischaemic infarction.

</details>

## FAQs

**Which chapters in pathology are most important for NEET PG 2026?**

The three highest-yield chapters by question output are Neoplasia, Cell Injury/Death, and Inflammation — together these account for roughly 40–50% of all pathology questions in most NEET PG papers. Within systemic pathology, Cardiovascular (MI timeline, RHD), Haematology (anaemia comparisons, leukaemia immunophenotyping), and GIT (UC vs Crohn's, gastric carcinoma) generate the most reliable question clusters. The best approach is to complete these chapters in depth first before spreading time across lower-yield topics like skeletal muscle pathology or salivary gland tumours.

**How many MCQs should I do daily for NEET PG Pathology?**

A minimum of 50 pathology MCQs per day during the systemic revision phase (days 21–50) is the standard recommendation. The goal is not volume alone — it is timed practice with error analysis. Every wrong answer should generate a 2-line note in an error notebook categorised as: content gap, recall failure (knew it but blanked), or question-type misread. At the end of the revision cycle, the error notebook becomes your most efficient final-week revision tool. Untimed MCQ practice builds familiarity but does not build the exam-pacing skill that separates 85th from 95th percentile performance.

**Is Robbins Basic Pathology sufficient for NEET PG, or do I need Robbins and Cotran Pathologic Basis of Disease?**

Robbins Basic Pathology (10th edition) is sufficient for NEET PG. It covers the core mechanisms, morphology, and clinical correlations tested in the exam without the excessive detail of Robbins and Cotran (the "Big Robbins"). For an India-specific exam-oriented approach, Harsh Mohan's Textbook of Pathology (8th edition) is widely used and aligns well with the question style of NEET PG and other national entrance tests. Pathoma by Dr. Husain Sattar is valuable for the cardiovascular, GI, and neoplasia sections — the video format reinforces mechanisms that are otherwise retained poorly from text alone. Using all three comprehensively is not necessary — pick one primary text and supplement with MCQ-based revision.

**What is the difference between grading and staging in oncology, and which has greater prognostic significance?**

Grading refers to the histological degree of differentiation — how closely the tumour resembles its tissue of origin. It is assessed by the pathologist on biopsy or resection material and typically graded I (well-differentiated) to III or IV (poorly/undifferentiated). Staging refers to the anatomical extent of the disease — whether the tumour is confined to the organ of origin, has invaded locally, spread to regional lymph nodes, or metastasised distantly. Staging (TNM system: T = tumour size/invasion, N = node involvement, M = metastasis) has greater prognostic significance for most solid tumours because metastatic disease defines a fundamentally different biological and clinical situation than localised disease, regardless of grade.

**Which tumour markers should I know for NEET PG 2026, and in what context are they tested?**

The seven core markers tested repeatedly are: CEA (colorectal monitoring; also gastric, pancreatic), AFP (HCC monitoring; yolk sac tumour; neural tube defects in maternal serum), CA-125 (ovarian carcinoma, particularly serous; monitoring recurrence), CA 19-9 (pancreatic carcinoma; also cholangiocarcinoma), PSA (prostate; total PSA + free PSA ratio), HCG (choriocarcinoma; also mixed germ cell tumours), and LDH (lymphoma staging; Ewing sarcoma; non-specific marker of cell turnover). Questions typically present a scenario — "which marker should be used to monitor this patient after surgery?" — rather than asking for the marker in isolation. Knowing the clinical use (diagnosis vs monitoring vs screening) for each marker is what the exam tests.

## Key Takeaways

- Pathology generates 15–20 questions per NEET PG paper; prioritisation by chapter is more valuable than uniform coverage.
- Tier 1 (Cell Injury, Inflammation, Neoplasia, Haemodynamic Disorders) should be completed before any systemic pathology.
- Tumour markers, necrosis types, granuloma patterns, and leukaemia immunophenotypes are reliably tested every cycle — know these cold.
- The two most common traps: brain infarct = liquefactive necrosis (not coagulative); most common cause of death in MI = arrhythmia (not pump failure).
- 60-day plan: General Pathology (days 1–20) → Systemic (21–40) → Haematology (41–50) → MCQ revision (51–60).
- Daily timed MCQs with error-notebook analysis is non-negotiable — familiarity without timing does not build exam performance.
