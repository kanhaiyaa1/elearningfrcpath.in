In 2026, they are embedded in the diagnostic workup of soft tissue tumours, acute leukaemias, lymphomas, and an expanding range of rare entities — and that clinical embedding has reached the exam hall. **NEET-SS 2026 and FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology now include molecular-marker-driven MCQs that test gene fusions, RNA-based diagnostic signatures, and the clinical scenarios in which RNA testing catches what DNA testing alone misses.** A candidate who can identify a round-cell sarcoma on H&E but cannot name the defining fusion gene, explain why FISH might give a false-negative in a complex EWSR1 rearrangement, or identify when RNA-seq changes a diagnosis is leaving marks on the table. ## What "Hidden RNA Signals" Means in Diagnostic Pathology

DNA-based testing (sequencing, FISH, array CGH) identifies point mutations, copy number changes, and most gene rearrangements. But it has blind spots:

**Cryptic fusions**: Some chromosomal rearrangements occur through complex breakpoints or small intrachromosomal deletions that FISH probes spanning the expected breakpoint region miss. RNA-seq detects the resulting fusion transcript regardless of the DNA-level complexity of the rearrangement.

**Aberrant splicing**: Some pathogenic variants affect splice sites without changing the coding sequence — undetectable by coding-exon sequencing alone. RNA-seq reveals the resulting anomalous transcript directly.

**Low-expression transcripts**: Some diagnostically important isoforms or fusion transcripts are expressed at levels too low to be reliably detected by RT-PCR-based methods but are detected by deep RNA-seq with sensitive fusion-calling algorithms.

**Fusion gene verification**: When DNA-level NGS identifies a potential structural variant, RNA-seq confirms whether the variant produces an in-frame, functional fusion transcript — essential for distinguishing driver fusions from incidental structural noise.

For NEET-SS and FRCPath MCQ purposes, the key point is this: when a question presents a tumour with an inconclusive FISH result, a round-cell morphology without a definitive IHC profile, or an acute leukaemia without a mutation at expected hotspots, **RNA-seq or RT-PCR for specific fusion transcripts is the correct next-step answer** — not a repeat biopsy, not additional IHC, and not a broader DNA-sequencing panel alone.

## Fusion Genes in Soft Tissue Tumours — The Core NEET-SS / FRCPath Bank

Soft tissue tumours are the highest-yield fusion-gene territory in both exams. Most round-cell and spindle-cell sarcomas have a characteristic recurrent translocation; recognising the morphology-fusion pair is a single-best-answer anchor.

### EWSR1-FLI1 — Ewing Sarcoma / PNET

**Translocation:** t(11;22)(q24;q12)
**Tumour:** Ewing sarcoma / Ewing family of tumours (EFT)
**Morphology:** Sheets of monotonous small round blue cells with scant cytoplasm and evenly distributed nuclear chromatin; "cobblestone" or "lobular" arrangement; rosette formation (Homer-Wright pseudo-rosettes in neural variant)
**IHC:** CD99 (MIC2) strong membranous positivity (highly sensitive, not specific), FLI1 nuclear positivity, NKX2.2 nuclear positivity (more specific than FLI1); variably synaptophysin, CD56
**Key exam point:** CD99 is positive in many round-cell tumours (lymphoblastic lymphoma, poorly differentiated synovial sarcoma, small cell carcinoma) — it is sensitive but not specific for Ewing. FLI1 and NKX2.2 IHC add specificity. FISH for EWSR1 rearrangement (break-apart probe) is the standard first-line molecular confirmation; RT-PCR for specific EWSR1-ETS fusion transcript confirms the partner gene. ~85% of EFT cases have EWSR1-FLI1; ~10% have EWSR1-ERG (t(21;22)); rare cases involve non-EWSR1 FUS-based fusions — these are the "FISH-negative Ewing sarcoma" cases where RNA-seq resolves the diagnosis.

### SS18-SSX1 / SS18-SSX2 — Synovial Sarcoma

**Translocation:** t(X;18)(p11;q11)
**Tumour:** Synovial sarcoma (monophasic and biphasic)
**Morphology:** Biphasic: glandular epithelial component + cellular spindle cell stroma; Monophasic: uniform spindle cells in fascicles with haemangiopericytoma-like vasculature; calcification common
**IHC:** TLE1 strong nuclear positivity (highly sensitive, moderately specific); focal CK and EMA positivity in both components (particularly the glandular component in biphasic type); CD34 negative (useful DDx from solitary fibrous tumour)
**Key exam point:** SS18-SSX2 fusion is associated with monophasic synovial sarcoma and slightly better prognosis than SS18-SSX1. FISH for SS18 rearrangement detects ~95% of cases; RNA-seq or RT-PCR identifies the specific SSX partner. TLE1 IHC is a useful screening tool — nuclear expression is a surrogate for the SS18-SSX translocation.

### PAX3-FOXO1 / PAX7-FOXO1 — Alveolar Rhabdomyosarcoma

**Translocation:** t(2;13)(q35;q14) — PAX3-FOXO1; t(1;13)(p36;q14) — PAX7-FOXO1
**Tumour:** Alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS)
**Morphology:** Nests of poorly differentiated small round cells with fibrovascular septa creating an "alveolar" pattern; cells at the periphery of nests appear adherent ("cobblestone wall"); multinucleated giant cells may be present
**IHC:** Desmin, myogenin (strong nuclear, diffuse in ARMS > embryonal RMS), MyoD1, myosin; MYOD1 L122R mutation is associated with spindle cell/sclerosing RMS
**Key exam point:** PAX3-FOXO1 is found in ~55% of ARMS; PAX7-FOXO1 in ~22%. ~20% of histologically "ARMS" cases are **fusion-negative** on FISH and RT-PCR — these fusion-negative ARMS actually have a prognosis closer to embryonal RMS. This is a clinically significant distinction that is examined: the presence of the PAX fusion, not just the morphology, defines the high-risk ARMS subgroup.

### COL1A1-PDGFB — Dermatofibrosarcoma Protuberans (DFSP)

**Translocation:** t(17;22)(q21;q13) — ring chromosome or linear translocation; supernumerary ring chromosomes containing sequences from both chromosomes 17 and 22
**Tumour:** Dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans
**Morphology:** Infiltrating spindle cell tumour with storiform pattern in the dermis extending into subcutis; "honeycomb" infiltration of fat; fibrosarcomatous transformation in ~15% (DFSP-FS)
**IHC:** CD34 positive (strong diffuse); PDGFR-β positive; Factor XIIIa negative (contrasts with dermatofibroma which is Factor XIIIa positive)
**Key exam point:** COL1A1-PDGFB fusion generates a chimeric protein that constitutively activates the PDGFR-β kinase — making DFSP sensitive to **imatinib** (a PDGFR inhibitor). This is the most clinically actionable fusion in DFSP and is tested explicitly: "What targeted therapy is appropriate for recurrent/metastatic DFSP?"

### FUS-DDIT3 (CHOP) — Myxoid Liposarcoma

**Translocation:** t(12;16)(q13;p11) — FUS-DDIT3; less commonly t(12;22) — EWSR1-DDIT3
**Tumour:** Myxoid liposarcoma (myxoid/round cell liposarcoma in WHO 5th Edition)
**Morphology:** Myxoid matrix with prominent plexiform ("chicken-wire") vasculature; lipoblasts at varying stages of differentiation; round-cell component (poor differentiation) associated with worse prognosis
**IHC:** S100 positive; MDM2 and CDK4 negative (contrasts with well-differentiated/dedifferentiated liposarcoma); DDIT3 IHC (nuclear) can serve as a screening tool
**Key exam point:** FISH for DDIT3 rearrangement is the primary confirmation; RT-PCR distinguishes FUS-DDIT3 (more common) from EWSR1-DDIT3. MDM2/CDK4 amplification is negative — important to distinguish from well-differentiated liposarcoma (MDM2+/CDK4+), which does not have a recurrent fusion.

## Fusion Genes in Haematological Malignancies — Leukaemia and Lymphoma

### Core-Binding Factor AML

**RUNX1-RUNX1T1** (previously AML1-ETO): t(8;21)(q22;q22) → AML with maturation (FAB M2 equivalent). Long Auer rods, salmon-coloured cytoplasmic granules, aberrant CD19/PAX5 expression. Good prognosis; responds well to high-dose cytarabine.

**CBFB-MYH11**: inv(16)(p13q22) or t(16;16) → AML with abnormal eosinophils (FAB M4eo equivalent). Abnormal eosinophils in the BM aspirate with large basophilic granules mixed with eosinophilic granules — morphologically distinctive. Good prognosis; same treatment as t(8;21).

**Exam trap:** Both are core-binding factor AMLs with good prognosis, but they are detected by different methods. t(8;21) is detectable by conventional karyotype and FISH; inv(16) may be missed on routine karyotype and requires FISH or RT-PCR for confirmation. RNA-seq detects both fusion transcripts reliably. Minimal residual disease (MRD) monitoring uses RT-qPCR for both fusions.

### KMT2A (MLL) Rearrangements

**KMT2A** (chromosome 11q23) has over 90 fusion partners. Most common in infant ALL/AML: KMT2A-AFF1 (t(4;11)), KMT2A-MLLT3 (t(9;11)), KMT2A-ELL (t(11;19)). Associated with mixed phenotype (MPAL), CD10-negative pro-B ALL, and monocytic AML (M4/M5). Very poor prognosis in most contexts. Detection: FISH break-apart probe for KMT2A; RNA-seq or RT-PCR identifies the partner and fusion type.

### BCL2-IGH — Follicular Lymphoma

**t(14;18)(q32;q21)**: Places BCL2 under control of the IGH enhancer, causing BCL2 overexpression and resistance to apoptosis. Present in ~85% of follicular lymphoma and ~25% of DLBCL. BCL2 protein IHC overexpression is a surrogate (useful screening), but the translocation is confirmed by FISH or PCR. Importantly, BCL2 protein IHC is positive in many reactive follicles and other lymphomas — it is not specific without cytogenetic confirmation in a classification context.

### ALK Fusions — ALCL and Lung

In **anaplastic large cell lymphoma (ALCL):** NPM1-ALK from t(2;5)(p23;q35) is the most common (~80% of ALK+ ALCL). ALK IHC pattern reflects the fusion partner: NPM1-ALK → cytoplasmic + nuclear staining (NPM1 shuttles to nucleus); non-NPM1 partners (TPM3-ALK, etc.) → cytoplasmic only. ALK+ ALCL has significantly better prognosis than ALK− ALCL.

In **lung adenocarcinoma:** EML4-ALK (chromosome 2p inversion) — see previous post for therapeutic implications.

## Detection Methods: What Each Test Does and Where It Fails

| Method | Detects | Key Advantage | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional karyotype | Chromosomal translocations, large deletions/gains | Genome-wide view | Needs dividing cells; misses cryptic/small rearrangements |
| FISH (break-apart probe) | Gene rearrangement (partner-agnostic) | Rapid, archival tissue compatible | Misses complex rearrangements; doesn't identify fusion partner |
| RT-PCR | Specific known fusion transcript | Highly sensitive, quantitative (MRD) | Only detects pre-designed targets; misses novel fusions |
| RNA-seq | All expressed fusion transcripts | Partner-agnostic, detects cryptic fusions | Higher cost, requires RNA quality; bioinformatic analysis needed |
| DNA NGS (panel) | SNVs, indels, copy number, some fusions | Comprehensive mutation landscape | Misses some fusion types (intronic breakpoints); RNA confirmation needed |

## How to Build Fusion-Gene MCQ Skills

**Step 1 — Morphology-to-fusion anchoring**

For each fusion in the table, the MCQ stem will describe the morphology and IHC, then ask for the molecular test or the expected fusion. Anchor each fusion to one or two morphological or IHC features that are truly distinctive. For Ewing sarcoma: monotonous small round blue cells + CD99 membranous. For synovial sarcoma: biphasic gland + spindle cell pattern + TLE1. For DFSP: storiform spindle cells in dermis + CD34 positivity.

**Step 2 — Detection-method questions**

A separate MCQ type presents a tumour diagnosis and asks "which test confirms this?" Know the primary detection method per tumour (from the table above). Know when RNA-seq is the answer: when FISH is negative but clinical suspicion remains high, when the fusion partner matters for prognosis (PAX3 vs PAX7 in ARMS), or when a suspected fusion is not covered by standard FISH probes.

**Step 3 — Therapy-implication questions**

Three fusions have direct therapeutic implications currently tested: COL1A1-PDGFB (DFSP → imatinib), NTRK fusions (pan-tumour → larotrectinib/entrectinib), and BCR-ABL1 (CML/ALL → TKI selection). A question that gives a fusion result and asks for targeted therapy is testing these triads. For NTRK fusions specifically, pan-TRK IHC is a validated screening test; RNA-seq or targeted DNA/RNA NGS confirms. Larotrectinib and entrectinib are tumour-agnostic — effective regardless of tumour histology if NTRK fusion is confirmed. This tumour-agnostic therapy concept is a high-yield 2026 topic.

## Exam Pearls and Mnemonic

**Pearl 1:** FISH for **EWSR1** rearrangement is a break-apart probe, not a fusion probe. It tells you EWSR1 is rearranged but does not tell you the partner gene (FLI1, ERG, etc.). RT-PCR or RNA-seq identifies the partner. MCQs that ask "what test identifies the specific fusion partner in Ewing sarcoma?" are looking for RT-PCR or RNA-seq, not FISH.

**Pearl 2:** In ARMS, **fusion-negative** cases (no PAX3-FOXO1 or PAX7-FOXO1 on FISH/RT-PCR) behave like embryonal RMS, not classic ARMS. The prognostic stratification in current RMS trials is based on fusion status, not histological pattern alone. A histological ARMS without molecular confirmation is not treated as high-risk ARMS.

**Pearl 3:** **TLE1 IHC** is the most useful screening marker for synovial sarcoma but is not perfectly specific — it can be positive in MPNST, solitary fibrous tumour, and other spindle cell tumours. Strong, diffuse nuclear TLE1 in a biphasic or monophasic spindle cell tumour near a joint in a young adult is the most specific IHC pattern for synovial sarcoma.

**Pearl 4:** **Pan-TRK IHC** (clone EPR17341) screens for NTRK1/2/3 fusions in any tumour type. Cytoplasmic positivity suggests NTRK1 or NTRK3 fusions; nuclear + cytoplasmic suggests NTRK2 fusions. IHC positivity requires confirmation by RNA-seq or DNA/RNA NGS before larotrectinib/entrectinib is used. Secretory breast carcinoma and secretory carcinoma of the salivary gland (both driven by ETV6-NTRK3) are the highest-yield tumour types for pan-TRK IHC questions.

**Pearl 5:** **APL (PML-RARA, t(15;17))** is the one fusion where treatment must begin before molecular confirmation is available if the morphology and IHC are consistent — DIC risk makes delay dangerous. RT-PCR is the gold-standard confirmation; karyotype may miss variant translocations involving RARA with other partners (which respond differently to ATRA).

**Mnemonic — SEPAD for the five highest-yield soft tissue fusion genes:**
- **S** — Synovial sarcoma → SS18-SSX (TLE1+)
- **E** — Ewing sarcoma → EWSR1-FLI1 (CD99+, NKX2.2+)
- **P** — Pleomorphic alveolar RMS → PAX3/7-FOXO1 (myogenin diffuse+)
- **A** — Adipocytic (myxoid liposarcoma) → FUS-DDIT3 (S100+, MDM2−)
- **D** — DFSP → COL1A1-PDGFB (CD34+, imatinib responsive)

## Practice MCQs

**Q1.** A 14-year-old male presents with a painful swelling of the right femur diaphysis. X-ray shows an "onion-skin" periosteal reaction. Biopsy shows sheets of monotonous small round blue cells with scant cytoplasm and vesicular chromatin. IHC: CD99 strong membranous+, NKX2.2+, FLI1+, TdT−, CD3−, CD20−, desmin−. **Which molecular test most definitively confirms the diagnosis, and what is the expected finding?**

- A) FISH for EWSR1 rearrangement — expected to show break-apart signal
- B) RT-PCR for BCR-ABL1 — expected to show p190 isoform
- C) FISH for MDM2 amplification — expected to show high-level amplification
- D) RT-PCR for SS18-SSX — expected to show SS18-SSX2 fusion transcript

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: A — FISH for EWSR1 rearrangement showing break-apart signal**

The clinical picture (adolescent male, diaphyseal bone lesion, onion-skin periosteal reaction) and morphology (monotonous small round blue cells, CD99+, NKX2.2+, FLI1+, lineage markers negative) are classic for Ewing sarcoma / EFT. FISH using a break-apart probe spanning the EWSR1 locus (chromosome 22q12) is the standard first-line molecular confirmation — separation of the 5' and 3' EWSR1 signals confirms EWSR1 rearrangement. RT-PCR for EWSR1-FLI1 (option A mentions FISH correctly) or RNA-seq then identifies the specific fusion partner. Option B (BCR-ABL1 p190) is the ALL-associated fusion — irrelevant here. Option C (MDM2 amplification) characterises well-differentiated/dedifferentiated liposarcoma, not Ewing sarcoma. Option D (SS18-SSX2) is the synovial sarcoma fusion — morphology and age are consistent with Ewing, not synovial sarcoma.

</details>

**Q2.** A 28-year-old woman presents with a 3 cm painless parotid mass. Core biopsy shows a well-circumscribed tumour with microcystic, tubular, and solid growth patterns. The cells have abundant pale eosinophilic cytoplasm with intracytoplasmic secretory material (PAS-diastase positive). IHC: S100+, CK7+, mammaglobin+, GATA3+, HER2−. **Which molecular alteration is diagnostic of this tumour, and what targeted therapy is available?**

- A) KRAS G12C mutation — targeted by sotorasib
- B) ETV6-NTRK3 fusion — targeted by larotrectinib or entrectinib
- C) SS18-SSX1 fusion — no approved targeted therapy
- D) BRAF V600E mutation — targeted by vemurafenib

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — ETV6-NTRK3 fusion, targeted by larotrectinib or entrectinib**

This is secretory carcinoma of the salivary gland (previously called mammary analogue secretory carcinoma / MASC). Morphological clues: microcystic/tubular/solid architecture, pale eosinophilic secretory cytoplasm, PAS-diastase positive intra- and extracellular secretion. IHC: S100+, GATA3+, mammaglobin+ — almost identical to secretory carcinoma of the breast. The defining molecular alteration is the **ETV6-NTRK3 fusion** from t(12;15), present in ~95% of cases. This fusion generates a constitutively active chimeric tyrosine kinase. As an NTRK fusion-driven tumour, secretory carcinoma is eligible for tumour-agnostic NTRK inhibitor therapy (larotrectinib, entrectinib) when resection is not feasible. Pan-TRK IHC (cytoplasmic positivity for NTRK3 partner fusions) serves as a screening tool; RT-PCR or RNA-seq confirms the specific fusion. This question tests the clinical intersection of rare salivary gland pathology, IHC pattern recognition, and molecular-targeted therapy.

</details>

**Q3.** A bone marrow trephine from a 45-year-old male shows hypercellular marrow with increased myeloid precursors, abnormal eosinophilic precursors with large purple-red granules intermixed with eosinophilic granules, and monocytoid features in some elements. Flow cytometry shows a blast population at 8% with myelomonocytic phenotype. Routine karyotype is reported as 46,XY — no abnormality detected. The haematologist asks for additional molecular testing. **Which test is most likely to reveal the underlying diagnosis, and what finding is expected?**

- A) FISH for BCR-ABL1 — expected to show t(9;22)
- B) FISH or RT-PCR for CBFB-MYH11 — expected to show inv(16) or t(16;16)
- C) RT-PCR for PML-RARA — expected to show t(15;17)
- D) FISH for KMT2A break-apart — expected to show 11q23 rearrangement

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — FISH or RT-PCR for CBFB-MYH11, expected to show inv(16) or t(16;16)**

The morphological hallmark here is **abnormal eosinophils** with mixed purple-red (basophilic) and eosinophilic granules in the BM, combined with a myelomonocytic blast phenotype — this is the classic picture of **AML with CBFB-MYH11**, formerly AML-M4Eo (FAB). The critical point in this question: **inv(16) is frequently missed on conventional karyotype** — the inversion is subtle and requires high-quality, well-banded metaphase spreads. A normal karyotype does NOT exclude AML with inv(16). FISH using probes flanking the MYH11/CBFB loci on chromosome 16, or RT-PCR for the CBFB-MYH11 fusion transcript, is the appropriate next test. This question directly tests knowledge of a classic diagnostic pitfall in AML workup and the superiority of FISH/RT-PCR over conventional karyotype for inv(16) detection.

</details>

## FAQs

**Which fusion genes are highest-yield for NEET-SS 2026 soft tissue tumour MCQs?**

The five highest-yield fusion genes for NEET-SS and FRCPath soft tissue tumour questions are: EWSR1-FLI1 (Ewing sarcoma), SS18-SSX (synovial sarcoma), PAX3/7-FOXO1 (alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma), COL1A1-PDGFB (DFSP), and FUS-DDIT3 (myxoid liposarcoma). For each, know the characteristic morphology, IHC panel, primary detection method (FISH vs. RT-PCR vs. RNA-seq), and any therapeutic implication. DFSP (imatinib) and NTRK-fusion tumours (larotrectinib/entrectinib) carry the most clinically actionable therapeutic implications in the current exam pattern.

**When is RNA-seq the correct answer over FISH in molecular pathology MCQs?**

RNA-seq is the appropriate answer when: (1) FISH for a known gene locus (e.g., EWSR1) is negative but clinical suspicion for a fusion-driven tumour remains high, suggesting a cryptic or atypical rearrangement; (2) the question asks to identify the specific fusion partner rather than just confirm rearrangement; (3) the question involves a rare tumour or acute leukaemia where the expected fusion may not be covered by standard single-locus FISH probes; (4) the question describes a tumour with complex cytogenetics where conventional karyotype and targeted FISH are inconclusive. RNA-seq detects all expressed fusion transcripts partner-agnostically, resolving cases that FISH and targeted RT-PCR cannot.

**What is the difference between FISH break-apart probes and FISH fusion probes?**

A break-apart FISH probe flanks both sides of a single gene locus (e.g., EWSR1). When the gene is rearranged, the two probe signals separate — confirming rearrangement regardless of the fusion partner. This is partner-agnostic. A fusion FISH probe uses two differently labelled probes — one for each partner gene (e.g., EWSR1 and FLI1). When the translocation is present, the two signals co-localise into a fusion signal. This confirms the specific fusion but only works for the intended partner pair. Break-apart probes are more sensitive for detecting all rearrangements at a locus; fusion probes confirm a specific translocation with higher specificity for that event.

**Why does FISH for inv(16) miss AML with CBFB-MYH11 on routine karyotype?**

Inv(16)(p13q22) is a paracentric inversion — both breakpoints are on the same chromosome arm, and the DNA is reinverted without change in chromosome length or morphology. On routine G-banded karyotype, this subtle inversion requires optimal chromosome spreading and banding to visualise. It is consistently reported as missed in 10–15% of cases on standard karyotype. FISH with probes flanking the MYH11 (16p13) and CBFB (16q22) loci reliably detects the inversion regardless of karyotype quality, as does RT-PCR for the CBFB-MYH11 fusion transcript. This diagnostic limitation of karyotype for inv(16) is a classic MCQ question at FRCPath level.

**What is pan-TRK IHC and how should aspirants interpret it for NEET-SS MCQs?**

Pan-TRK IHC (clone EPR17341) detects the TRKA, TRKB, and TRKC receptor tyrosine kinase proteins encoded by NTRK1, NTRK2, and NTRK3. Any tumour expressing a pathogenic NTRK fusion will generally show pan-TRK IHC positivity because the fusion protein is constitutively expressed. Cytoplasmic IHC staining is typical for most NTRK fusions; nuclear + cytoplasmic staining suggests NTRK2 fusions. Pan-TRK IHC is used as a tumour-type-agnostic screening test — a positive result triggers confirmatory RNA-seq or DNA/RNA NGS for fusion characterisation before NTRK inhibitor therapy. For MCQs: pan-TRK IHC positive + consistent morphology (e.g., secretory carcinoma of salivary gland) → next test is molecular confirmation (RT-PCR for ETV6-NTRK3 or RNA-seq) → confirmed fusion → eligible for larotrectinib/entrectinib.

## Key Takeaways

- Fusion genes and RNA-based diagnostics are now core content in NEET-SS 2026 and FRCPath Part 1 oncopathology and haematopathology MCQs — not specialist extras.
- The SEPAD mnemonic (Synovial, Ewing, PAX-RMS, Adipocytic liposarcoma, DFSP) covers the five highest-yield soft tissue fusion pairs for exam purposes.
- Detection method knowledge matters: FISH identifies rearrangement; RT-PCR identifies specific fusions; RNA-seq resolves cryptic/FISH-negative cases — the appropriate next test differs by clinical scenario and is directly examined.
- Three fusion genes carry therapeutic implications that are exam-tested: COL1A1-PDGFB (DFSP → imatinib), NTRK fusions (any tumour → larotrectinib/entrectinib), and BCR-ABL1 (CML/ALL → TKI by isoform).
- Inv(16)/CBFB-MYH11 is the classic "missed on karyotype" AML — always confirm with FISH or RT-PCR when morphology shows abnormal eosinophils with mixed granules in a myelomonocytic AML.
