INI SS (Institute of National Importance Super Specialty) is the premier pathway to DM/MCh admissions at India's top government institutes — AIIMS New Delhi, PGIMER Chandigarh, JIPMER, NIMHANS, and SCTIMST. For pathology aspirants, INI SS is the direct route to DM Oncopathology, DM Haemato-pathology, and DM Neuropathology at institutes that produce the majority of India's specialist pathologists in these fields.

The critical difference between INI SS and NEET SS is not just the exam — it is the nature of what is tested. INI SS questions are consistently more clinical in application and more current in molecular content than NEET SS. Candidates who prepare for INI SS by rote recall alone do not pass. This guide covers the full examination structure, seat availability, high-yield content areas, and the preparation approach that actually works.

---

## What Is INI SS?

INI SS is conducted by **AIIMS New Delhi** on behalf of all Institutes of National Importance (INIs). It is a completely separate examination and counselling process from NEET SS — a different paper, different conducting authority, different seat matrix, and different counselling procedure. A candidate appearing in INI SS does not automatically participate in NEET SS, and vice versa. Many competitive candidates appear in both.

**Participating institutes in the INI SS 2026 cycle:**

- AIIMS New Delhi (the apex institute; most competitive)
- AIIMS Bhopal, Jodhpur, Patna, Rishikesh, Bhubaneswar, Nagpur, Raipur, Mangalagiri (newer AIIMS with evolving seat matrices)
- PGIMER Chandigarh
- JIPMER Puducherry
- NIMHANS Bangalore
- SCTIMST Thiruvananthapuram

The older and better-established institutes — AIIMS New Delhi, PGIMER, NIMHANS — have the most structured training programmes and the strongest clinical exposure for pathology DM candidates. The newer AIIMS institutes are building their programmes but may have fewer cases, less structured curriculum, and smaller faculty at this stage.

## Eligibility Criteria

- **Qualifying degree:** MD Pathology (or MD General Medicine for select streams) from an MCI/NMC-recognised institution
- **Registration:** Valid registration with MCI/NMC or a State Medical Council
- **Attempt limit:** No restriction on number of attempts under current INI SS policy
- **Age:** No upper age limit under current policy
- **Completion of MD:** Candidates must have completed their MD degree (or be in the final semester with results expected before joining — check the specific notification for the exact cutoff)

Candidates holding DNB Pathology should verify their eligibility explicitly in each cycle's notification. The treatment of DNB holders for INI SS eligibility has historically been inconsistent between cycles; do not assume eligibility from a previous year's notification.

## Exam Pattern

| Parameter | Details |
|---|---|
| Mode | Computer-Based Test (CBT) |
| Total Questions | 90 MCQs |
| Duration | 90 minutes |
| Question Type | Single Best Response (SBR) |
| Correct answer | +4 marks |
| Wrong answer | −1 mark |
| Unattempted | 0 marks |
| Language | English only |

**The negative marking changes strategy fundamentally.** Unlike NEET PG or FRCPath Part 1 where unanswered questions cost nothing, every wrong answer in INI SS costs a net 5-mark swing (lose 1 instead of gaining 4). A candidate who attempts 90 questions with 60% accuracy scores 240 − 30 = 210. A candidate who attempts 75 questions with 80% accuracy scores 240 − 15 = 225. Selective answering with high accuracy consistently outperforms blanket attempts. The standard guidance: only attempt a question if you can eliminate at least 2 of the 5 options.

**90 questions in 90 minutes = 60 seconds per question.** This is tighter than it appears — image-based questions and complex clinical scenario MCQs can consume 90–120 seconds. Time discipline and knowing when to skip and return is as important as content preparation.

## High-Yield Topics for INI SS Pathology 2026

INI SS questions are application-based, not recall-based. A question will rarely ask "what is the name of the mutation in GBM" — it will give you a clinical scenario, present a molecular result, and ask you to interpret its diagnostic or prognostic significance. Preparation must be anchored in understanding, not memorisation.

### General Pathology — Advanced Topics Tested in INI SS

**Cell Injury and Intracellular Accumulations:**
- Lipofuscin (wear-and-tear pigment; brown granules; peroxidation of polyunsaturated lipids; marker of ageing/atrophy)
- Mallory-Denk bodies (alcoholic liver disease; keratin aggregates; ubiquitin+)
- Lewy bodies (Parkinson disease; synuclein+ eosinophilic inclusions in substantia nigra neurons)
- Negri bodies (rabies; cytoplasmic viral inclusions in hippocampal neurons and Purkinje cells)

**Amyloidosis** — a reliably tested topic in INI SS:
- AL amyloid (primary; plasma cell dyscrasia; immunoglobulin light chains; affects heart, kidney, tongue, nerves)
- AA amyloid (secondary; chronic inflammation — TB, RA, IBD, FMF; serum amyloid A precursor; affects kidney, spleen, liver)
- ATTR amyloid (transthyretin; hereditary or senile systemic; cardiac deposition; increasing therapeutic relevance — tafamidis)
- Congo red staining → apple-green birefringence under polarised light (pathognomonic)
- Typing methods: IHC (most practical), mass spectrometry on laser-captured material (most accurate; particularly important when IHC is equivocal)

**Inflammasome and NETs — newer syllabus additions:**
- NLRP3 inflammasome activates caspase-1 → cleaves pro-IL-1β and pro-IL-18 → pyroptosis; tested in context of gout (urate crystals activate NLRP3), atherosclerosis, and inflammatory bowel disease
- NETs (Neutrophil Extracellular Traps): chromatin + granule proteins extruded by activated neutrophils; role in sepsis, thrombosis (NETosis-mediated clot formation), lupus (NETs as source of nuclear antigens)

### Molecular Pathology — Driver Mutations by Tumour Type (High INI SS Yield)

| Mutation | Tumour Types | Therapeutic Implication |
|---|---|---|
| BRAF V600E | Papillary thyroid Ca, Melanoma, Hairy cell leukaemia, CRC (≈10%) | Vemurafenib/dabrafenib (melanoma); BRAF + MEK inhibition |
| KRAS | Pancreatic Ca (>90%), CRC, NSCLC adenocarcinoma | Anti-EGFR therapy resistance in CRC (KRAS mutant = no cetuximab) |
| IDH1/IDH2 | Glioma grade 2/3, AML, Cholangiocarcinoma | IDH inhibitors (enasidenib IDH2; ivosidenib IDH1) |
| EZH2 | Follicular lymphoma, DLBCL | Tazemetostat (EZH2 inhibitor) |
| ALK rearrangements | NSCLC adenocarcinoma, ALCL, IMT | Crizotinib, alectinib, lorlatinib |
| c-KIT | GIST, Mastocytosis, AML-M2 with t(8:21) | Imatinib (GIST); avapritinib (PDGFRA D842V) |
| EGFR (exon 19 del / exon 21 L858R) | NSCLC adenocarcinoma | Erlotinib, gefitinib, osimertinib (T790M) |
| BRCA1/BRCA2 | Breast, Ovarian, Pancreatic, Prostate | PARP inhibitors (olaparib, niraparib) |
| HER2 amplification | Breast, Gastric, CRC (emerging) | Trastuzumab, pertuzumab, T-DM1, trastuzumab deruxtecan |

**MSI (Microsatellite Instability):** Lynch syndrome testing methodology — IHC for MMR proteins (MLH1, PMS2, MSH2, MSH6) as initial screen; reflexing to PCR/NGS-based MSI testing for confirmation. MLH1 loss → check BRAF V600E / MLH1 promoter methylation to distinguish Lynch from sporadic MSI-H. MSI-H = biomarker for response to pembrolizumab (pan-tumour approval).

**Liquid biopsy:** ctDNA (circulating tumour DNA) — clinical uses: early detection (investigational), treatment monitoring, resistance mutation identification (e.g., EGFR T790M in plasma before re-biopsy), minimal residual disease. Limitations: false negatives in low-volume disease; no morphological context; not yet standard of care for most indications.

### Oncopathology — INI SS Specific

**WHO 5th Edition CNS Classification (2021) — mandatory for INI SS:**
- Glioblastoma, IDH wild-type: requires IDH wt + either histological features (necrosis/MVP) OR molecular signature (TERT promoter mutation + EGFR amplification + +7/−10 chromosome)
- Astrocytoma, IDH-mutant (Grades 2, 3, 4): IDH mutant + ATRX loss; Grade 4 requires CDKN2A/B homozygous deletion or necrosis/MVP
- Oligodendroglioma, IDH-mutant and 1p19q codeleted (Grades 2, 3)
- Diffuse midline glioma, H3K27-altered: H3 K27M mutation or EZHIP overexpression; pediatric predominant; always Grade 4
- Medulloblastoma: molecular groups — WNT-activated (best prognosis), SHH-activated TP53-wild (intermediate), SHH-activated TP53-mutant (poor), Non-WNT/Non-SHH (poor)

**IHC panels for carcinoma of unknown primary (CUP):** A high-frequency INI SS topic:

| CK7/CK20 Pattern | Common Primary Sites |
|---|---|
| CK7+ / CK20− | Lung, Breast, Ovary, Cholangiocarcinoma, Thyroid |
| CK7− / CK20+ | Colorectal carcinoma, Merkel cell carcinoma |
| CK7+ / CK20+ | Urothelial, Pancreatic, Gastric |
| CK7− / CK20− | Prostate, Renal cell, Hepatocellular, Squamous cell (many sites) |

Additional markers: TTF-1 (lung adenocarcinoma + SCLC + thyroid), PAX8 (kidney, thyroid, gynecological tract), CDX2 (colorectal, small bowel), GATA3 (breast, urothelial), NKX3.1 (prostate), HepPar1/arginase (hepatocellular).

**Predictive vs prognostic biomarkers — a reliable trap question:**
- **Prognostic biomarker:** predicts outcome (overall survival, disease-free survival) regardless of treatment — e.g., tumour grade, stage, ER status (predicts survival in breast Ca)
- **Predictive biomarker:** predicts response (or resistance) to a specific treatment — e.g., HER2 amplification (predicts response to trastuzumab), KRAS mutation (predicts resistance to anti-EGFR therapy), PD-L1 expression (predicts response to checkpoint inhibitors)
- Some biomarkers are both: ER in breast carcinoma is both prognostic (ER+ = better prognosis) and predictive (ER+ = responds to endocrine therapy)

### Haemato-pathology — INI SS Specific

**Myelodysplastic Syndromes (WHO 2022 classification update):**
The 2022 WHO revision renamed and restructured MDS categories. Key changes: MDS is now termed **MDS with defining genetic abnormalities** (del 5q, SF3B1 mutation, biallelic TP53 mutation) or **MDS, morphologically defined** (replacing prior dysplasia-based naming). Blast thresholds remain: <5% = MDS, 5–9% = MDS with increased blasts-1, 10–19% = MDS with increased blasts-2, ≥20% = AML.

**Plasma cell myeloma — high-yield for INI SS:**
- CRAB criteria: hyperCalcaemia (>11 mg/dL), Renal failure (creatinine >2 mg/dL), Anaemia (Hb <10 g/dL), Bone lesions
- FISH cytogenetics for risk stratification: high-risk = t(4;14), t(14;16), del 17p; standard-risk = t(11;14), hyperdiploidy
- Smouldering myeloma vs active myeloma: smouldering has M-protein and/or plasma cells ≥10% but no CRAB criteria or SLiM-CRAB biomarkers

**Coagulation high-yield:**
- **TTP vs HUS:** TTP = ADAMTS13 deficiency (<10%) → ultra-large VWF multimers → platelet thrombi in arterioles and capillaries; pentad = MAHA + thrombocytopenia + fever + renal failure + neurological. HUS = Shiga toxin (STEC) or complement dysregulation → renal-predominant. Treatment differs: TTP = plasma exchange; aHUS = eculizumab (anti-C5)
- **HIT:** Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia — IgG antibodies against PF4-heparin complex activate platelets → paradoxical thrombosis; 4T score (thrombocytopenia degree, timing, thrombosis, other causes); treatment = direct thrombin inhibitors (argatroban), NOT heparin or warfarin
- **VWD Type 2B** — specifically tested: gain-of-function VWF mutation → spontaneous binding to platelet GPIb → clearance of large VWF multimers + platelets → thrombocytopenia + paradoxically low large multimers; distinguish from HIT and other thrombocytopenic states

### Cytopathology

**Bethesda System for Thyroid FNA (TBS-Thyroid 2023 update):** All 6 categories with malignancy risk and recommended management:

| Category | Name | Risk of Malignancy | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Non-diagnostic | Variable | Repeat FNA |
| II | Benign | 5–10% | Clinical follow-up |
| III | AUS/FLUS | 10–30% | Repeat FNA or molecular testing |
| IV | Follicular neoplasm | 25–40% | Surgical lobectomy |
| V | Suspicious for malignancy | 50–75% | Near-total thyroidectomy vs lobectomy |
| VI | Malignant | 97–99% | Surgical management |

**Effusion cytopathology IHC panel** — tested in INI SS as a multi-component scenario question:
- Reactive mesothelial cells: WT1+, D2-40+, calretinin+, MOC31−, Ber-EP4−
- Mesothelioma: WT1+, D2-40+, calretinin+, MOC31−/weak, Ber-EP4−; BAP1 loss (IHC) supports malignant mesothelial
- Metastatic adenocarcinoma: WT1−, D2-40−, calretinin−, MOC31+, Ber-EP4+; site-specific markers (TTF-1 for lung, PAX8 for gynecological)

## Practice MCQs — INI SS Pathology Style

**Q1.** A 68-year-old woman presents with progressive fatigue, renal impairment (creatinine 2.4 mg/dL), and hypercalcaemia (12.2 mg/dL). Serum protein electrophoresis shows an M-spike. Bone marrow biopsy reveals 35% plasma cells. FISH cytogenetics shows: t(4;14) positive, del 17p positive, t(11;14) negative, hyperdiploidy negative. Which of the following most accurately describes this patient's risk category and treatment implication?

- A) Standard risk myeloma — t(11;14) absence defines standard risk; triplet therapy with VRd (bortezomib-lenalidomide-dexamethasone) is appropriate
- B) High-risk myeloma — t(4;14) and del 17p together define double-hit high risk; intensified induction and early autologous SCT consideration
- C) Smouldering myeloma — plasma cell percentage and M-spike without organ damage criteria
- D) Low-risk myeloma — hyperdiploidy absence is protective; oral melphalan is standard

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — High-risk myeloma; t(4;14) and del 17p define double-hit high risk**

The CRAB criteria are met (hyperCalcaemia 12.2 mg/dL, Renal failure creatinine 2.4 mg/dL) confirming **active multiple myeloma** — not smouldering (option C requires absence of CRAB criteria and SLiM-CRAB biomarkers). FISH cytogenetics risk stratification in myeloma: **high-risk lesions = t(4;14), t(14;16), del 17p** (TP53 loss). Standard-risk = t(11;14) (translocation involving BCL1/CCND1), hyperdiploidy. This patient has two high-risk lesions — t(4;14) AND del 17p — classifying as **double-hit** high-risk myeloma, which carries the worst prognosis in myeloma cytogenetic risk models (mSMART, R-ISS). Treatment implication: VRd (bortezomib + lenalidomide + dexamethasone) induction remains backbone, but high-risk patients benefit from daratumumab-based quadruplet induction (DARA-VRd) and early autologous stem cell transplant where eligible, with lenalidomide + bortezomib maintenance. Option A is incorrect: standard risk is defined by the presence of hyperdiploidy or t(11;14), not absence of t(11;14) alone. Option D is incorrect: oral melphalan is an outdated first-line therapy.

</details>

**Q2.** A 54-year-old male presents with a 2-week history of confusion, focal neurological deficit, and raised ICP. MRI shows a ring-enhancing left temporal lesion. Stereotactic biopsy shows a diffuse glial tumour with nuclear atypia and mitoses but NO necrosis and NO microvascular proliferation. Molecular analysis: IDH wild-type, TERT promoter mutation present, EGFR amplification present, chromosome 7 gain and chromosome 10 loss (+7/−10). What is the WHO 5th Edition diagnosis and grade?

- A) Astrocytoma, IDH-mutant, Grade 3 — mitoses without necrosis defines Grade 3
- B) Glioblastoma, IDH wild-type, WHO Grade 4 — molecular signature alone is sufficient for GBM diagnosis under WHO 5th Edition even without histological necrosis or MVP
- C) Diffuse astrocytic glioma, NOS — IDH wild-type with insufficient molecular data for classification
- D) High-grade neuroepithelial tumour, unclassified — pending MGMT methylation status

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Glioblastoma, IDH wild-type, WHO Grade 4**

This question tests the most important update in the **WHO CNS 5th Edition (2021)**: the diagnosis of **Glioblastoma, IDH wild-type, Grade 4** no longer requires histological necrosis or microvascular proliferation (MVP). Under the 4th Edition, necrosis or MVP was required. Under the 5th Edition, in an IDH wild-type diffuse astrocytic tumour, the presence of the **GBM molecular signature** — TERT promoter mutation + EGFR amplification + +7/−10 chromosome pattern — is sufficient to classify the tumour as GBM Grade 4, even if the histology appears lower-grade. This reflects the biological equivalence of the molecular signature and the histological features in defining GBM behaviour. Option A (IDH-mutant astrocytoma Grade 3) requires an IDH mutation — this tumour is IDH wild-type, excluding the astrocytoma category. Option C (NOS) would only apply if molecular data were incomplete — here the full GBM molecular signature is present, making NOS inappropriate. Option D (MGMT methylation status) is a prognostic/predictive marker for temozolomide response, not a diagnostic criterion for classification.

</details>

**Q3.** A 45-year-old woman presents with epistaxis, menorrhagia, and easy bruising since childhood. Platelet count is 145 × 10⁹/L (mildly low). VWF antigen is 35% (low). VWF ristocetin cofactor activity is 15% (low). RIPA (ristocetin-induced platelet aggregation) shows enhanced aggregation at LOW ristocetin concentrations. Large VWF multimers are absent on multimer analysis. What is the most likely diagnosis and the most important treatment consideration?

- A) VWD Type 1 — partial VWF quantitative deficiency; DDAVP is first-line
- B) VWD Type 2A — loss of large multimers due to defective VWF; DDAVP may be ineffective; VWF concentrate preferred
- C) VWD Type 2B — gain-of-function VWF mutation causing spontaneous platelet binding; DDAVP is contraindicated as it may worsen thrombocytopenia
- D) VWD Type 3 — complete VWF absence; DDAVP is ineffective; VWF/FVIII concentrate required

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: C — VWD Type 2B; DDAVP contraindicated**

The key diagnostic clue is **enhanced RIPA at LOW ristocetin concentrations** combined with **absence of large VWF multimers** and **mild thrombocytopenia**. This pattern is pathognomonic of **VWD Type 2B**: a gain-of-function mutation in VWF (typically in the A1 domain that binds GPIbα) causes VWF to bind platelet GPIb spontaneously, without requiring ristocetin. This results in: (1) clearance of large VWF multimers (they are consumed by platelet binding), explaining absent large multimers; (2) mild-to-moderate thrombocytopenia (platelets are also cleared as part of the VWF-platelet complex). DDAVP (desmopressin) is **contraindicated in Type 2B** because it releases endogenous VWF from endothelial cells — the released VWF, carrying the gain-of-function mutation, will spontaneously bind platelets and worsen thrombocytopenia. Treatment is VWF concentrate (purified or recombinant VWF) which provides normal VWF without the gain-of-function mutation. Type 2A (option B) also lacks large multimers but shows reduced (not enhanced) platelet binding — the multimer loss in 2A is due to increased ADAMTS13 cleavage (loss-of-function). Type 1 (option A) has normal multimer pattern, reduced VWF quantity. Type 3 (option D) has undetectable VWF and severe bleeding.

</details>

## FAQs

**Is INI SS harder than NEET SS Pathology?**

The two exams test different things, making direct difficulty comparison imprecise. INI SS consistently features more application-based molecular pathology and more current classification updates (WHO 2022, WHO CNS 5th edition) than NEET SS. NEET SS has a larger question bank pattern, with more breadth across all pathology subspecialties. Candidates who find conceptual reasoning easier tend to prefer INI SS-style questions; those with strong recall prefer NEET SS-style broad coverage. In practice, the most competitive candidates prepare for both simultaneously — the content overlap is approximately 80%, and the format adjustment (selective answering with negative marking vs full attempt) is the primary distinction.

**Can DNB Pathology holders appear in INI SS?**

Eligibility of DNB Pathology candidates for INI SS varies between cycles. Some years, DNB holders with a valid NMC-equivalent registration have been eligible; in other cycles the notification has restricted eligibility to MD degree holders. Always read the official AIIMS notification for the specific cycle you are targeting — do not rely on previous year policies or third-party eligibility guides. If in doubt, contact the AIIMS Exam Cell directly before submitting an application.

**How many attempts are allowed in INI SS?**

Under the current INI SS policy, there is no upper limit on attempts. Unlike some other entrance examinations, INI SS does not impose a maximum number of tries. The absence of an attempt limit is strategically important for candidates who may need to appear multiple cycles to build competitive rank — the negative marking format means a poor-preparation attempt can produce a significantly negative score, so timing your attempts when genuinely prepared is advisable.

**What is the best way to practise negative marking strategy for INI SS?**

The only effective way is timed mock exams run under +4/−1 conditions from the start of your preparation — not just in the final weeks. Practising in a no-negative-marking environment and then switching to negative marking close to the exam does not give you enough time to recalibrate your selective-answering instincts. In your mock analysis, track separately: questions you attempted and got right, questions you attempted and got wrong (these are the costly errors), and questions you skipped but knew on review. The goal is to drive down wrong attempts while maintaining or increasing correct attempts — not simply to attempt more or fewer questions. Our [NEET-SS pathology MCQ bank at eLearning FRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/neet-ss-pathology) includes negative marking simulation mode designed for exactly this calibration.

**Which pathology subspecialty has the most seats in INI SS — Oncopathology, Haemato-pathology, or Neuropathology?**

Across all INI institutes combined, Haemato-pathology DM tends to have the most total seats — AIIMS New Delhi and PGIMER Chandigarh both have competitive haematopathology programmes with 2–3 seats each. Oncopathology DM seats are slightly fewer but highly sought after given the career trajectory. Neuropathology DM has the fewest seats nationally — NIMHANS Bangalore is the dominant training centre and has 2–3 seats; most other institutes have 0–1. Candidates targeting Neuropathology DM should be aware that the competition-to-seat ratio in this subspecialty is the most intense of the three, and NIMHANS rank is the primary determinant of admission. For a full subspecialty comparison including career outcomes and training structure, the [DM pathology branch choice guide](https://elearningfrcpath.in/blog/dm-pathology-branch-choice-neet-ss-2026/) covers these differences in depth.

## Key Takeaways

- INI SS is separate from NEET SS — different exam, different counselling, different institutes; appear in both to maximise seat coverage.
- Negative marking (−1) in INI SS demands selective answering: only attempt questions where you can eliminate at least 2 options.
- 90 questions in 90 minutes = 60 seconds per question — time discipline and knowing when to skip is a preparation skill, not an exam-day improvisation.
- WHO 5th Edition CNS classification and WHO 2022 haematopathology classification are mandatory — questions from these appear every cycle.
- Predictive vs prognostic biomarker is a reliable trap question — know the distinction with examples.
- VWD Type 2B and TTP vs aHUS are high-frequency haematopathology specifics — both test mechanistic understanding, not recall.
- Amyloid typing by mass spectrometry, NLRP3 inflammasome, and liquid biopsy/ctDNA are newer-syllabus topics that have appeared in recent INI SS papers.
