\## The Game-Changer You Must Know



The WHO 5th Edition (WHO-HEM5) has eliminated the 20% blast requirement for diagnosing AML with NPM1 mutation. This is a direct exam target.



\## Why Did WHO Remove the Threshold?



Studies showed that patients with NPM1-mutated myeloid neoplasms with <20% blasts have:

\- Shorter survival compared to wild-type AML

\- Better outcomes with upfront intensive (AML-type) chemotherapy



Waiting for 20% blasts meant delayed treatment and worse prognosis.



\## Key Morphologic Clues for NPM1-Mutated AML



\- Myelomonocytic or monocytic differentiation (common)

\- Cup-like nuclear invaginations in >10% blasts (highly specific)

\- CD34 negative in \~80% of cases

\- Multilineage dysplasia can be present (doesn't change prognosis)



\## IHC Shortcut



Cytoplasmic NPM1 staining by immunohistochemistry can serve as a surrogate marker for NPM1 mutation — useful when molecular testing is delayed.



\## Exam Pearl



\*\*Q:\*\* A 55-year-old presents with pancytopenia. BM shows 15% blasts with cup-like nuclei, CD34 negative, cytoplasmic NPM1 positive. Diagnosis?



\*\*A:\*\* AML with NPM1 mutation — despite <20% blasts, WHO-HEM5 criteria are met.
