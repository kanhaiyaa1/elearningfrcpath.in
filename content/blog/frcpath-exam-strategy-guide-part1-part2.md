FRCPath (Fellowship of the Royal College of Pathologists) is the benchmark pathology qualification in the UK and is recognised in over 50 countries — including India, the UAE, Singapore, Australia, and Canada. Passing it signals a level of diagnostic competence that opens doors to consultant-level pathology posts globally. But the exam has a reputation for being difficult, particularly Part 1, where first-attempt pass rates sit around 55–65%. The candidates who pass on first or second attempt are not necessarily smarter — they approached preparation systematically. This guide sets out exactly how to do that.

---

## What Is FRCPath?

FRCPath is awarded by the **Royal College of Pathologists (RCPath), London**, following successful completion of a structured examination pathway. It certifies competency across pathology subspecialties:

- **Histopathology** — the most widely sat pathway globally
- Haematology
- Clinical Biochemistry
- Microbiology and Virology
- Immunology
- Cytopathology
- Forensic Pathology
- Chemical Pathology

This guide focuses on **Histopathology FRCPath** — the pathway most relevant to the majority of candidates reading this, including MD Pathology graduates in India targeting UK or international positions, and pathology trainees in UK ST programmes.

## FRCPath Structure: What You Are Actually Sitting

| Exam | Format | Typical Sitting Point |
|---|---|---|

| **Part 2** | Written Papers (SAQs + Essays) + Practical OSPE | After ~4–5 years of ST-level training |

Both parts are conducted **twice per year**, with sittings typically in **May/June** and **October/November**. Applications open approximately 3 months before the exam date. For the Autumn 2026 sitting specifically, [the application window and deadline details are covered in a dedicated post](https://elearningfrcpath.in/blog/frcpath-autumn-2026-application-neet-ss-dual-plan/) — including the critical fact that Autumn 2026 is the last sitting in the current format before Spring 2027 brings a new examination structure.

## FRCPath Part 1: Histopathology — In Depth

### Exam Format

- **Paper 1:** 90 Best-of-5 MCQs — 2.5 hours (100 seconds per question)
- **Paper 2:** 60 Extended Matching Questions (EMQs) — 2.5 hours (~2.5 minutes per stem group)
- Total: 150 questions across a single exam day
- **Pass mark:** approximately 60–65%, set by the borderline group method (varies each sitting — there is no fixed percentage cutoff)
- **No negative marking** — every unanswered question is a lost opportunity

The borderline group method means the pass mark is recalculated each sitting based on the performance of candidates judged to be borderline competent. This is why the stated pass mark varies slightly between sittings and why candidates should not bank on a specific percentage target — aim higher than you think is necessary.

### Core Syllabus: What Gets Tested

**General Pathology** carries the highest question weight in Part 1:

- Cell injury, adaptation, and death — reversible vs irreversible, necrosis types, apoptosis pathways
- Inflammation and healing — acute vs chronic, chemical mediators, granulomatous disease, wound healing
- Neoplasia — benign vs malignant criteria, grading, staging, oncogenes, tumour suppressors, carcinogenesis
- Circulatory disorders — thrombosis, embolism, infarction, shock, DIC
- Genetic disorders — Mendelian and chromosomal; molecular basis
- Immunopathology — hypersensitivity types, autoimmunity, transplant rejection, amyloid

**Systemic Histopathology** high-yield areas:

| System | Key Exam Topics |
|---|---|
| GI Tract | Barrett's oesophagus → adenocarcinoma, IBD (UC vs Crohn's), colorectal carcinoma (FAP/HNPCC/sporadic), GIST (c-KIT/PDGFRA), appendiceal tumours |
| Liver & Biliary | Viral hepatitis serology, cirrhosis (macro/micro nodules), HCC (AFP, predisposing factors), PSC vs PBC, bile duct hamartomas |
| Lung | Adenocarcinoma (TTF-1, EGFR/ALK), squamous cell (p63, PTHrP), small cell (neuroendocrine markers, paraneoplastic), mesothelioma (calretinin, WT1), ILD |
| Breast | DCIS vs LCIS, invasive carcinoma subtypes, Nottingham grading (tubules + nuclear grade + mitoses), ER/PR/HER2 |
| Gynaecological | HPV → cervical SIL/carcinoma; endometrial carcinoma Type 1 (endometrioid, PTEN, MSI) vs Type 2 (serous, p53, POLE); ovarian tumour WHO classification |
| Skin | BCC vs SCC vs melanoma, Breslow thickness, Clark level, dysplastic naevus, Merkel cell carcinoma |
| CNS | Glioma classification (IDH mutation, 1p19q codeletion, ATRX, TERT); meningioma; medulloblastoma molecular groups; WHO CNS 5th edition |
| Lymphoma | Hodgkin (RS cells, CD15/CD30, subtypes); DLBCL; Follicular (BCL-2, t14:18); Mantle Cell (BCL-1/cyclin D1, t11:14); Burkitt (c-MYC, t8:14) |
| Renal | Clear cell RCC (VHL), papillary RCC (MET), chromophobe RCC (KIT), oncocytoma (Hürthle cell), nephroblastoma |
| Bone & Soft Tissue | Osteosarcoma (metaphysis, sunburst), Ewing sarcoma (EWSR1-FLI1, t11:22), GIST, synovial sarcoma (SS18-SSX) |
| Head & Neck | Pleomorphic adenoma (PLAG1), Warthin's, adenoid cystic carcinoma (perineural invasion, MYB-NFIB), nasopharyngeal carcinoma (EBV) |

## FRCPath Part 1 MCQ Strategy

### Best-of-5 (BOF) Approach

The BOF format presents a clinical or histological scenario and asks you to select the single best answer from five options. Two options are usually clearly wrong — eliminate these first. The remaining three are where your preparation is tested: often one is a plausible distractor (a related concept, slightly wrong detail, or correct answer for a different disease), one is partially correct, and one is definitively correct.

**Elimination first, selection second.** Reading all five options before committing reduces the error rate from gut-response answering.

**Never leave a question blank.** With no negative marking, an unanswered question has a 0% return; a guess has a 20% expected return. Even when you have no idea, eliminate what you can and choose from what remains.

### Extended Matching Question (EMQ) Strategy

EMQs present a list of 8–12 answer options followed by 4–6 clinical scenario stems. Each stem matches to one option; options can be used more than once or not at all.

**Read the option list first** — before reading any stem. This activates your recall for all the concepts in play. Then read each stem and match. This is faster than reading each stem cold and then scanning all options each time.

EMQs test clinical scenario application more than recall — the scenario will include a defining detail (e.g., "bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy with non-caseating granulomas and elevated ACE") that should point you to one answer. If you cannot identify the clinching detail, the question is probably testing a less-familiar condition: use pattern matching from the option list.

### Image-Based Questions

A proportion of Part 1 questions include histopathological images. Active image practice is non-negotiable — reading about Reed-Sternberg cells is not the same as recognising them on a glass-style image.

Recommended image practice resources:
- **Virtual Pathology Leeds** (free, annotated slides)
- **PathPresenter** (slide collections)
- **PathoPic** (image library)
- Image-based MCQs in the [FRCPath Part 1 MCQ bank at eLearning FRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-part-1-histopathology-course)

Commonly tested images: Reed-Sternberg cells, Auer rods, signet ring cells, psammoma bodies, Councilman bodies, Langhans giant cells, Aschoff nodules, haemosiderin-laden macrophages, Reinke crystals, Dutcher bodies.

### Time Management

- BOF: 90 questions in 150 minutes = 100 seconds per question
- EMQ: 60 stems in 150 minutes, but stems come in groups — allocate ~12 minutes per group of 4–6 stems
- Flag difficult questions and move — returning to a flagged question fresh is more efficient than staring at it
- Reserve the final 10 minutes to review flagged questions and ensure no blanks

**Volume target before sitting:** 3,000+ MCQs minimum. Candidates who fail Part 1 overwhelmingly report insufficient MCQ practice, not insufficient content knowledge. Content without application does not convert to marks.

## FRCPath Part 2: Histopathology — In Depth

### Format (Current 2024–2025 Structure)

| Component | Format | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Written Paper 1 | Short Answer Questions (SAQs) | 3 hours |
| Written Paper 2 | Extended Essays / Problem-solving | 3 hours |
| Practical (OSPE) | Microscopy + grossing + IHC + ancillary data stations | Half day |

### Written Paper Strategy

**SAQs demand structure, not flow.** Examiners mark to a mark scheme, not to the quality of prose. Bullet points covering every relevant domain (definition → pathogenesis → morphology → clinical features → complications → prognosis) score better than a paragraph that covers three domains well and misses two entirely.

A common SAQ format: *"Describe the histological features of [condition X] and the differential diagnoses you would consider."* The expected answer covers: (1) key architectural features, (2) cytological features, (3) relevant IHC markers, (4) molecular features if applicable, (5) DDx with distinguishing features for each.

**Essay / extended response** questions test reporting judgment and clinical integration. These often present a complex case scenario and ask how you would manage it — what ancillary tests, what the report should state, what the MDT implications are. The College publishes examiner reports for recent sittings: reading these is the single most efficient Part 2 preparation step, as they reveal exactly what candidates missed.

**Past papers** are available on the RCPath website. Work through them under timed conditions, then review against the published examiner reports. This is the Part 2 equivalent of MCQ bank practice for Part 1.

### Practical OSPE Strategy

The OSPE (Objective Structured Practical Examination) has multiple stations, each timed:

1. **Slide interpretation** — H&E slides; identify tissue, architecture, diagnosis; common tumours, inflammatory conditions, infections
2. **Macro grossing station** — describe specimen type, orientation, measurements, cut surfaces; identify key areas to sample and explain sampling rationale
3. **IHC interpretation** — given a panel result and H&E context, interpret staining and state diagnosis with reasoning
4. **Ancillary data station** — cytogenetics, molecular results, flow cytometry data; integrate with morphology
5. **Communication / MDT station** — present a case to a simulated MDT; demonstrate clinical integration and communication of uncertainty

**For every slide:** Use a systematic approach — tissue type → overall architecture → cellular composition → specific pathological features → differential diagnosis → most likely diagnosis → what additional information or stain would help. Do not jump to a diagnosis before completing the systematic description.

**Gross description:** Practise dictating specimen descriptions out loud, timed. The OSPE station has a time limit; a fluent, structured gross description is a practised skill, not a spontaneous one.

**IHC panel interpretation:** Know the core diagnostic panels cold — lymphoma phenotyping, carcinoma vs mesothelioma, primary vs metastatic, spindle cell tumour differentiation. The [FRCPath exam guide at eLearning FRCPath](https://elearningfrcpath.com/frcpath-exam-guide-2026) includes IHC panel reference sets specifically structured for OSPE preparation.

## FRCPath Passing Rates and Realistic Expectations

FRCPath Part 1 Histopathology has a first-attempt pass rate of approximately **55–65%** across recent sittings. Part 2 runs higher at around **70–80%**, partly because candidates who reach Part 2 have already demonstrated persistence and preparation discipline through Part 1.

Most candidates sit Part 1 two to three times before passing — this is normal, documented in published data, and does not reflect poorly on a candidate's career trajectory. What it does reflect: insufficient MCQ volume on first attempt, or undertiming of the paper. The fix is not to know more content — it is to practise more questions under exam conditions.

**3,000 MCQs** is the minimum volume target before a Part 1 sitting. Candidates who clear 5,000 MCQs across multiple question banks with full explanation review have the highest documented pass rates.

## FRCPath for International and Indian Candidates

If you are sitting FRCPath from India, the Middle East, or Southeast Asia:

- **Part 1 can be sat internationally** — the College has approved examination centres in India (multiple cities), the UAE, Singapore, and other locations. Check the RCPath examinations page for the current list of international centres available for each sitting.
- **Part 2 is typically UK-based** — the written papers and OSPE are conducted in the UK. Plan travel and accommodation well in advance; costs and logistics are significant and are not factored in when candidates compare FRCPath to local examination routes.
- **Eligibility:** International candidates without UK GMC registration follow the CESR (Certificate of Eligibility for Specialist Registration) or direct examination route depending on their career stage. Clarify your eligibility category directly with RCPath before applying.
- **Application timing:** Applications typically open 10–12 weeks before the sitting date. For a sitting in October/November 2026, the application window opens in August 2026. Missing the window means waiting 6 months for the next sitting.

## Exam Pearls for FRCPath Candidates

**Pearl 1:** Read every RCPath examiner report published for your exam diet. These documents state explicitly what candidates wrote that was wrong or incomplete — they are the closest thing to a marking scheme you will find for Part 2, and they reveal which Part 1 topics generate the most errors.

**Pearl 2:** WHO 5th Edition Blue Books are no longer optional for Part 1. The 2022 classification updates — particularly CNS, soft tissue, haematopathology, and GI tumours — are reflected in current Part 1 questions. If you are using notes or question banks from before 2022, check for outdated classifications.

**Pearl 3:** For EMQs, practise reading the option list first without reading the stems. This sounds counterintuitive but activates your recall for all concepts in the question before you encounter the scenarios — it is a faster and more accurate approach than reading each stem cold.

**Pearl 4:** In the OSPE, examiners reward systematic description over a correct guess. Stating a diagnosis before completing a systematic description suggests you pattern-matched rather than assessed. Structure earns marks even when your diagnosis is partially wrong.

**Mnemonic — PASS for FRCPath exam preparation:**
- **P** — Practice volume (3,000+ MCQs for Part 1; past papers timed for Part 2)
- **A** — Active image work (not passive reading about histological features)
- **S** — Syllabus alignment (WHO 5th Ed, RCPath curriculum — not generic pathology textbooks alone)
- **S** — Systematic approach (BOF elimination, EMQ option-first, OSPE slide structure)

## Practice MCQs — FRCPath Part 1 Style

**Q1.** A 58-year-old male smoker presents with a 4 cm right hilar mass on CT. Bronchoscopic biopsy shows nests of cells with scant cytoplasm, finely granular ("salt and pepper") chromatin, and nuclear moulding. Immunohistochemistry: synaptophysin positive, chromogranin positive, TTF-1 positive, CK7 positive, p40 negative, CD56 positive. Which of the following paraneoplastic syndromes is most specifically associated with this tumour type, and what is the underlying mechanism?

- A) Hypercalcaemia — PTHrP secretion by tumour cells
- B) Cushing's syndrome — ectopic ACTH secretion causing cortisol excess
- C) Hypertrophic pulmonary osteoarthropathy — periosteal reaction from tumour-derived prostaglandins
- D) Superior vena cava syndrome — direct tumour compression of the SVC

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Cushing's syndrome via ectopic ACTH secretion**

The morphology (nests, scant cytoplasm, salt-and-pepper chromatin, nuclear moulding) and IHC (synaptophysin+, chromogranin+, CD56+, TTF-1+) define **small cell lung carcinoma (SCLC)** — a high-grade neuroendocrine carcinoma. SCLC is the lung tumour most strongly associated with paraneoplastic endocrine syndromes due to its neuroendocrine origin and peptide-secreting capacity. **Ectopic ACTH secretion** causing Cushing's syndrome is the most characteristically associated paraneoplastic syndrome — the ACTH is produced at a rate that overwhelms normal feedback and causes severe hypokalaemic alkalosis, hyperglycaemia, and weight gain. Hypercalcaemia via PTHrP (option A) is the classic paraneoplastic syndrome of **squamous cell lung carcinoma** (p40+, p63+, CK5/6+, no neuroendocrine markers). SIADH (not listed) is also an SCLC paraneoplastic, mediated by ectopic ADH. Eaton-Lambert syndrome (antibodies against presynaptic voltage-gated calcium channels) is the classic neuromuscular paraneoplastic of SCLC. Hypertrophic pulmonary osteoarthropathy (option C) is more associated with large lung tumours generically, not specifically SCLC. SVC syndrome (option D) is a mechanical complication, not a paraneoplastic syndrome.

</details>

**Q2.** A 34-year-old woman has a lumpectomy for a 2.2 cm breast carcinoma. Histology shows infiltrating cords and single cells with intracytoplasmic lumina and no tubule formation; E-cadherin immunohistochemistry is negative. ER is strongly positive (Allred score 8/8). HER2 is 1+. Ki67 is 12%. Nottingham grade is 2. What is the most accurate combined diagnosis and molecular subtype?

- A) Invasive ductal carcinoma (NST), Luminal A — E-cadherin loss is a non-specific artefact
- B) Invasive lobular carcinoma, Luminal A — E-cadherin loss + infiltrating single files/cords is pathognomonic of the lobular phenotype
- C) Invasive ductal carcinoma (NST), HER2-enriched — HER2 1+ is borderline positive
- D) Invasive lobular carcinoma, Triple negative — ER positivity in lobular carcinoma is inconsistent with the lobular phenotype

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Invasive lobular carcinoma, Luminal A**

The morphology — infiltrating single cells and cords ("Indian file" pattern), intracytoplasmic lumina (targetoid mucin vacuoles), absence of tubule formation — combined with **loss of E-cadherin** defines **invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC)**. E-cadherin is a cell adhesion molecule encoded by CDH1; its loss (via CDH1 mutation, promoter methylation, or loss of heterozygosity) is pathognomonic of the lobular phenotype and explains the discohesive infiltration pattern. E-cadherin loss is not a non-specific artefact (option A is incorrect). The molecular subtype is determined by receptor status: **ER strongly positive (Allred 8/8) + HER2 1+ (negative) + Ki67 12% (low) = Luminal A** — the best-prognosis molecular subtype. HER2 1+ by IHC is negative (not amplified); IHC 2+ requires ISH reflex; IHC 3+ is positive — so option C is incorrect. Triple negative (option D) by definition requires ER negative, PR negative, and HER2 negative — ER positivity at Allred 8/8 excludes it.

</details>

**Q3.** A 62-year-old male presents with progressive headaches and focal neurological deficit. MRI shows a 5 cm right temporal lobe mass with ring enhancement. Stereotactic biopsy shows hypercellular glial tumour, geographic necrosis with pseudopalisading, and microvascular proliferation. Molecular analysis: IDH wild-type, TERT promoter mutated, EGFR amplified, chromosome 7 gain / chromosome 10 loss. What is the WHO 5th Edition diagnosis, and what is the prognostic implication of the molecular profile?

- A) IDH-mutant astrocytoma, Grade 4 — IDH mutation confirms glioblastoma by the 5th edition criteria
- B) Glioblastoma, IDH wild-type, WHO Grade 4 — TERT promoter mutation + EGFR amplification + +7/−10 pattern confirms this diagnosis even without necrosis or microvascular proliferation
- C) Diffuse midline glioma, H3K27-altered, WHO Grade 4 — H3 K27 alteration is implied by IDH wild-type status
- D) Oligodendroglioma, IDH wild-type, WHO Grade 3 — 1p19q codeletion testing is required before classifying as astrocytic

<details>
<summary>View Answer & Explanation</summary>

**Answer: B — Glioblastoma, IDH wild-type, WHO Grade 4**

Under **WHO CNS 5th Edition (2021)**, the diagnosis of **Glioblastoma, IDH wild-type, Grade 4** requires either: (a) histological features of GBM (necrosis + microvascular proliferation, as present here) OR (b) molecular signature of GBM in a lower-grade-appearing diffuse glioma — specifically **TERT promoter mutation + EGFR amplification + chromosome 7 gain/chromosome 10 loss** (the "+7/−10" signature). This molecular triad defines GBM biologically even without classic histology — a major update from the 4th edition where necrosis/MVP was required. In this case, both histological features AND the molecular signature are present, confirming GBM unambiguously. Option A is incorrect: **IDH-mutant astrocytoma Grade 4** (previously called "secondary GBM") requires an IDH mutation — this tumour is IDH wild-type. Option C is incorrect: diffuse midline glioma requires H3 K27M or H3 G34 alteration (histone mutations) — IDH wild-type alone does not imply H3 alteration. Option D is incorrect: oligodendroglioma by definition requires IDH mutation AND 1p19q codeletion — IDH wild-type status excludes it.

</details>

## FAQs

**How many times can you sit FRCPath Part 1, and is there a limit?**

RCPath does not publish a fixed lifetime attempt limit for FRCPath Part 1. In practice, most candidates pass within 2–3 attempts. Candidates who have multiple failed attempts are typically offered educational support through their deanery or training programme. International candidates sitting outside UK training programmes should check with RCPath directly about attempt policies applicable to their registration category, as these may differ from those for UK trainees in a formal ST programme.

**Can I sit FRCPath Part 1 from India without being a UK trainee?**

Yes. FRCPath Part 1 is open to international candidates who meet the eligibility criteria, which primarily require a recognised primary medical qualification and postgraduate pathology training. You do not need to be a UK GMC-registered trainee to sit Part 1. RCPath has approved international examination centres in India (check the RCPath website for current cities) where the paper is held simultaneously with the UK sitting. Part 2, however, is typically conducted in the UK — candidates should factor travel and accommodation costs into their planning when aiming for the full FRCPath qualification.

**What is the difference between FRCPath and MRCPath?**

MRCPath (Membership of the Royal College of Pathologists) was the old qualification structure — candidates passed Part 1 to become MRCPath and then Part 2 to become FRCPath. This terminology is now largely historical: the current structure confers the FRCPath Fellowship upon passing both parts. Candidates who passed Part 1 under the old system are sometimes referred to as MRCPath holders, but the current awarding body does not issue a separate MRCPath certificate — completion of both parts awards FRCPath directly.

**How does FRCPath Histopathology compare to NEET-SS DM Pathology for an Indian MD Pathology graduate?**

The two qualifications serve different purposes and are not direct competitors. FRCPath is a UK-recognised fellowship that opens pathways to consultant posts in the UK, Commonwealth countries, and internationally. NEET-SS DM Pathology (Oncopathology, Haematopathology, Neuropathology) is the Indian super-specialty route that leads to academic and senior positions within India. Many MD Pathology graduates in India pursue both — using the substantial content overlap (approximately 70% in WHO 5th edition tumour classification, IHC marker panels, and molecular pathology) to prepare for both simultaneously. The key difference is format: FRCPath uses BOF/EMQ and Part 2 written + OSPE; NEET-SS uses a three-section 50-minute-per-section format with digital pathology images.

**What is the best single resource for FRCPath Part 1 MCQ practice?**

A dedicated FRCPath-specific MCQ bank is the most efficient resource — general pathology question banks (designed for USMLE or other exams) use different clinical contexts, question styles, and emphasis. The FRCPath BOF style tests specific diagnostic histopathological knowledge, IHC marker application, and WHO classification in a way that USMLE-style pathology MCQs do not.
